# Week 4 — Quiz

Ten questions. Lectures closed.

---

**Q1.** The recruiter screen is structured to answer four questions. Which of the following is **not** one of them?

- A) Are you who your resume says you are?
- B) Will you pass the technical loop?
- C) Have you read the company's most recent SEC filing?
- D) Is your compensation expectation inside band?

---

**Q2.** Your "tell me about yourself" answer runs 4 minutes and 15 seconds on the recording. The most likely problem is:

- A) The answer is too short and needs more detail.
- B) You're listing or repeating; trim the path paragraph.
- C) Recruiters prefer longer self-intros.
- D) The length is fine; recruiters tune out after 90 seconds anyway.

---

**Q3.** Which "why this company?" answer is strongest?

- A) "I think you're doing really exciting work and I'd love to be part of it."
- B) "Your engineering culture seems great, and I admire the team."
- C) "Three things: your engineering blog post on the ledger split-read/write design from last month; your stage — Series B with ~120 engineers, payments as the core product; and the fact that {Engineer X}'s PyCon talk on idempotency is in my actual reference list at work."
- D) "I'm passionate about your mission and want to grow my career here."

---

**Q4.** A recruiter in California asks "what are you currently making at your job?" Which response is correct?

- A) Disclose the exact figure; California requires you to.
- B) Refuse to disclose; salary-history questions are restricted in California, and pivot to target comp.
- C) Lie upward by 20% to anchor the offer higher.
- D) Refuse to answer and end the call.

---

**Q5.** You look up your target company on levels.fyi. Median TC for Senior in SF is $390k; 75th percentile is $440k; 90th percentile is $510k. What's your defensible **high anchor** on the screen call?

- A) $510k — anchor as high as possible.
- B) $440k — the 75th percentile, your defensible top with public data.
- C) $390k — the median, to seem reasonable.
- D) $300k — well below median, to ensure they say yes.

---

**Q6.** A recruiter responds to your comp anchor with "that's higher than where this role typically lands — we're usually closer to $360-$390k. Is the lower end of your range still workable?" The best response is:

- A) "Sure, $360 is fine."
- B) "The $360 floor is workable, especially if there's flexibility on the equity or signing side. I'd want to talk through the mix once we have a better sense of fit."
- C) "No, I won't go below $440k."
- D) Silence; let them name a higher number.

**Q7.** Which of the following is **not** a typical component of a tech compensation package?

- A) Base salary
- B) Equity (RSUs or options)
- C) Signing bonus
- D) Health insurance premium reimbursement listed as a comp line item

**Q8.** A recruiter asks "do you have any questions for me?" near the end of the screen. You have no prepared questions and say "no, you covered everything." On the recruiter's rubric, this is most likely scored as:

- A) Positive — efficient candidate.
- B) Neutral — neither helps nor hurts.
- C) Negative — low engagement; recruiters score the closing question more than candidates realize.
- D) Bonus points — saving time for both parties.

---

**Q9.** The "through-line" in a self-intro is:

- A) The university degree you list at the end of the resume.
- B) The phrase or theme connecting your current and prior roles — a domain, a kind of system, a kind of problem.
- C) The buzzword paragraph at the start of the About section.
- D) The first sentence of every reply in the screen.

---

**Q10.** At a FAANG-tier public company, which lever is typically **most-flexible** during negotiation for a senior IC role?

- A) Base salary — bands are wide and easily moved.
- B) Equity (RSU grant size) — band-constrained on base, more give on equity grants.
- C) Annual bonus percentage — usually fully negotiable.
- D) Health insurance plan upgrades.

---

## Answer key

<details>
<summary>Reveal after attempting</summary>

1. **C** — The four questions are: are you who your resume says, will you pass technical, do you want this role specifically, is comp in band. SEC filings are not on the rubric.
2. **B** — At 4+ minutes you're listing or repeating. The target is 120-180 seconds. Trim the path paragraph; it's usually the first place to cut.
3. **C** — Names a specific artifact (the blog post), a specific stage descriptor (Series B, ~120 engineers, payments-as-product), and a specific person + named work. The others are interchangeable across any company.
4. **B** — In California (and several other US states + the EU), salary-history questions are restricted. Pivot to target comp with the standard response from Lecture 2 §4.
5. **B** — The 75th percentile is the defensible high anchor on a screen call without competing offers. The 90th typically requires explicit leverage you don't yet have.
6. **B** — Acknowledge the band, hold the anchor, restate flexibility on the lever (equity / signing) rather than on TC. Don't capitulate; don't refuse.
7. **D** — Standard components are base, equity, signing, and annual bonus. Health insurance is a benefit, not a comp-line item the recruiter negotiates.
8. **C** — Recruiters score the closing question on a low-engagement / high-engagement scale. Zero questions reads as low engagement; 2-3 informed questions is the sweet spot.
9. **B** — The through-line connects your current and prior roles by a shared theme — a domain, a system type, a problem area — so the self-intro reads as a trajectory, not a list of disconnected jobs.
10. **B** — FAANG-tier public companies have tight base bands but significant flexibility on RSU grant size. The mid-stage scaleup is the inverse (base + signing more flexible). Knowing which lever shapes what you ask for.

</details>

If under 7, re-read the lecture you missed. If 9+, you're ready for the homework.
