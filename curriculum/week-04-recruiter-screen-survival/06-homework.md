## Problem 1 — Build the screen-call cheat sheet (45 min)

Combine outputs from Exercises 1, 2, and 3 into a single one-page document you'll keep open during real screen calls.

Create `c13-week-04/screen-call-cheat-sheet.md`. The structure:

```markdown
# Screen-call cheat sheet — {your name}

## 1. Self-intro (90-180 sec)
- [Bullets only — not prose. From Exercise 1's recording-03 bullets.]

## 2. Why this company (drop-in per company)
- **Company A:** [Reason 1 / Reason 2 / Reason 3 — one phrase each, from Exercise 2.]
- **Company B:** [...]
- **Company C:** [...]
- **Company D:** [...]
- **Company E:** [...]

## 3. Why are you looking
- [One-line push + one-line pull + one-line timing, from Lecture 1 §6.]

## 4. Logistics
- Notice period: {N weeks}
- Work auth: {one-sentence}
- Location: {city, remote-fine? on-site OK?}
- Earliest start: {date}

## 5. Comp
- High anchor TC: $X (per company, see table)
- Base case TC: $Y
- Walkaway TC: $Z
- Justification sentence: "Based on levels.fyi for {level} at peer companies in {location}..."
- Per-company comp table: [paste 5-row table from Exercise 3]

## 6. Three questions for the recruiter
- Q1 (reusable, process): [...]
- Q2 (reusable, team / role): [...]
- Q3 (company-specific, drop in per call): [...]
```

**Acceptance:**

- All 6 sections populated.
- Fits on a single laptop screen at normal zoom — the full document is under ~200 words *outside* the per-company tables and reasons.
- Top of file has a date stamp; the cheat sheet will revise after every real screen.

## Problem 2 — The "why are you looking?" dignified non-answer (40 min)

In `c13-week-04/why-leaving.md`, draft and rehearse your answer to "why are you looking right now?"

**Part A — Draft.** Apply Lecture 1 §6's structure: one sentence of push, one sentence of pull, one optional sentence on timing.

```markdown
## Draft

**Push (1 sentence):** [What you're moving away from at your current role, framed without insult.]

**Pull (1 sentence):** [What you're moving toward, framed positively.]

**Timing (optional):** [Why now, in one sentence.]
```

**Part B — Variants.** Draft a variant for each of the following situations *that could apply to you* (skip any that obviously don't):

- Currently employed, voluntary search.
- Recently laid off.
- Coming back from a gap.
- New grad / first job.
- Currently happy, but specifically interested in this role.

**Part C — The badmouthing audit.** Read each variant aloud. For any sentence, check: would your current manager wince if they read it? Would a recruiter write down "potential badmouthing" on their notes? Rewrite until both audits pass.

**Acceptance:**

- One drafted answer for your primary situation.
- Variants drafted for at least one alternative situation.
- Badmouthing audit notes inline; no sentence reads as a current-employer complaint.
- Maximum 4 sentences per variant.

## Problem 3 — Three questions for the recruiter (35 min)

In `c13-week-04/recruiter-questions.md`, draft a bank of questions for the closing beat. Categorise them.

**Categories** (from Lecture 1 §8):

1. **Process and timing** — about the loop, stages, end-to-end timeline.
2. **Team shape** — about the team this role would sit on, the reporting manager.
3. **Hiring bar / what they're optimising for** — the recruiter's read on what separates passing candidates from declining ones.
4. **Compensation structure** — only if not yet covered, or if you want a structural clarification.
5. **Recruiter's own read** — "anything you'd recommend I emphasise or prep for in the next stages?"

Draft **2 questions per category**, for a total of 10. Mark **3 as "reusable defaults"** — the ones you'd use on a generic screen. The other 7 are situational.

**Acceptance:**

- 10 questions total, 2 per category.
- 3 marked as reusable defaults.
- Each question is one sentence, conversational, and could not be answered from the company's public website.
- File annotated: which questions you'd save for a hiring-manager interview (different from a recruiter screen) and which are recruiter-specific.

## Problem 4 — Salary-history-question rehearsal (35 min)

This is a short focused drill on the one comp question that catches people off guard.

In `c13-week-04/salary-history-rehearsal.md`:

**Part A — The legal landscape.** Spend 10 minutes finding out: in your **jurisdiction** (state if US, country if elsewhere), is the recruiter legally allowed to ask about your current or past salary? Cite the law / regulation / guidance in one line. (US states: search "{your state} salary history ban." EU: search "{country} pay transparency directive 2023.")

**Part B — Draft the response.** Whether or not the question is legal where you are, draft your one-sentence pivot:

```markdown
**My response if asked:**
> "I'd rather discuss target comp than history — for context, I'm targeting {your range} based on the level and the market."
```

**Part C — The follow-up.** Some recruiters will press: "we'd just like to know what you're currently making to make sure we're in band." Draft a one-sentence response that holds the line without ending the call:

```markdown
**My follow-up response:**
> "Understood — I'm not sharing the current number, but I can tell you my target range is $X-$Y, and if that's outside your band let me know so we don't waste each other's time."
```

**Part D — Rehearse.** Read both responses aloud 5 times. Time them; each should be under 15 seconds.

**Acceptance:**

- Jurisdiction note with one-line legal citation (or "not legally restricted, but I will still not disclose").
- Drafted response and drafted follow-up.
- A note that you've rehearsed each aloud 5+ times.

## Problem 5 — Read Patrick McKenzie's salary-negotiation essay (60 min)

Read end to end: <https://www.kalzumeus.com/2012/01/23/salary-negotiation/>.

It's the single most-cited reference in the comp section of this curriculum. ~12,000 words; an hour read.

In `c13-week-04/mckenzie-notes.md`, write a structured response:

**Part A — Three principles you're going to apply.** From the essay, list 3 specific principles or tactics you're going to use on your next real screen call. Quote the line from the essay, then explain in 1-2 sentences how it applies to *your* current cycle.

**Part B — One principle you disagree with or find dated.** The essay is from 2012; some advice (e.g., on the "never name first number" rule, pre-pay-transparency laws) is partially obsolete. Pick one, name it, explain why.

**Part C — Cross-check against levels.fyi.** Open your Exercise-3 comp table. For one of your five target companies, work through: would Patrick's advice change your high anchor / base case / walkaway? Why or why not?

**Acceptance:**

- Three principles cited and translated to your cycle.
- One disagreement / dated point named.
- One Exercise-3 row revisited with cross-check notes.
- Total write-up: 300-500 words. Not less; not much more.

## Problem 6 — Week-4 reflection (40 min)

`c13-week-04/reflection.md`, 300-400 words:

1. Before this week, what was your default answer to "what compensation are you targeting?" Be honest: was it the hedge, the undersell, the unjustified ask, the decline, or were you already at the defensible answer?
2. Of the three exercises this week, which one moved the needle most for you, and why? (Most candidates report Exercise 1 — the self-intro recording — because the gap between "what I thought I sounded like" and "what I sounded like" is the largest. But yours may differ.)
3. The mini-project is a recorded mock screen with self-feedback. What do you most expect to discover about yourself on the recording? What do you most fear?
4. What one habit are you carrying into Week 5 — concrete, testable, not aspirational? (Example: "Before every screen call, I will spend 15 minutes pre-filling the cheat-sheet's per-company section.")
