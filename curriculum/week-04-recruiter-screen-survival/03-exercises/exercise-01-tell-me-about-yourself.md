# Exercise 1 — Tell me about yourself

**Time:** ~60 minutes. **Output:** A bulleted draft of your 2-3 minute self-intro, a recording of you delivering it (audio minimum, video preferred), and three rewrites against the rubric.

## Goal

Produce a 2-3 minute "tell me about yourself" answer that you can deliver from bullets (not a script), under 180 seconds, with at least one quantified outcome and a forward-looking close. Test it empirically by recording yourself three times and measuring against a rubric.

## Setup

You need:

- Your **current v2 resume** (from Week 2 mini-project). The self-intro must agree with it.
- A **stopwatch** — your phone is fine.
- A **recording app** — phone voice memo, Loom, QuickTime, OBS, or Yoodli. Audio is enough; video is better because you'll catch posture and eye-contact issues.
- A **markdown file to draft in**: `c13-week-04/exercise-01-tell-me-about-yourself.md`. Don't draft the script in your head; write it down so you can revise it.
- A **quiet room** for the recording. Headphones with a mic give the cleanest audio.

## Process

### Step 1 — Pull the raw material (10 min)

In your draft file, capture the inputs:

```markdown
## Raw material from the resume

**Current role:** {Title} at {Company}, {start date – present}.

**One-sentence on what you build:** [The one most-defining responsibility, in plain English.]

**Top 2-3 technologies in the current role:** {tech 1, tech 2, tech 3}.

**Top 2 quantified outcomes from the last 18 months:**
1. [One STAR+R bullet from the resume, rewritten as prose. Must include a number.]
2. [Second outcome. Same constraint.]

**Prior role (one-liner):** {Title} at {Company}, {dates}, doing {one phrase}.

**Through-line (the thread across both roles):** [What's the common domain, system type, or problem? One phrase.]

**Forward-looking direction:** [The kind of role you're targeting next. One sentence.]
```

This is your raw material. The script will be built from these inputs and nothing else.

### Step 2 — Draft v1 (10 min)

Using Lecture 1, §4's three-paragraph structure, write a first draft in your file:

```markdown
## Draft v1

**Paragraph 1 — current state (60-90 sec):**
> [Role + employer + one-sentence on what you build + 2-3 technologies + 1-2 quantified outcomes.]

**Paragraph 2 — path (30-60 sec):**
> [Prior role + transition + through-line phrase.]

**Paragraph 3 — next (20-30 sec):**
> [Kind of role + kind of company + specific connecting sentence about the role you're screening for.]
```

Constraints on the draft:

- Total word count: **300-450** (corresponds to 120-180 spoken seconds at normal pace).
- **Zero** instances of "passionate," "lifelong learner," "team player," "self-starter," "wear many hats," "results-driven."
- At least **one** quantified outcome — a dollar figure, a latency, a user count, a team size, a percentage.
- The opening sentence must work as standalone — a recruiter who hears only the first 10 seconds should already know your current role and employer.

### Step 3 — First recording, untimed (5 min)

Open your recording app. Hit record. Deliver Draft v1, reading from the bullets (not the prose).

Important: **read from bullets, not from the prose.** The prose is for word-counting and structure-checking; the delivery is from bullets so you sound conversational, not read-aloud.

Stop the recording when you finish. Save it as `recording-01-untimed.{m4a|mp4}`.

Now check: how long did it take? Write the time in your file.

### Step 4 — Review the first recording (5 min)

Play it back. Note in your draft file:

```markdown
## Recording 1 review

**Length:** [seconds]
**Issues observed:**
- [ ] Opened with college / something other than current role
- [ ] Used a banned phrase ("passionate," "team player," etc.)
- [ ] No quantified outcome in paragraph 1
- [ ] Hedge words ("kind of," "sort of," "I guess," "maybe")
- [ ] Trailing "yeah" or "so" at end of sentences
- [ ] Forward-looking sentence missing or generic
- [ ] Filler ("um," "uh," "like") at a count of: ___
- [ ] Pace too fast (under 90 sec total) / too slow (over 240 sec)
```

Most candidates' first recordings have 3-5 of these issues. That's expected. The point of the recording is to *see* them rather than guess.

### Step 5 — Revise to v2 (10 min)

Edit the bullets to fix the issues. Specifically:

- If you opened with college, rewrite the opening to lead with your current role.
- If a banned phrase appeared, replace it with a specific noun (a technology, a domain, a system).
- If no number was in paragraph 1, insert one. Look at the resume for the strongest quantified bullet.
- If hedge words appeared, cut them — or replace with neutral connectors ("I", "we", "the team").
- If the close was generic ("looking for a new opportunity"), rewrite it to reference the specific role / company you're screening for. (Phrase this part as a placeholder — `{the role I'm interviewing for at this specific company}` — since you'll personalise per call.)

### Step 6 — Second recording, timed (5 min)

Re-record Draft v2 from the revised bullets. **Use the stopwatch this time.** Aim for 120-180 seconds.

If under 120 seconds: you're rushing or skipping a paragraph. Re-record more deliberately.

If over 180 seconds: you're listing or repeating. Trim the bullets.

Save as `recording-02-timed.{m4a|mp4}`. Note the length in your draft file.

### Step 7 — Review and revise to v3 (10 min)

Play recording 2 back. Update the issues checklist. For each remaining issue, fix the bullets.

**Specifically attack:**

- **Pace.** If you're at 130-150 seconds, you're in the sweet spot. If you're 170-180, you're at the high end and any nervous talking on a real call will push you over — trim 30-50 words.
- **Filler words.** Count "um" and "uh." Above 5 per minute is high. The fix is not to "remove filler"; it's to pause silently instead. Add a `(pause)` marker between your bullet groups.
- **The opening sentence.** Replay just the first 10 seconds. Does it stand alone as informative? If not, rewrite the first bullet.

### Step 8 — Third recording, final (5 min)

Re-record v3. Save as `recording-03-final.{m4a|mp4}`.

This is the version you'll use as your baseline. Future revisions (after each real screen) will build on this.

## Common mistakes

- **The prose script in your hand on the real call.** You wrote out the full paragraphs to draft. You think you'll "just glance at it." On the real call, you read from it, your delivery flattens, the recruiter notices. Use bullets only after this exercise.
- **The 4-minute version.** "I had so much to cover I needed the extra time." No, you needed to cut. The recruiter has 30 minutes for the whole call. Your self-intro is not the place to spend 4 of them.
- **The "interesting career story" tangent.** You diverted to explain why you switched from research to product, or from frontend to backend, in a 90-second mini-story. Save for beat 5 ("why are you looking?") only if it's directly relevant.
- **The generic close.** "And that's why I'm looking for new opportunities!" — too vague. The close should name a kind of role or a connecting sentence to the role you're screening for.
- **Skipping the recording.** You read your draft aloud once, decided it sounded fine, and didn't record. You will discover on the first real call that you sound 30% worse than you thought. The recording is the load-bearing artifact of this exercise.

## Stretch

- **The 90-second variant.** Some screens (chat / async / very short calls) ask for a "30-second elevator pitch." Draft a 90-second version of your intro that drops paragraph 2 (the path) entirely. Save both.
- **The 60-second variant.** The "hallway" version, for use at meetups, networking events, or when a recruiter cold-DMs you on LinkedIn. Cut to current role + one outcome + one-sentence forward. ~150 words.
- **The role-pivot variant.** If you are pivoting domains (e.g., from web backend to ML platform), draft a version that reframes your prior experience to lead with the *transferable* skills, not the literal role. The through-line phrase carries most of the weight here.
- **Yoodli analysis.** Run recording 3 through Yoodli's free tier. It will flag filler-word density, pace, and one or two other metrics. Compare its numbers against your self-assessment.

## Why this matters

You will deliver this answer 10-30 times across a 12-week cycle. Every screen call, every cold-call from a recruiter who saw your LinkedIn, every meet-and-greet, every "give me the 2-minute version" at the start of every interview round. A weak self-intro costs you 5 minutes of every call's opening, which is the part where the recruiter is forming first impressions that shape the rest of their scoring.

The 60-minute investment in this exercise is paid back the first time you deliver a tight 150-second answer on a real call and watch the recruiter visibly relax — because they now have a clean shape to slot into their notes, and the rest of the call can move faster. Recruiters reward candidates who make their job easy. The self-intro is the first place you do that.
