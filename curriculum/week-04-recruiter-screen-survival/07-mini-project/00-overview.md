# Mini-Project — Recorded Mock Recruiter Screen with Self-Feedback

> By the end of this week, the artifacts you'll lean on in every real screen call — the 2-3 minute self-intro, the five "why this company" answers, the comp band — exist on paper. The mini-project is the operational test: do they hold up when you deliver them under live conditions, on the clock, to a camera?

**Estimated time:** 5-7 hours, spread Friday-Saturday. The recording is non-negotiable; the self-review against the rubric is where the value lives.

## What you ship

A **recorded mock recruiter screen with self-feedback**, delivered as five artifacts:

1. **A recorded mock screen** — 20-30 minutes of you delivering all seven beats of a recruiter call, against a script you read or a peer plays. Video preferred; audio acceptable.
2. **A self-review document** — you re-watch the recording and score yourself against the rubric in §3 of this file. Specific timestamps for each issue.
3. **A revised screen-call cheat sheet (v2)** — the version after self-review, with the top 3 issues addressed.
4. **A second short recording (5-10 min)** — the highest-leverage beat (typically self-intro or comp) redone after revising the cheat sheet, demonstrating the fix.
5. **A peer-feedback record (if Challenge 1 done)** — peer's scored feedback from the live mock, plus the changes you applied.

All artifacts committed to `c13-week-04/mini-project/`. The recordings can be stored outside the repo and linked if they're large; the markdown files and the cheat-sheet text live in the repo.

## How to run the recording

Two valid recording modes. Choose one (or do both, if you've done Challenge 1).

### Mode A — Solo recording, self-scripted

You play both sides. Time-box at 25 minutes. The peer-script from Challenge 1 (`challenge-01-mock-recruiter-screen.md`, Part B) is your prompt list — read each beat's recruiter line aloud, pause, then answer it as the candidate. The recording captures only your candidate-side audio (or video); the recruiter-side is implied.

This is the easier version to schedule. The cost: you don't get the live-interruption rep. The benefit: you can replay the recruiter prompt list verbatim and you control the pace.

### Mode B — Peer-played recording (the Challenge-1 mock)

If you did Challenge 1, the recording from that session **is** the mini-project's recording artifact. Use it directly. The peer-feedback form goes into the artifact pack.

Mode B is strictly higher-fidelity. Use it if you can schedule a peer; fall back to Mode A if not.

## The self-review rubric

After the recording (within 24 hours, while it's fresh), re-watch the full recording with this rubric. Score each beat 1-5; for each score under 4, note a timestamp.

### Beat 2 — Tell me about yourself (3-4 min)

| Score | What it looks like |
|------:|---------------------|
| 5 | 120-180 sec; opens with current role; ≥1 quantified outcome; through-line phrase; forward-looking close; zero banned phrases; <3 fillers/min. |
| 4 | All of the above with 1 minor miss (e.g., one filler-heavy patch, or close was slightly generic). |
| 3 | Two or three misses. Length might be 100s or 220s. |
| 2 | Substantive structural problem (opened with school, missing forward close, no numbers). |
| 1 | Failed to land structurally — the recruiter is still confused about who you are after the answer. |

**My score:** ____ **Timestamp(s) of issues:** ____

### Beat 3 — Resume probing (4-6 min)

| Score | What it looks like |
|------:|---------------------|
| 5 | Spoke about the project with clear ownership ("I", not "the team"); named specific technical decisions you made; named what you'd do differently. |
| 4 | Strong but slightly vague on one of: scope, your specific role, or the "what would you change" question. |
| 3 | One generic patch — e.g., "the team made some decisions about the architecture." |
| 2 | Significant hand-waving; couldn't speak to depth on a project you listed. |
| 1 | Recruiter would not believe you actually worked on this. |

**My score:** ____ **Timestamp(s) of issues:** ____

### Beat 4 — Why this company (3-4 min)

| Score | What it looks like |
|------:|---------------------|
| 5 | Three specific reasons, each surviving the substitution test from Exercise 2; total 90-120 sec; no banned phrases. |
| 4 | Three reasons, but one is weaker than the others (slightly generic). |
| 3 | Two specific + one generic, OR three reasons all on the same axis (e.g., all about engineering culture). |
| 2 | One specific, two generic. |
| 1 | Generic across all three — could be said about any company. |

**My score:** ____ **Timestamp(s) of issues:** ____

### Beat 5 — Why are you looking (2-3 min)

| Score | What it looks like |
|------:|---------------------|
| 5 | 4 sentences or fewer; push + pull + (optional) timing; no badmouthing; no defensiveness. |
| 4 | Clean but slightly long (5-6 sentences) or one mildly defensive phrase. |
| 3 | Meandering; takes 30+ seconds longer than it should; or one near-badmouth phrase. |
| 2 | Rambling answer; or a current-employer complaint of any size. |
| 1 | Outright badmouthing, or a confused answer that suggests you don't know why you're looking. |

**My score:** ____ **Timestamp(s) of issues:** ____

### Beat 6 — Logistics + comp (5-7 min)

Score logistics and comp separately.

**Logistics sub-score:**

| Score | What it looks like |
|------:|---------------------|
| 5 | All four logistics questions answered in <15 sec each; no hesitation; numbers precise. |
| 4 | One hesitation or one slightly-vague answer. |
| 3 | Two answers required reaching / hedging. |
| 2 | Multiple "I'd have to check" responses. |
| 1 | Major fumble on a core logistic (e.g., work auth or location). |

**Comp sub-score:**

| Score | What it looks like |
|------:|---------------------|
| 5 | Named a range; anchored at the 75th; one-sentence levels.fyi justification; held the anchor after the flinch; signalled flexibility on the lever, not the TC. |
| 4 | Range + anchor + justification, but capitulated slightly under the flinch. |
| 3 | Named a number but anchored at the median, or justification was thin. |
| 2 | Hedged ("flexible / whatever's competitive") or named an unjustified number. |
| 1 | Major comp failure — declined to name anything, or named a number 25%+ above defensible band with no leverage. |

**Logistics score:** ____ **Comp score:** ____ **Timestamp(s) of issues:** ____

### Beat 7 — Your questions + close (4-6 min)

| Score | What it looks like |
|------:|---------------------|
| 5 | Asked 2-3 informed questions; questions were recruiter-appropriate (not technical-depth questions for the engineer interview); closed professionally. |
| 4 | 2-3 questions but one was slightly generic, OR 1 question only. |
| 3 | One question, or all generic. |
| 2 | "No, you covered everything." |
| 1 | Awkward / dismissive close. |

**My score:** ____ **Timestamp(s) of issues:** ____

### Overall

**Total possible:** 35 (Beat 2 + Beat 3 + Beat 4 + Beat 5 + Logistics + Comp + Beat 7, each /5). **Your total:** ___ / 35.

**Interpretation:**

- **28-35:** Strong. You're ready for a real screen. Watch the recording once more, fix the timestamped issues, then book the screen.
- **21-27:** Mid. The structure is there, but specific beats need work. Identify the lowest-scored beat and run a focused drill (one beat, 15 min, two takes) before the real call.
- **14-20:** Needs work. The mock revealed multiple structural issues. Schedule a second full mock — ideally with a peer (Challenge 1) — before booking real screens.
- **Below 14:** Stop. The recording is telling you something important. Re-read Lectures 1 and 2, redo Exercise 1's recording from scratch, and run a second mock the next day.

## How to actually re-watch yourself

The mini-project's single most-difficult moment is the first time you press play on your own recording. Most candidates do not do this voluntarily; the discomfort of watching yourself is real and well-documented.

A few practical notes:

- **Watch alone first.** Don't show anyone else. The first watch is for your own diagnosis, not a performance.
- **Note before you react.** Pen in hand. When something makes you wince, write down the timestamp and what specifically made you wince. Don't pause to fix in your head; just record.
- **Watch at 1x first, then 1.5x for a second pass.** 1.5x highlights pacing issues and filler-word density that 1x normalises.
- **Separate "appearance" from "content."** "I don't like how my face looks" is appearance. "I said 'um' 18 times in 90 seconds" is content. The rubric scores content. Set the appearance discomfort aside.
- **Treat the recording as a peer's recording.** Imagine a friend sent you this video and asked for feedback. The version of you giving honest feedback to a friend is the version that scores the rubric well.

## How the cheat sheet evolves through the cycle

After every real screen call, spend 10-15 minutes updating the cheat sheet:

1. What questions did the recruiter ask that you didn't have an answer ready for? Add an answer.
2. What part of your self-intro didn't land — did the recruiter ask a clarifying follow-up that suggested they were confused? Trim or rewrite.
3. What comp question caught you off-guard? Add a one-line response to the sheet.
4. What questions did you ask the recruiter that didn't elicit useful answers? Replace with sharper ones.

By Week 8, your cheat sheet will be the most-revised document in your portfolio. That's the intent. The v1 from this week is the starting point; v6 by Week 12 is what passes a Staff-level screen.

## Stretch

- **Run the mini-project twice in the week.** Once Friday (Mode A solo), once Saturday (Mode B with peer). The Saturday version usually scores 4-7 points higher because the Friday review surfaced the fixes.
- **Pair-review with a cohort peer.** Trade recordings: you self-review yours, then watch theirs and give scored feedback against the same rubric, then have them do the same for you. Two passes over the rubric (yours + a peer's) catch issues each individually misses.
- **Add a behavioural-question drill.** Run an extra 10 minutes after Beat 7 where the "recruiter" (you, or a peer) asks one classic behavioural question: "Tell me about a time you disagreed with a teammate." Score the answer with the STAR rubric. This will become Week 7's focus; previewing it now de-loads next month.
- **Build the "next-screen prep checklist."** A 5-minute pre-call routine (read the JD again, fill in the per-company section of the cheat sheet, open levels.fyi for that specific company). Commit it next to the cheat sheet.

## Why this matters

The recruiter screen is the cheapest filter in the entire pipeline. Companies run 4-8x more recruiter screens than they make offers, and the screen is where most candidates eliminate themselves on issues that 90 minutes of preparation would have fixed. The mini-project's recording is the diagnostic that surfaces those 90 minutes of fixes *before* the cost of a real screen — not as a missed offer, but as a friction-free first call with someone you're then on a four-stage interview loop with.

This week's artifacts are also the first interview-loop artifacts you'll *carry forward*. The cheat sheet, the self-intro recording, the per-company "why us" drafts, the comp band — they all live for the rest of the cycle. Resume-and-portfolio weeks (1-3) produced documents; this week produces the live-delivery toolkit. Make it count.
