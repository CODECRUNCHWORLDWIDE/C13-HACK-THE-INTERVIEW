# Week 4 — The Recruiter Screen Survival Kit

> *The recruiter screen is the cheapest, most-skipped 30 minutes in the entire interview pipeline. It is also where two-thirds of candidates eliminate themselves. This week you stop being one of them.*

Welcome to Week 4 of **C13 · Hack the Interview**. Weeks 1–3 produced the artifacts: a target list, a v2 resume, a polished LinkedIn, a curated GitHub. By the time you hit Week 4, those artifacts are doing their job — inbound is starting to land in your inbox and recruiters are scheduling 30-minute screening calls.

The recruiter screen is the first conversation in the pipeline where a *human* on the company's side is evaluating you in real time. It is not a technical interview. It is not a culture interview. It is a **fit-and-friction filter** — a structured 30 minutes designed to answer four questions: is this person who their resume says they are, will they pass the technical loop, do they want this specific job, and is their comp expectation inside band. If all four answer "yes," you move forward. If any one answers "no" — or "I can't tell" — you don't.

The audience this week is the recruiter. The recruiter is not your friend, not your enemy, and not your hiring manager. They are an internal employee of the company whose job is to fill a specific role this quarter, and your job is to make their job easy. That means: have a tight three-minute answer to "tell me about yourself," know enough about the company to answer "why us" without bluffing, and have a comp number ready that you can defend with public data (which is where levels.fyi lives in this week).

The work in Week 4 is not deep technical preparation — that's Week 5 onward. The work is reps on the four or five recruiter-screen scripts that show up in every screen call, plus enough comp homework that the salary question doesn't catch you flat-footed.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this week, you will be able to:

- **Describe** the four questions every recruiter screen is structured to answer, and the order they typically appear in a 30-minute call.
- **Deliver** a 2-3 minute "tell me about yourself" answer that maps to your resume, leads with current work, and lands on a clear "what I'm looking for next."
- **Answer** "why this company?" for any company on your Tier-A target list with at least three specific reasons that are not generic ("great culture," "smart people," "ambitious mission").
- **State** a target compensation range with a confident high anchor, a defensible base, and one sentence of public-data justification (levels.fyi, salary.com, or company-disclosed bands).
- **Diagnose** the five most common ways candidates fail recruiter screens: meandering self-introductions, generic company answers, undefended comp numbers, missing logistics, and asking zero questions at the end.
- **Run** a recorded mock recruiter screen on yourself, score the recording against a rubric, and fix the three biggest issues before the real call.

## Prerequisites

- You completed **Weeks 1–3**. You have a target list, a v2 resume, a polished LinkedIn, and a curated GitHub.
- You have **at least one real screen scheduled, or you expect one in the next 2-3 weeks**. (If not, work this week as a pre-load; rerun the mini-project the week before your first screen.)
- You have a **phone with a recording app** or a laptop with screen-recording (Loom, QuickTime, OBS — any will do). The mini-project requires you to record yourself.
- You can sit with the discomfort of watching yourself on video. The recording is the load-bearing tool of the week; skipping it removes 70% of the value.

### Bootstrap path

If you have no screen scheduled yet, fine — Week 4 still lands. Treat the mini-project as a dress rehearsal. The first real recruiter call after Week 4 is the live run; you'll know within 5 minutes whether the prep held. If you have a screen scheduled this week, prioritize Exercises 1, 2, and 3 in that order and skip the homework's stretch problems until after the call.

## Topics covered

- The anatomy of the 30-minute recruiter call: the four questions, the seven beats, the timing
- Internal vs. agency recruiters revisited (compressed from Week 1): incentive differences that matter on the screen call itself
- "Tell me about yourself" as a 2-3 minute scripted answer, not an autobiography
- "Why this company?" — the three-reason structure, and how to prep one for every Tier-A target in under 20 minutes
- "Why are you leaving / why are you looking?" — the dignified non-answer that doesn't burn the bridge
- The comp conversation: range vs. point, base vs. total, the order of who-names-the-number-first
- levels.fyi as a public-data tool: how to read the percentiles, how to translate company/level/location into a defensible band
- The recruiter-screen logistics check: notice period, work authorization, location, interview availability — the boring questions that disqualify candidates who fumble them
- "Do you have any questions for me?" — the closing question, scored more than candidates realize
- Note-taking during the call: what to write down, what to skip
- The post-screen email: the 4-sentence follow-up that closes the loop
- Mock-screen recording, self-review, and the rubric you'll use weekly going forward

## Stretch goals

- Read **Patrick McKenzie's "Salary Negotiation"** essay end to end before the comp lecture: <https://www.kalzumeus.com/2012/01/23/salary-negotiation/>. It is the single most-cited reference in this curriculum's comp section.
- Read **levels.fyi's "How to negotiate tech salary" guide**: <https://www.levels.fyi/blog/how-to-negotiate-tech-salary>. Skim the structure; bookmark the percentile pages for the five companies on your Tier-A target list.
- Do a **listening pass on two podcast episodes** where engineers discuss recruiter calls — the *Pragmatic Engineer* podcast and Gergely Orosz's *Inside the Hiring* posts both surface real recruiter dynamics. Notice the phrases that recur.
- If you have a friend who is currently an internal recruiter at a tech company, **buy them coffee** and ask: "What's the difference between the candidates who pass your screen and the ones who don't?" Their answer will compress half of this week's lectures into 20 minutes.

## What "done" looks like by Sunday night

By the end of the week, the following should be true. If any of these aren't, stay with this week's work before moving on.

- You can deliver a 2-3 minute "tell me about yourself" answer from memory, in under 180 seconds, that maps to your resume and lands on a clear forward-looking sentence.
- You have written, in your own words, a three-reason "why this company?" answer for each of the **five** Tier-A targets you most expect to screen with in the next 4 weeks.
- You have a documented compensation band — base, target total, and a high anchor — for each of those same five companies, with a one-sentence levels.fyi (or equivalent) justification per company.
- You have **recorded at least one mock recruiter screen** (15-30 minutes), scored it against the rubric, and identified the three biggest issues you'll fix before the next real call.
- A peer has played recruiter for you for at least one full mock and given you written feedback. You've applied at least 2 pieces of that feedback.
- You have a one-page "screen-call cheat sheet" that fits on a single screen — the rough script structure, the comp numbers, the logistics answers, and the questions you'll ask at the end. This is the artifact you'll keep open during real calls for the rest of the cycle.

This is the recruiter-screen baseline. You will revise the cheat sheet after every real screen for the rest of the cycle; this week's version is v1.
