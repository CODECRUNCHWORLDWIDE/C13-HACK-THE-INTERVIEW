# Challenge 1 — Mock recruiter screen with a peer

**Time:** ~60 minutes (including coordination). **Difficulty:** Easy/Medium. **Materials:** A willing peer, a video-call tool with recording, the screen-call cheat sheet (in progress), this challenge file as the peer's script.

## Problem

You can rehearse your self-intro and read your comp band into the mirror all week. None of that approximates the live dynamic of a recruiter who interrupts mid-sentence, probes a follow-up you weren't expecting, and ends with "what questions do you have for me?" with 90 seconds left on the clock. The challenge is to run that live dynamic once before you take a real screen call.

## The protocol

### Part A — Find the peer (10 min coordination)

Ideal peer: a **C13 cohort member**, a **C2 peer**, or a **friend who has been on a hiring side recently** (former or current recruiter, hiring manager, or someone who has run 10+ screens themselves).

Less ideal but acceptable: any technical friend who can read a script and ask follow-up questions. The script in Part B is structured to compensate for a peer who hasn't run real screens.

**Not appropriate:** a close friend who knows your work so well they can't simulate fresh-eyes evaluation. They will give you the benefit of the doubt and you'll learn nothing about how a stranger would read you.

Send a one-line request:

> "Hey — I'm doing the Week-4 mock recruiter screen. Can you spend 45 minutes with me on Zoom / Meet / FaceTime? You'll play the recruiter; I'll send a 1-page script. I'll record it for self-review."

### Part B — The recruiter script (send to the peer in advance)

This is what your peer reads. Send it 24-48 hours before the mock so they have time to skim.

> ## You are playing a recruiter at a tech company
>
> Your role: an internal recruiter at a hypothetical mid-stage tech company (~150 engineers, Series C, B2B SaaS). You're running a 30-minute first-round screen for a Senior Software Engineer position.
>
> **Your job:** ask the questions below, in order, with follow-ups. Take light notes. Time-box the call to 30 minutes; warn the candidate at the 5-minutes-left mark.
>
> **Question set (use as a backbone; deviate naturally for follow-ups):**
>
> **Beat 1 — Intro (2 min).** "Hi {candidate}, thanks for taking the time. I'm {peer name} from {hypothetical company}'s recruiting team. We're hiring for a Senior Software Engineer on the platform team — does that match what you're expecting from this call?"
>
> **Beat 2 — Tell me about yourself (3-4 min).** "Great. To kick things off, can you walk me through your background — who you are, what you've been working on, what brought you here?" Let them speak. If they run under 90 seconds, say "anything else you'd want me to know about your current work?" If they run over 4 minutes, gently interject at the 3:30 mark with "got it — let me ask one follow-up here."
>
> **Beat 3 — Resume probing (4-6 min).** Pick **one project** they mentioned in the self-intro and ask a follow-up: "Tell me more about {project}. What was your specific role? What was the most technically interesting part? What would you do differently if you started over?" Then pick a *prior* role and ask "what was the biggest difference between {prior company} and {current company} in how the work was structured?"
>
> **Beat 4 — Why this company (3-4 min).** "Why is our company specifically interesting to you?" (You are a generic mid-stage Series-C B2B SaaS. They should have prepped something to substitute in. If they give a generic answer, push: "what specifically about us, vs. similar-stage companies?")
>
> **Beat 5 — Why are you looking (2-3 min).** "Can you share why you're looking right now / why you're considering leaving {current employer}?" Listen for: any badmouthing, hesitation, or signs of conflict. Don't probe deeply; this is a sanity-check.
>
> **Beat 6 — Logistics + comp (5-7 min).** Run through, one at a time:
> - "What's your notice period at {current employer}?"
> - "Will you require work authorisation / sponsorship?"
> - "Where are you currently based, and are you open to {hybrid in SF / fully remote / fully on-site}?"
> - "What kind of compensation are you targeting for this role?"
>
> For the comp question: **push back lightly once.** "That's helpful — the top end of what you mentioned is above where this role typically lands, we're usually closer to {invent a number 10-15% below their high anchor}. Is the lower end of your range still workable?" Listen for how they handle the flinch.
>
> **Beat 7 — Their questions + close (4-6 min).** "We have a few minutes left — what questions can I answer for you?" Let them ask. Answer briefly. Close with: "Thanks — I'll send a follow-up by {N days}. Anything else before we wrap?"
>
> ## After the call — feedback structure (10 min)
>
> Once the call ends, fill in the feedback form below and send to the candidate within 24 hours.
>
> 1. **Tell me about yourself (1-5):** How clear was the candidate's professional shape after the self-intro? (5 = "I could pitch them to my hiring manager in one sentence." 1 = "I have no idea what they do.")
> 2. **Resume probing (1-5):** When you asked about a specific project, did they speak about it with appropriate depth and ownership, or did they hand-wave?
> 3. **Why this company (1-5):** Specific or generic?
> 4. **Why looking (1-5):** Dignified or rambling/defensive/badmouthing?
> 5. **Comp answer (1-5):** Confident with a defensible justification, or hedging / under-anchored / over-anchored without leverage?
> 6. **Their questions (1-5):** Did they ask 2-3 informed questions about process, team, or role?
> 7. **Overall — would you move this candidate forward to a technical screen?** Yes / No / Maybe. One-sentence reason.
> 8. **One specific thing to fix before their real screen.** The single highest-leverage change.

### Part C — Run the mock (30 min)

On the call:

1. **Record.** Both video and audio. Zoom / Meet / Loom — any tool that produces a file you can re-watch. Tell the peer once, then ignore the camera.
2. **Pretend it's real.** Wear what you'd wear to a real screen, sit where you'd sit, have the cheat sheet open. Treat the peer as a recruiter you met five minutes ago.
3. **Don't pause to coach yourself.** If you fumble a beat, keep going. The recording is for review, not for fixing in real time.
4. **Take notes on the peer's side.** As if they were a real recruiter — name, stated comp band, loop structure they describe, etc. The note-taking practice itself is part of the rep.

### Part D — Review and feedback (15 min)

Within 24 hours:

1. **Watch the recording yourself first.** Note 3 things that surprised you about how you came across.
2. **Read the peer's feedback form.** Note the score per beat and the "one specific thing to fix."
3. **Compare your self-review with the peer's.** Where you agree, that's a confirmed issue. Where you disagree (you thought it was fine; they didn't), that's the more interesting feedback.
4. **Decide what to apply.** For the "one specific thing to fix," draft the fix into the screen-call cheat sheet before the next real call.

## Common findings

- **Self-intro ran 3:30+ at first attempt.** Even after Exercise 1's recording, the live dynamic stretches the intro. The fix: trim more from the path paragraph and rehearse the transitions out loud.
- **Comp answer collapsed under the flinch.** "That's higher than our band" caused you to immediately drop to "okay, what would work for you?" The fix: rehearse Lecture 2's holdthrough-the-flinch response specifically.
- **No questions at the end.** You ran out of time / forgot you'd prepared questions. The fix: write the three questions on the cheat sheet visibly enough that you can't miss them at the close.
- **Notice-period fumble.** "Uh, I think two weeks? I'd need to check my contract." Sounds unserious. The fix: know the answer to within a day.
- **Generic "why this company" because you didn't pre-fill the substitute.** Mock recruiter is a generic Series-C SaaS; you didn't draft a tailored answer in advance. The fix: pick *one* of your real Tier-A targets and substitute their three-reason answer mentally for the duration of the mock. Closer to a real-screen rep.

## Stretch

- **Run a second mock with a different peer.** Different reactions surface different issues. A non-technical peer often catches things a technical peer normalises (jargon density, assumption of context).
- **Run a mock specifically on a "weak" beat.** If the first mock revealed comp as your worst beat, schedule a 15-minute drill focused only on the comp exchange — your peer asks the question 5 different ways, you practice the response each time.
- **Reverse the mock.** Play recruiter for the peer, using the same script. Listening to them stumble through it will sharpen your own answers within the same hour.
- **Submit the recording to Yoodli or a similar tool** for an AI-assisted second opinion on filler words and pace. The metrics are blunt but useful.

## Why this matters

The mock is the highest-fidelity simulation you can run before the real call. It catches the issues that live-recording yourself in a quiet room cannot — interruption handling, follow-up depth, the flinch on comp, the energy of a stranger asking the same question 50 candidates have answered this week. The candidates who walk into a recruiter screen having done one mock outperform the candidates who didn't, on the same content, by a margin large enough that recruiters notice.

This challenge is also the foundation for the mini-project, which is the *self-only* version of the same recording. The mock is the harder, more useful version. If you can find a peer for it, do it.
