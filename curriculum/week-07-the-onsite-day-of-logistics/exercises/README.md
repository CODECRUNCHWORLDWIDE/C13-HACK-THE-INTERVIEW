# Week 7 — Exercises

Three exercises. Each operates on a different beat of the onsite loop — planning it, surviving the hostile moments inside it, and learning from it after.

1. **[Exercise 1 — Mock day script](exercise-01-mock-day-script.md)** — author an hour-by-hour run-of-show for your real or expected onsite, from the night before through the post-loop two hours, as the document you read the morning of (~90 min)
2. **[Exercise 2 — Deflection rehearsal](exercise-02-deflection-rehearsal.md)** — a recorded peer roleplay of the seven hostile-question patterns and the eleven EEOC-protected categories, scored on a per-category fluency table (~90 min)
3. **[Exercise 3 — Debrief self-eval](exercise-03-debrief-self-eval.md)** — a scored, structured self-evaluation of a loop you just completed, run within 2 hours of it ending (~60 min)

## How to work them

The order is partly fixed by what each exercise depends on. **Do Exercise 1 first** — the run-of-show is the planning artefact, and the night-before/morning-of/loop/post-loop structure it produces is what the other two exercises hang off. Exercise 2 is the deflection rehearsal; run it once you have read Lecture 3, ideally a week before any real loop so the revised scripts have time to settle into your cheat sheet. Exercise 3 is timing-bound: it is run within 2 hours of a loop ending — either the Week 7 mini-project's 4-hour mock or a real onsite — so you do not schedule it freely; you trigger it off a loop.

**The three exercises chain into the week's challenges.** Exercise 1's run-of-show names the post-loop slot where Challenge 2's after-action gets filled out; Exercise 3's self-eval is the longer-form sibling of that after-action. Treat the run-of-show, the self-eval, and the after-action template as one revisable system, not three one-off documents.

If you cannot find a peer for Exercise 2, the solo fallback (record the script-card questions, respond to playback, self-score the fluency table) holds most of the timed-delivery and self-review value, at the cost of the live-eye-contact pressure that is the point of the drill. Try hard for a peer on this one — the discomfort of having a real person ask "do you have kids" in roleplay is exactly the discomfort you want to spend now rather than in front of a hiring committee.
