# Challenge 2 — After-Action Template

**Time:** ~75 minutes (drafting the template; filling out a test run on the mini-project mock OR a recent loop). **Output:** A reusable after-action template at `c13-week-07/after-action-template.md`, plus one filled-in test run as `c13-week-07/after-action-{date}.md`. The template will be the artefact you fill out within 2 hours of every real onsite for the remainder of the cycle.

## Goal

Build a single reusable document that captures, in a structured form, everything worth remembering from a real onsite loop within 2 hours of the loop ending. The template is designed to be:

- **Fast to fill out** — 30 minutes maximum from blank to complete, even after a tiring four-hour loop.
- **Specific to your situation** — your typical company targets, your typical round types, the categories of question you are most likely to encounter.
- **Evergreen** — usable across every loop in this cycle and the next one, with only small revisions per loop.
- **Diagnostically useful** — the artefact informs your run-of-show for the next loop, your cheat-sheet revisions, and the issues you bring to your next mock or to a mentor.

The template is the substitute for trying to remember the loop a week later. It is also the substitute for trying to plan the next loop in the absence of structured data from the previous one. The candidates who run an after-action after every real loop converge on much higher hit-rates over a 5-10 loop cycle than candidates who do not.

## Setup

You need:

- **The US military's AAR format or the aviation industry's CRM debrief format** as reference. The resources file links both. Read at least the first page of each.
- **Your debrief self-eval template from Exercise 3** as a starting point. The after-action is a leaner version of the self-eval, tuned for speed.
- **A markdown file**: `c13-week-07/after-action-template.md`. The template lives here unfilled; the filled versions are dated copies.
- **A specific loop to test the template on** — the mini-project mock from this week, a recent real loop, or (if neither is available) the most-recent loop you ran in a previous cycle.

## Process

### Step 1 — Build the template (45 min)

Open `c13-week-07/after-action-template.md`. The structure below is the recommended starting point; revise it to fit your specific patterns.

```markdown
# After-action — {company / mock} — {date}

> Fill out within 2 hours of the loop ending. Total fill-out time should be
> under 30 minutes. If you find yourself going long, you are over-writing;
> trim.

## §1 — The facts (3 min)

- **Company:** ___
- **Role / level:** ___
- **Mode:** in-person / virtual
- **Date and time:** ___
- **Total length:** ___
- **Number of rounds:** ___ scheduled, ___ completed
- **Recruiter:** ___
- **Next-step timeline given by recruiter:** ___ (when I should expect to
  hear; what the next step is)

## §2 — The rounds (15 min, ~3 min per round)

For each round:

### Round {N} — {type} — {time}

- **Interviewer:** ___ (name, title if known)
- **What they asked, in one sentence:** ___
- **What I did:** ___ (one sentence)
- **Self-grade (1-5):** ___
- **What I'd change:** ___ (one sentence; the specific habit)
- **Read of the interviewer:** engaged / decided-positive / decided-negative
  / hostile-by-design / hostile-by-accident / tired / unclear
- **Hostile-pattern observed (if any):** Pattern 1-7, see Lecture 3
- **Illegal question asked:** yes / no
  - If yes: category and verbatim question: ___
  - My deflection: ___
  - My self-grade on the deflection (1-5): ___

(Repeat for every round, including the lunch interview if applicable.)

## §3 — The cross-round picture (3 min)

- **My energy curve:** name the round that was strongest, the round that was
  weakest, and the inflection point.
- **My narration coverage drift:** did I narrate more or less by round 4
  than by round 1?
- **My break discipline:** bathroom count, water consumed, snack between
  rounds yes/no.
- **The recruiter midpoint check:** what they asked, what I said, what they
  took from it.
- **The closing:** what the recruiter framed for next steps, what I said
  about how the day went.

## §4 — The signals worth noting (5 min)

- **Specific phrases the interviewers used that I want to remember:** ___
  (interviewers tend to use a small vocabulary; capture it for reuse)
- **The single specific thing that surprised me:** ___ (an unexpectedly hard
  question, an unexpectedly warm interviewer, a logistics issue I had not
  planned for, a topic I had not prepared for)
- **The single specific thing I am most proud of:** ___ (one moment in the
  loop where I produced clearly strong signal)
- **The single specific thing I most regret:** ___ (one moment I will think
  about for weeks; better to name it now than to ruminate)

## §5 — The forward (3 min)

- **The three things I will do differently in the next loop:** ___, ___,
  ___ (concrete, revisable habits)
- **The cheat-sheet revisions:** ___ (specific lines, old → new)
- **The run-of-show revisions:** ___ (which section, what change)
- **The next loop or mock scheduled:** ___ (date, company)
- **One sentence to my future self about this loop:** ___

## §6 — The legal note (1 min, only if relevant)

Only fill out if an illegal question was asked. The decision about whether
to escalate (Lecture 3 Section 9) is for tomorrow, not now. Today's job is
to record the facts.

- **Verbatim question(s) asked:** ___
- **Round and timestamp:** ___
- **Interviewer:** ___
- **My response:** ___
- **The category(ies) it touched:** ___ (one or more of the eleven EEOC
  categories or salary-history)
- **Whether the question seemed in-good-faith or hostile:** ___
- **Whether it was patterned (multiple questions touching the same
  category):** ___
- **My initial inclination on escalation:** deflect-and-private-note / surface
  to recruiter / surface to HR / decline-and-letter / file-with-EEOC
- **Decision deferred to:** {date 24-48 hours from now}

(The escalation decision is made in 24-48 hours, not in the heat of the
after-action. The note records the facts; the decision sits.)

## §7 — Stop here (timer)

If you have spent more than 30 minutes on this document, stop. Close the
file. Eat a meal. Sleep. The document is good enough. Returning to it
tomorrow will produce diminishing returns.
```

### Step 2 — Test the template (25 min)

Make a copy of the template, dated, and fill it out for a specific loop. Two options:

**Option A — The mini-project mock from this week.** The 4-hour peer mock is the freshest analog of a real loop you have. Fill out the after-action against it. The exercise is high-fidelity because the mock is a realistic loop with peer interviewers.

**Option B — A recent real loop.** If you have done a real onsite within the last 30 days, fill out the after-action retroactively. The retro version will be less accurate than a 2-hour-fresh version (some details will be lost) but is still useful for testing the template.

**Option C — A previous-cycle loop.** Less ideal; the memory is older, the names are partial, the specific phrases are mostly lost. Use only if A and B are both unavailable.

The test run produces two artefacts:

- The filled-in document `c13-week-07/after-action-{date}.md`.
- A short revision note at the bottom of the template itself: what worked, what felt clumsy, what to revise about the template before the next real loop uses it.

### Step 3 — Revise the template (5 min)

Based on the test run, revise the template. Common revisions:

- **Too many subsections.** Cut the ones you found yourself skipping.
- **Not enough specificity in the prompts.** Add concrete examples beneath each field if it helped you fill the field out.
- **Missing a section you needed.** Add it. Common additions: a "compensation context" section if you are negotiating; a "company-specific note" section for variants like Amazon's bar raiser.
- **Wrong order.** If you found yourself jumping around the document, reorder it to match your natural fill-out flow.

## Acceptance criteria

- The reusable template `c13-week-07/after-action-template.md` exists, is committed, and is yours (not the boilerplate; revised to fit your situation).
- One test run `c13-week-07/after-action-{date}.md` is filled out completely.
- The test-run document was filled out in under 30 minutes (record the actual time at the top of the document).
- The template has been revised based on the test-run experience; the revision is noted at the bottom of the template.

## Why this challenge matters

The 24 hours after a loop is the highest-leverage period in the entire cycle. The interviewers are writing their write-ups; the debrief is converging; your own performance is freshest. You cannot influence the company's process during that window, but you can record your side of it, and the recording is the only input to the next loop's plan.

The candidates who run the after-action consistently — every real loop, within 2 hours, against a template they have already built — improve faster than the candidates who try to recall the loop from memory a week later. The two interventions look similar on paper; in practice the first produces actionable data and the second produces vague impressions.

The other reason to run this challenge: the template becomes a recruiting artefact. When you eventually mentor a peer through their first cycle, the after-action template is one of the highest-leverage artefacts you can hand them. The version you build this week is the v1; you will refine it across loops; the eventual version will be one of the most useful documents in your professional library.

## Stretch — the company-specific after-action

If you are interviewing at a small set of target companies (the typical pattern from Week 1's target list), build a company-specific variant of the template for each major target. The Amazon variant has a bar-raiser section; the Google variant has a hiring-committee-not-the-interviewers framing; the Stripe variant has a one-room-model note. The variants are 80% the same as the generic template; the 20% differences are worth the extra 15 minutes of upfront work per company.

## Stretch — the after-action review with a mentor

If you have a mentor or a senior peer who has been through several cycles, share an after-action document with them within 48 hours of a real loop. Their reading produces feedback that your own re-reading cannot. The mentor's job is not to predict the company's decision (they cannot); it is to identify the patterns in your performance you cannot see yourself.

## A note on tone

The after-action should be sober and specific. The temptations to avoid:

- **The post-loop celebration tone.** "I crushed Round 2!" The document is a record, not a hype reel. If Round 2 was strong, write "Round 2 went well — specifically the X moment." Specific is better than effusive.
- **The post-loop catastrophe tone.** "I bombed Round 3." Possibly true; certainly not useful. Write what specifically did not land, and what you would change. The doom narrative does not survive the next morning's re-reading.
- **The hedged everything-is-uncertain tone.** "I'm not sure if it went well or not." Pick a read; you can revise tomorrow. The document is most useful when it commits to a position you can later test against the company's decision.

Sober, specific, committed to a read. The military's AAR format is sober by design; the aviation industry's CRM debrief is sober by design. Borrow their tone.
