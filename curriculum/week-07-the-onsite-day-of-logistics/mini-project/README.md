# Mini-Project — A 4-Hour Mock Onsite with Peers

> By the end of this week, the artefacts you'll lean on in every real onsite — the run-of-show, the seven hostile patterns, the eleven deflection scripts, the pre-call routine, the after-action template — exist on paper or in your muscle memory. The mini-project is the operational test: do they hold up across four hours, with multiple peer interviewers, with a lunch break, with one rehearsed hostile round and one rehearsed illegal-question moment, with no breaks longer than 10 minutes between rounds? The 45-minute pair mock from Week 6 was the screen-screen dress rehearsal. This is the loop dress rehearsal.

**Estimated time:** 5-7 hours, spread across Friday-Saturday (or whatever weekend works for the cohort). The mock itself is 4 hours; the prep is 60-90 minutes; the after-action is 45-60 minutes.

## What you ship

A **4-hour mock onsite with peer-played interviewers and a written debrief**, delivered as five artefacts:

1. **A 4-hour mock onsite** — four rounds plus a lunch interview, played by peers, on real shared editors (CoderPad / HackerRank CodePair / a whiteboard tool for system design / video for the behavioural and lunch), with audio recording for at least 2 of the 4 rounds.
2. **A peer-feedback record** — each peer interviewer writes a 3-5-sentence debrief after their round, scored against the rubric in §3 below. Five debriefs total (four rounds plus lunch).
3. **An after-action document** — you fill out the after-action template from Challenge 2 within 2 hours of the mock ending.
4. **A revised run-of-show and cheat-sheet** (v2 of each) — the version after the after-action, with the top 3 issues addressed.
5. **A reflection** — 500-700 words, written within 24 hours of the mock, on what the four-hour shape revealed that solo prep would not have.

All artefacts committed to `c13-week-07/mini-project/`. Recordings may be linked from outside the repo if they are large; the markdown files and the cheat-sheet text live in the repo.

## Why a 4-hour mock matters

The 45-minute pair mock from Week 6 builds the screen-screen muscle. It does not build the loop muscle. The loop muscle is structurally different:

- **You have to perform under fatigue.** The round-three quality of any candidate is meaningfully worse than their round-one quality; the practice surface for this is a multi-round mock, not a single 45-minute call.
- **You have to switch contexts across rounds.** Coding → coding → lunch → system design → behavioural is a context-switching exercise as much as a content exercise. The lunch interview's friendly tone followed by the system-design round's deep technical pressure is the kind of transition no single 45-minute mock prepares for.
- **You have to manage energy.** Water, snack, bathroom, slow breath, the cheat-sheet glance. The mechanical habits of the day are the load-bearing structure; they only become muscle memory through use.
- **You have to recover from a bad round.** Statistically, in any 4-round loop, one round will go worse than the others. The recovery between rounds — the deliberate forgetting, the reset ritual, the not-carrying-the-previous-round-into-the-next — is the muscle most candidates lack.

A 4-hour mock with peers, with real round-shapes and a real lunch interview and the deliberate seeding of at least one hostile pattern and one illegal-question moment, is the only practice surface that builds the loop muscle.

## How to run the 4-hour mock

### Setup

You need:

- **2-4 peers** who will play interviewers. Ideal: four peers, each running one round. Acceptable: two peers, each running two rounds (with a costume-change between, see below). Solo-mode fallback is described separately at the end.
- **A 4-hour block** on the calendar. Saturday morning 09:00-13:00 is the typical slot for the C13 cohort. The block must be uninterrupted; the dress-rehearsal value collapses if anyone takes a real phone call in the middle.
- **A shared editor or two** for the coding rounds (CoderPad / HackerRank CodePair). A whiteboard tool (Miro, Excalidraw, Figma, or a physical whiteboard if in-person) for the system-design round.
- **A recording setup** for at least 2 of the rounds. Audio is sufficient; video is ideal.
- **A quiet space** (in-person if all peers are local; virtual otherwise).
- **The candidate's run-of-show document** from Exercise 1 — printed or open in a second tab — as the candidate's reference.
- **The peer scripts** (Section 4 below). Each peer reads their own script before the mock.

### The schedule

A typical 4-hour mock runs:

| Time | Activity | Length | Peer |
|------|----------|--------|------|
| 09:00-09:10 | Greet, audio check, brief intro (recruiter role; one peer plays this) | 10 min | Peer R |
| 09:10-10:00 | Round 1 — Coding (medium LeetCode) | 50 min | Peer A |
| 10:00-10:10 | Between-round gap (water, bathroom, walk) | 10 min | — |
| 10:10-11:00 | Round 2 — Coding (medium LeetCode, different pattern) | 50 min | Peer B |
| 11:00-11:15 | Between-round gap (water, snack, walk) | 15 min | — |
| 11:15-12:00 | Round 3 — Lunch interview (informal but scored; includes one seeded illegal question) | 45 min | Peer C |
| 12:00-12:10 | Between-round gap (bathroom, slow breath) | 10 min | — |
| 12:10-13:00 | Round 4 — System design OR Behavioural (one of the two; flip a coin or pick by skill gap) | 50 min | Peer D |
| 13:00-13:15 | Recruiter closing (one peer plays this) | 15 min | Peer R |

Total structured time: 4 hours 15 minutes. Hard stop at 13:15.

If you only have 2 peers, Peer A also plays Peer C and Peer R (with a 5-minute break to "change role" — a deliberate context-switch on the peer's side too); Peer B also plays Peer D. The mock loses some fidelity but the loop shape holds.

### The candidate's job

Run the loop exactly as you would a real onsite:

- **Use your run-of-show document** as the morning-of plan. If the run-of-show says "30-minute warm-up at 08:30, water 500ml at 08:45," do that.
- **Treat each round as the real round.** The hostile pattern in Round 4 is real; the illegal question at lunch is real; the system-design round's pressure is real. Do not break character to ask "is this the part where I'm supposed to deflect?"
- **Use the recovery beats.** Water, bathroom, slow breath, cheat-sheet glance. The mechanical habits are the rehearsal; skipping them in the mock is skipping the rehearsal.
- **Stay until the closing.** The closing-with-recruiter at 13:00 is the last graded beat. Do not relax visibly at 12:55.
- **Recording.** Record at least Round 2 (coding under fatigue) and Round 4 (the hostile or system-design round). The recordings are for your re-watch in the self-eval.

### The peers' job

Each peer:

- **Reads their script in advance.** The scripts are in Section 4. Each peer needs about 30 minutes of prep.
- **Plays the role straight.** No mid-round coaching; no winking at the candidate; no "let me give you a hint because I want you to do well." Play the part.
- **Writes a 3-5 sentence debrief within 30 minutes of their round ending.** While the round is fresh. The debrief is the artefact the candidate will receive.
- **Joins a 15-minute group debrief at 13:15** (or at a scheduled time afterward) to discuss the candidate's overall performance, similarly to a real loop's debrief panel.

## The peer scripts

### Peer R — Recruiter (10 min greet + 15 min closing)

```markdown
# Peer R — recruiter script

## Opening (10 min, 09:00-09:10)

You are playing the recruiter. Greet the candidate warmly. Walk them
through:

- "Here's the schedule for today: four rounds, lunch, and we'll close out
  around 13:15."
- Confirm any logistics (bathroom location if in-person; the Zoom link
  layout if virtual).
- Ask how they're feeling. Listen to their answer; do not coach.
- Hand off to Peer A.

## Midpoint check (5 min, around 11:00-11:05)

Find a moment in the gap between Round 2 and lunch. Ask the candidate:
"How's it going so far?" Listen to their answer. Note (in your debrief
notes) whether the answer was composed and specific or vague.

Do not respond beyond a polite "great, glad to hear it" or "okay, good
to know." Recruiters at real loops do not coach at the midpoint.

## Closing (15 min, 13:00-13:15)

After Round 4 ends, walk the candidate through the closing. Ask:

- "How was your day?"
- "Do you have any last questions for me?"
- Outline the next-step timeline: "I'll be debriefing with the
  interviewers this afternoon and aim to follow up by {fictional date,
  e.g., Tuesday}." 
- Close: "Thanks for coming today. Looking forward to following up."

## Your debrief (3-5 sentences within 30 minutes of close)

Focus on:
- The candidate's composure at the opening
- Their midpoint answer
- Their closing answer
- Any observation about their overall presentation across the day
```

### Peer A — Round 1 Coder (50 min)

```markdown
# Peer A — Round 1 coding script

You are playing the first technical interviewer. The candidate is fresh
(round 1 of 4); your job is to give them a clean run at a medium-shape
problem.

## The problem (pick one)

### Problem A — Group Anagrams (LeetCode 49 — medium)

> Given an array of strings, group the anagrams together. You may return
> the answer in any order.
>
> Example:
>   Input: ["eat", "tea", "tan", "ate", "nat", "bat"]
>   Output: [["bat"],["nat","tan"],["ate","eat","tea"]]
>
> Expected progression: O(n*k*log k) sort-key approach → discuss O(n*k)
> count-key approach as the follow-up.

### Problem B — Longest Substring Without Repeating Characters (LeetCode 3)

> Given a string s, find the length of the longest substring without
> duplicate characters.
>
> Example: "abcabcbb" → 3 ("abc")
>
> Expected progression: brute O(n²) → sliding window with hash O(n).

## The structure of your round

- 0-3 min: greet, brief self-intro from each side
- 3-7 min: read the problem; let candidate ask clarifying questions
- 7-12 min: candidate walks through example, sketches brute force
- 12-15 min: candidate proposes optimisation
- 15-35 min: candidate codes
- 35-42 min: candidate tests
- 42-47 min: follow-up question (complexity / extension)
- 47-50 min: candidate's questions back; close

## What to score

- Problem-solving (1-5): clarifying questions, brute-force-first, optimisation
- Coding (1-5): clean, idiomatic, runs
- Communication (1-5): narration coverage, recovery after silent bursts
- Testing (1-5): ran on examples, edge cases, stress
- Closing (1-5): question quality, composure

## Your debrief (3-5 sentences within 30 minutes)

Focus on what the candidate did and did not do; the specific moments of
strong signal and weak signal; one recommendation.
```

### Peer B — Round 2 Coder (50 min, post-fatigue)

```markdown
# Peer B — Round 2 coding script

You are playing the second technical interviewer. The candidate is now
slightly more tired (round 2 of 4). The problem you give should be a
different pattern from Round 1.

## The problem (pick one)

### Problem A — Top K Frequent Elements (LeetCode 347 — medium)

> Given an integer array nums and an integer k, return the k most
> frequent elements.
>
> Example: nums = [1,1,1,2,2,3], k = 2 → [1,2]
>
> Expected progression: hash-count + sort O(n log n) → heap O(n log k) →
> bucket sort O(n) as a stretch.

### Problem B — Course Schedule (LeetCode 207 — medium)

> Given numCourses and a list of prerequisites, return whether you can
> finish all courses.
>
> Expected progression: topological sort (DFS-cycle-detect or
> Kahn's algorithm).

## Structure and scoring

Same structure as Round 1. This round is calibrated for the second-round
fatigue effect; watch for narration drift, voice softening, and any
sign that the candidate is carrying Round 1's mood into Round 2.

## Your debrief (3-5 sentences)

Specifically note any cross-round drift you observed.
```

### Peer C — Lunch interview (45 min, includes seeded illegal question)

```markdown
# Peer C — Lunch script

You are playing a peer engineer hosting the candidate at lunch.
The tone is informal — but the lunch is scored.

## The lunch

- Start by suggesting where to sit (in-person) or by chatting about your
  day (virtual). The tone is friendly, casual.
- Ask the candidate how the morning went. Note their answer in your debrief.
- Talk about the team's work for 5-10 minutes. (Make something up — you're
  on the [fictional] payments team and you've been working on a
  rate-limiting redesign.)
- Eat (or pretend to eat).
- Around minute 20, slip in one illegal question, in a warm small-talk tone:
  "By the way — do you have kids?" or "Where are you from originally?"
  Pick one. Ask it casually, conversationally; do not signal that this is
  the test.
- Note the candidate's response. Did they deflect cleanly? Did they answer?
  Did they panic? Did they lecture?
- Continue the conversation normally; do not signal a "good job" or
  "bad job" mid-lunch.
- Around minute 40, wrap up and walk them back.

## What to score

- Composure throughout: 1-5
- Engagement and conversational quality: 1-5
- Deflection on the illegal question: 1-5
- Banned-behaviour avoidance (no complaining, no inside-info pumping): 1-5
- "Would I want to work with this person on a normal Tuesday?": Yes / No / Lean

## Your debrief

Specifically note the deflection grade and the "would I want to work with
them" read.
```

### Peer D — Round 4: System design OR Behavioural (50 min, with hostile beats)

```markdown
# Peer D — Round 4 script

You are the round-4 interviewer. The candidate is now in their late-day
fatigue zone. Your job is to test composure under sustained pressure.

## Pick one of two rounds (flip a coin or pick by candidate's skill gap)

### Option A — System Design

Prompt: "Design a URL shortener that handles 100M URLs per day, with
analytics on click counts."

Expected coverage:
- Functional requirements clarification (5 min)
- API design (10 min)
- Data model and storage (10 min)
- Generation of short codes (10 min)
- Analytics pipeline (10 min)
- Failure modes and bottlenecks (5 min)

Hostile beat: At minute 25, push back on the candidate's storage choice.
"Are you sure {their choice} is right? What if I told you we don't have
budget for that?" — Pattern 1 (pushback).

### Option B — Behavioural

Run 4-5 prompts back to back, including at least two of the seven
hostile patterns:

1. "Tell me about a project that failed." (Pattern 3)
2. "Walk me through your design choice on {project they just described}.
   Why did you go that way and not {alternative}?" (Pattern 2)
3. "Are you sure that's the lesson you should take from that experience?"
   (Pattern 7 — twice)
4. "Imagine a peer engineer pushes back on your design in code review,
   and won't let it merge. What do you do?" (Pattern 6)

## What to score

- Composure under hostile pressure: 1-5
- Specificity of answers: 1-5
- Self-critique without surrender: 1-5
- Closing: 1-5

## Your debrief

Specifically note how the candidate handled the seeded hostile beat —
did they collapse, surrender, defend rigidly, or acknowledge and revise?
```

40+ would be impossible (only 35 dimensions on a 1-5). 28-34: strong; the real loop is ready. 21-27: solid; identify the two weakest beats and rehearse them before the real loop. Under 21: the loop muscle is not yet built; run a second mock before the real loop.

## The peer debrief

After the mock, schedule a 30-minute group debrief with all the peer interviewers. Each peer reads their 3-5 sentence debrief aloud. You take notes; do not defend; do not explain.

After the round-by-round debriefs, one open question: "If you were a real interviewer writing your real write-up, what would the recommendation be?" Each peer gives a one-word answer: strong hire / hire / lean hire / lean no-hire / no-hire / strong no-hire.

The aggregated peer recommendation is your loop's verdict. It is not a real verdict; it is a calibration. Compare it to the read you had on the loop yourself (Beat 7 of the self-review). The gap between your read and the peer's read is the diagnostic.

## Solo-mode fallback

If you genuinely cannot find peers, the mock can be run partially solo with significant cost. Solo procedure:

- **Self-script Rounds 1 and 2** using the peer scripts above. Read the prompt aloud; pause; respond as if to a real interviewer; record.
- **Run the lunch alone** by writing a 3-paragraph imagined-peer-conversation and grading your responses to it.
- **Run Round 4 as a solo behavioural drill** with the four hostile prompts read aloud from the script.
- The cost: you do not get the live-eye-contact pressure, the surprise of an illegal question landing in a warm tone, the actual fatigue of a real multi-hour conversation. The muscle does not build as well. Use solo mode only if peers are genuinely unavailable, and run the proper mock within 2-3 weeks before the real loop.

## Why this is the most important week of the C13 curriculum

The technical content of an onsite — the algorithms, the system design, the behavioural framework — is built across many C13 weeks and C2 weeks. The day-of muscle built this week is built in this week and (later) in real loops. There is no other practice surface for it.

The candidates who run this 4-hour mock report, almost universally, two things: their next real onsite felt shorter than this mock did, and their cognitive reserve at hour three was higher than they had any reason to expect. Both are the same effect: the mock established a much higher fatigue baseline; the real loop falls inside it. You have practised for the worst case; the typical case feels lighter by comparison.

The cost of the mock: one weekend day, three to four favours from peers, the discomfort of being scored on a lunch conversation, the discomfort of someone asking you "do you have kids" in roleplay. The cost of skipping the mock and arriving at the real loop unrehearsed: a real loop you cannot retake.

The math is obvious; only the scheduling friction is real. Schedule the mock by Tuesday so it actually happens.
