# Week 7 — Resources

Free, public, no signup unless noted. The week leans on three classes of source: (1) US federal employment-law guidance for the legal frame, (2) published company candidate guides for the loop shape, and (3) the standard interview-prep references for the behavioural and pacing content.

## US federal employment-law guidance — the legal frame

The legal framing for "what an interviewer is and is not allowed to ask" in the United States lives in two agencies' guidance. Read both. They are short. They are the source you cite — not Reddit, not a bootcamp blog — when a question lands in a real loop and you need a deflection that is calm, accurate, and unimpeachable.

- **EEOC — Pre-Employment Inquiries**. The primary federal guidance on what interviewers may and may not ask. Covers race, colour, religion, national origin, sex, pregnancy, age (40+), disability, genetic information, and citizenship. The "Prohibited Practices" pages are the most-cited:
  <https://www.eeoc.gov/employers/pre-employment-inquiries>
- **EEOC — Prohibited Employment Policies/Practices**. Companion page to the above; covers what employers may not base decisions on, with concrete examples:
  <https://www.eeoc.gov/prohibited-employment-policiespractices>
- **EEOC — Laws Enforced by EEOC**. Title VII, ADA, ADEA, GINA, Equal Pay Act — the statutes the deflection language ultimately rests on. Read at least the one-paragraph summary of each:
  <https://www.eeoc.gov/laws-enforced-eeoc>
- **US Department of Labor — Equal Employment Opportunity**. The DOL's pages on EEO complement the EEOC's; the DOL's page on the Americans with Disabilities Act is the most-useful for the disability deflection scenario specifically:
  <https://www.dol.gov/general/topic/discrimination>
- **US Department of Labor — Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) salary-history pages**. Salary-history questions are no longer federally prohibited but are prohibited in many states (California, New York, Colorado, Washington, several more). The DOL's state-by-state map is the canonical reference:
  <https://www.dol.gov/agencies/whd/state>
- **EEOC — How to File a Charge of Employment Discrimination**. You are very unlikely to ever need this from an interview. It exists. The fact that it exists is the underlying reason your polite deflection works — interviewers know the line and know what it costs to cross it visibly:
  <https://www.eeoc.gov/how-file-charge-employment-discrimination>

**Non-US jurisdictions.** This week's legal frame is US-jurisdiction. For non-US loops, find the equivalent statute and authority:

- **United Kingdom — Equality Act 2010**, enforced by the Equality and Human Rights Commission (EHRC). Protected characteristics: age, disability, gender reassignment, marriage and civil partnership, pregnancy and maternity, race, religion or belief, sex, sexual orientation:
  <https://www.equalityhumanrights.com/en/equality-act/equality-act-2010>
- **Canada — Canadian Human Rights Act**, enforced by the Canadian Human Rights Commission. Provincial human-rights codes apply for most private-sector roles:
  <https://www.chrc-ccdp.gc.ca/en>
- **European Union — Employment Equality Framework Directive** (2000/78/EC) and member-state implementations:
  <https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:32000L0078>

## Published company candidate guides

The shape of the onsite loop varies materially by company. The candidate guides below are the closest thing to "what the recruiter will actually walk you through." Read the one for your specific company before Monday; treat Lecture 1's generic shape as a fallback.

- **Google — How We Hire**. Google's official candidate guide. Covers the loop shape, the hiring committee model (the people who interview you do not decide), and the dimensions Google scores on:
  <https://www.google.com/about/careers/applications/how-we-hire/>
- **Meta — Engineering Interview Prep (Onsite)**. Meta's published onsite-prep page; covers their coding, behavioural, and system-design rounds at the SWE level:
  <https://www.metacareers.com/swe-prep-onsite>
- **Amazon — In-Person Interview**. Amazon's interview structure is unusual because of the bar raiser; this page is the official primer:
  <https://www.amazon.jobs/en/landing_pages/in-person-interview>
- **Microsoft — Interview Tips for Software Engineers**. Less detailed than the above but still the official source:
  <https://careers.microsoft.com/v2/global/en/interview-tips>
- **Apple — How We Hire**. Apple's process is more opaque than peers; the page is short but worth reading for the framing:
  <https://www.apple.com/careers/us/how-we-hire.html>
- **Stripe — Interview process** (engineering). Stripe publishes the loop shape directly; the "what to expect" page is useful:
  <https://stripe.com/jobs/interview-guide>
- **Airbnb — Interview Process**. Less formal than the above; the most useful is the engineering-blog-tagged "interviewing at Airbnb" series:
  <https://careers.airbnb.com/>
- **Netflix — How We Hire**. Netflix is unusual; the culture-fit round is heavier than at most companies and the loop is shorter:
  <https://jobs.netflix.com/work-life-philosophy>

## Glassdoor — interview process reports by company

Glassdoor is the highest-volume source of candidate-reported onsite process descriptions. The reports are anonymous, anecdotal, and individually skeptical, but the patterns across reports for a given company-and-role are usually real signal. The "Interviews" tab on a company's Glassdoor page is the surface; the "Difficulty" rating and the question banks below it are sometimes useful, sometimes noise.

- **Glassdoor — main interview surface**:
  <https://www.glassdoor.com/Interview/index.htm>
- **Glassdoor — company-specific search pattern**. Use the search box: `{company} onsite interview` or `{company} interview process software engineer`. The most-recent 5-10 reports for a given company-and-role are usually the best signal; reports older than 2 years describe pre-pandemic loops and may not match the current shape.

The skeptical lens. Glassdoor reports skew toward two populations: the candidate who got an offer (and is celebrating) and the candidate who got rejected (and is venting). The median candidate experience is underrepresented. Read at least 5-10 reports before drawing conclusions and discount the most positive and most negative as outliers.

## levels.fyi — compensation comparison for offer-stage context

levels.fyi is the canonical free public reference for engineering compensation by company, level, location, and total compensation breakdown (base, bonus, equity). It does not affect your onsite preparation directly, but the loop you are running ends in an offer conversation, and entering that conversation without the levels.fyi data is the most-common reason candidates accept below-market offers.

- **levels.fyi — main**:
  <https://www.levels.fyi/>
- **levels.fyi — company comparison tool**:
  <https://www.levels.fyi/comp.html>
- **levels.fyi — internships and entry-level data** (useful for new-grad loops):
  <https://www.levels.fyi/internships/>

The week's primary use of levels.fyi: when you are filling out the after-action template (Challenge 2), the "expected comp band for this role at this level" column comes from levels.fyi, not from speculation. Week 9 will treat negotiation in depth; this week treats levels.fyi as the reference you read for context, not the tool you negotiate from yet.

## Cracking the Coding Interview — free preview chapters

Gayle Laakmann McDowell's *Cracking the Coding Interview* is the canonical interview-prep book. The full book is paid (the public library carries it; the digital library — Libby, Hoopla — often does too). The publisher's and Amazon's free previews cover the front matter of the book, which is the relevant content for Week 7.

- **CTCI — official site, free chapters listing**:
  <https://www.crackingthecodinginterview.com/>
- **Amazon — CTCI preview**. The "Look Inside" feature usually exposes the table of contents and the first 1-2 chapters, including chapter 7 (Behavioral Questions). Search the book title:
  <https://www.amazon.com/dp/0984782850>
- **Google Books — CTCI preview**. Sometimes covers slightly different chapters from Amazon's preview; worth a second look:
  <https://books.google.com/books?id=jD8iswEACAAJ>

The chapters that matter for Week 7 specifically:

- **Chapter 7 — Behavioral Questions**. The STAR framing, the "tell me about yourself" structure, the categories of behavioural prompts. McDowell's framing is the canonical version.
- **Chapter 8 — The Offer and Beyond**. Not Week 7's focus but worth scanning for the post-loop dynamics.
- **The "Before the Interview" front matter** — the day-before and morning-of routine. McDowell's version is short and overlaps with this week's Lecture 1.

The algorithm chapters (9-17) are Week 8's surface, not Week 7's. Do not let yourself sink into them this week.

## Reading the room — short references

- **Erving Goffman — *The Presentation of Self in Everyday Life* (1959).** The canonical academic reference for how people perform in social settings, including the half-conscious signals of engagement and disengagement. Out of copyright in some jurisdictions; the public library carries it everywhere. Read at least the introduction:
  <https://www.gutenberg.org/> (search the Open Library / Internet Archive for free editions)
- **Internet Archive — *The Presentation of Self in Everyday Life***:
  <https://archive.org/details/presentationofse00goff_0>
- **Albert Mehrabian's "55-38-7" framing**. The popular (and oversimplified) reading of his research on nonverbal communication. The original is more nuanced; the Wikipedia summary is a fair gateway:
  <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Mehrabian>
- **Atul Gawande — "The Checklist Manifesto" (book)**. Public library or Internet Archive borrow. The relevance for Week 7 is the chapter on how surgeons use micro-checklists between high-stakes steps; the analogy to between-rounds resets is direct.

## Pacing and energy — short references

- **Anders Ericsson — "Deliberate Practice" papers (free preprints)**. Most of the empirical work is paywalled; the popular summaries are reasonable. The Wikipedia entry is a fair gateway and links the primary literature:
  <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Practice_(learning_method)#Deliberate_practice>
- **Roy Baumeister — ego-depletion literature (caveat: replication issues)**. The "you have a finite supply of self-control per day" claim has weakened with replication; the practical message — that decision-making degrades across a long day — survives. Read with the caveat in mind. Wikipedia summary:
  <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ego_depletion>
- **Daniel Kahneman — *Thinking, Fast and Slow* chapters on attention and fatigue.** Library; chapters 2-3 are the relevant ones.

## Hostile-question and stress-interview references

- **Harvard Business Review — "How to Handle Hostile Interview Questions" (open article)**. HBR's articles are sometimes paywalled; search for it via the HBR site and the public web archive:
  <https://hbr.org/>
- **Adam Grant — "Friction" essays**. Grant's writing on how to disagree productively without damaging the relationship is the most-actionable short reference for the "pushback without conflict" beat in a hostile round:
  <https://www.adamgrant.net/>
- **Reddit r/cscareerquestions — "stress interview" tagged threads**. Anecdotal but high-signal for which companies use stress interviews as deliberate calibration and which are just rude:

## Tools you'll use this week

- **A laptop with a markdown editor** — VS Code, Cursor, anything. The run-of-show, the after-action template, and the mini-project debrief live in your portfolio repo.
- **A peer (or two)** who will play interviewer for the mini-project. One peer for two rounds plus a second peer for two more rounds is the typical setup; a single peer for all four rounds works at the cost of variety.
- **A calendar with a 4-hour block** for the mini-project. The block must be uninterrupted; the dress-rehearsal value collapses if you take real phone calls in the middle.
- **A wired headset and a quiet room** — if the mock is remote. If the mock is in-person, a study room at a library or campus building is fine.
- **A notebook or scratch pad** — for the post-round notes between each round of the mini-project.
- **A stopwatch and a kitchen timer** — phone is fine; the stopwatch tracks the round, the kitchen timer enforces the between-round resets so you do not let one round bleed into the next mentally.

## Style references for the after-action document

- **The US military's after-action review (AAR) format**. The Army's TRADOC publication on AARs is in the public domain; the format — "what was supposed to happen / what happened / why the difference / what to do differently" — is the cleanest template for a post-loop debrief:
  <https://armypubs.army.mil/> (search "after action review" — TC 25-20 is the most-cited)
- **Internet Archive — AAR-related Army manuals**:
  <https://archive.org/> (search "Army TC 25-20")
- **The aviation industry's CRM (crew resource management) debrief format**. The "safety, content, action items" three-part structure is also a clean post-loop template. The FAA's CRM advisory circular is the public source:
  <https://www.faa.gov/regulations_policies/advisory_circulars/>

## Glossary

| Term | One-line definition |
|------|---------------------|
| **The onsite** | The four-to-six-hour, four-to-five-interviewer loop that follows the technical phone screen and precedes the offer conversation. May be in-person or virtual; structure is similar in both. |
| **The loop** | Synonym for the onsite, emphasising the multi-interviewer nature and the collective debrief that produces the actual decision. |
| **The round** | One 45-60 minute slot within the loop, scoped to a specific dimension (coding, system design, behavioural, lunch). |
| **The debrief** | The meeting (usually within 24-48 hours of the loop, sometimes synchronously after, sometimes asynchronously via a shared doc) in which the interviewers from the loop discuss the candidate and produce the hiring recommendation. The actual scoring event. |
| **The bar raiser** | An Amazon-specific role: a senior employee from outside the hiring team who participates in the loop to enforce the hiring bar. Effectively a veto. Amazon-specific terminology; analogous roles exist informally at other companies. |
| **The hiring committee** | A Google-specific role: a group that reviews the loop's write-ups and produces the recommendation. The interviewers themselves do not make the call. Other companies use similar models with different names. |
| **The lunch interview** | An informal round during the loop, usually run by a peer engineer, scoped as low-stakes but in practice often scored. Treat as a scored round. |
| **The recruiter handoff** | The recruiter's day-of role: meeting you, walking you to the first interviewer, returning to walk you to the next, the midpoint "how's it going?" check, the closing conversation. Not a scored round; affects the recruiter's write-up. |
| **The run-of-show** | The hour-by-hour plan you write for yourself before the loop — the night before, the morning of, every gap, the lunch, the closing, the post-loop two hours. |
| **Reading the room** | The skill of inferring engagement, disengagement, "they have decided," and "the interviewer is hostile by company prescription versus by personal style" from a single 45-minute round. Sub-skills: tone, follow-up patterns, the rate of clarifying questions, the closing-line phrasing. |
| **The deflection** | The polite, scripted, non-confrontational response to an illegal or near-illegal question that preserves the interview without answering the question. |
| **Protected categories (US)** | The EEOC-defined categories on which hiring decisions may not be based: age 40+, race, colour, religion, national origin, sex, pregnancy, disability, genetic information, citizenship beyond legal-right-to-work, and retaliation for prior protected activity. |
| **The hostile-question pattern** | A class of question designed to elicit a stress response from the candidate as a calibration tool, distinct from a personally rude interviewer. Seven common patterns; Lecture 3 enumerates them. |
| **The after-action template** | The 30-minute document you fill out within 2 hours of every real onsite, capturing the rounds, the interviewers, the questions, the read of the room, and the three things you'd do differently. The template is reusable across loops. |
| **The energy budget** | The cognitive and physical reserves you bring to a four-hour loop, and the practice of allocating them — water, snack, the bathroom break, the between-round resets — across the day. |
