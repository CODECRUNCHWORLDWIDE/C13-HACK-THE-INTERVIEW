# Week 7 — Quiz

Ten questions. Lectures closed.

**Q1.** A candidate notices, around minute 25 of a 45-minute round, that the interviewer's follow-up questions have shortened ("mhm, sure, and?") and the interviewer's eyes have drifted to the clock twice. The strongest read and the strongest response are:

- A) The interviewer is bored; speed up to finish quickly and end the round early.
- B) The interviewer has likely decided (positively or negatively); the remaining 20 minutes is confirmation, not building the case. Land the testing or wrap-up phase cleanly; do not introduce new directions; ask one good question at the end. If you suspect decided-negative, consider one well-chosen clarifying question that genuinely re-opens the problem.
- C) Ignore the signals; they are not reliable across a 45-minute window.
- D) Surface the perception directly: "Are you still engaged with this round?"

---

**Q2.** Under US federal law (the EEOC's enforcement framework), which of the following is the **most accurate** statement about pre-employment questions in protected categories?

- A) Asking any question touching a protected category is automatically illegal and criminally prosecutable.
- B) Questions about protected categories are sometimes asked legally for specific BFOQs (bona fide occupational qualifications); however, basing a hiring decision on the answer to a protected-category question is the federal exposure. In practice, candidates should deflect such questions without answering them; the company's legal exposure exists regardless of the candidate's intent.
- C) Questions about age are illegal under all conditions, including asking the candidate's date of birth on the employment application.
- D) Salary-history questions are federally prohibited everywhere in the United States.

**Q3.** During the lunch interview at a Meta onsite loop, the peer engineer says: "Don't worry, this part doesn't count — we're just here to chat." The strongest candidate response is:

- A) Visibly relax, share personal stories the candidate would not share in a formal round, and ask candid questions about company politics.
- B) Treat the lunch as a scored round regardless of the framing. Eat a normal-sized portion of a non-messy food; ask one thoughtful, specific question about the team's recent work; let the peer steer; avoid complaining about morning rounds; avoid pumping for inside information. Operate as if the peer will be asked, after lunch, "would you want to work with this person?"
- C) Refuse to discuss anything not directly technical; quote McDowell on the limits of behavioural interviewing.
- D) Skip lunch and use the hour to review the cheat sheet for the afternoon rounds.

**Q4.** The seven hostile-question patterns from Lecture 3 include all of the following **except**:

- A) The pushback question (Pattern 1)
- B) The "are you sure?" repeated probe (Pattern 7)
- C) The salary-history demand
- D) The interrupting interviewer (Pattern 5)

**Q5.** A candidate at hour three of a six-hour loop is feeling notably tired, has not used the bathroom since the morning, and is approaching the most-important round (system design). The strongest move is:

- A) Power through; asking for a bathroom break would look unprofessional.
- B) Ask the recruiter, on the next walk, for a 90-second bathroom break and a refill of the water bottle; use the gap for two slow breaths and one cheat-sheet glance; arrive at the next round on the schedule the round needs, not the schedule the loop wants. The 3% time cost on the round is more than recovered by the 10-20% sharpness gain.
- C) Skip the system-design round and ask to reschedule it.
- D) Drink an energy drink to power through the tiredness.

---

**Q6.** A candidate is asked, in the middle of an otherwise warm lunch interview, "Do you have kids?" The strongest response from the candidate is:

- A) "Yes, two — they're great." Answer factually; the question was small talk.
- B) "I'm not going to answer that — you can't ask me that legally." Cite the EEOC; refuse the question.
- C) "That's not something I'd typically discuss in an interview. But I can tell you that I'm fully committed to the role; my work history shows the kind of focus and continuity I bring." Deflect with a one-sentence pivot to a legitimate topic; preserve the lunch; record the question privately for the after-action.
- D) Stand up and leave the lunch.

**Q7.** The structure of a typical bigtech onsite loop, from Lecture 1, includes:

- A) One coding round, one behavioural round, one system-design round, and one lunch — 3 hours total.
- B) 2-3 coding rounds, 1-2 system-design rounds (more at senior+ levels), 1-2 behavioural rounds, and a lunch interview, totalling 4-6 hours across 4-5 distinct interviewers, with a calibrated debrief in the 24-48 hours after the loop that produces the actual decision.
- C) A single 90-minute mega-round covering all dimensions, no lunch.
- D) Pure take-home assignments, no synchronous rounds.

---

**Q8.** A candidate finds themselves stuck on a coding problem at minute 28 of a 45-minute coding round. They have written nothing in three minutes. The strongest recovery move is:

- A) Continue staring at the screen silently; the answer will come.
- B) Surrender: "I don't think I can solve this." Stop the round.
- C) Surface the stuckness clearly and invite collaboration: "I'm stuck. Let me re-examine what I have." Walk through the current approach out loud; name the specific point of confusion; explicitly invite a hint ("Is there an aspect of the input I'm not using?"). The composure-under-stuckness signal is part of the score; visibly working through the stuck moment is stronger than collapsing into silence or surrendering.
- D) Switch to a different problem and ask the interviewer to grade you on that one instead.

---

**Q9.** The midpoint "how's it going?" check from the recruiter is best handled by:

- A) A vague "fine, I guess" — keeps the recruiter from forming opinions.
- B) A detailed re-litigation of every round so far so the recruiter can correct any misimpressions with the interviewers.
- C) A composed, specific, positive answer ("Really good — Sam's coding round was a great problem and Priya's behavioural let me talk about [project]; I'm enjoying the day") that gives the recruiter material they can carry to the next interviewers and signals composure to the recruiter's own write-up.
- D) Asking the recruiter how each individual round went so far.

---

**Q10.** The after-action template is most valuable when filled out:

- A) The day after the loop, when emotions have settled.
- B) A week after the loop, after the company has communicated a decision.
- C) Within 2 hours of the loop ending — when the granular memory of each round, each interviewer's phrasing, each specific question, and each read of the room is still fresh; the document is the primary input to the next loop's run-of-show; the 24-48-hour window after which detail decays into vague impression is the cost of delaying.
- D) Only after the offer (or rejection) is received, so it can be calibrated against the outcome.

---

## Answer key

<details>
<summary>Reveal after attempting</summary>

1. **B** — The signals (shorter follow-ups, glances at the clock) indicate the interviewer has likely formed a tentative read by minute 25. The strongest move is to land the existing round cleanly and not undo it; if you suspect decided-negative, a real clarifying question that re-opens the problem can sometimes reset the read. (A) loses the round; (C) ignores reliable signals; (D) is over-direct and breaks rapport.
2. **B** — Protected-category questions are not always categorically illegal in every framing, but basing hiring decisions on the answers is the federal exposure under Title VII / ADEA / ADA / PDA / GINA. The candidate's practical move is to deflect. (A) overstates the legal frame; (C) gets age wrong (some employment applications legally ask for DOB for benefits / verification); (D) is wrong — salary-history is prohibited by state law in many states but is not yet federally prohibited.
3. **B** — Lunch is informally scored at most companies and fully scored at a few. The Meta "build your network" framing is recruiter language; the peer engineer's brief is different. Treat as scored. (A) loses the lunch; (C) and (D) miss the lunch entirely.
4. **C** — The salary-history demand is an illegal-category question (in many states) or a negotiation-anchor question (everywhere), not one of the seven hostile-question patterns in Lecture 3. The seven hostile patterns are: pushback, "why did you choose this," failure question, "what would you do differently," interrupting interviewer, hypothetical hostile, "are you sure?" repeated probe.
5. **B** — Asking for the bathroom and water on the next walk is normal, expected, and high-leverage. The recruiter routes; the round starts 90 seconds later; the candidate is sharper for the round. (A) loses the back half of the loop; (C) is excessive; (D) introduces a new variable on the day of the loop.
6. **C** — Deflect with a one-sentence pivot to a legitimate topic (the candidate's work commitment, the role itself, the team). Preserves the lunch; protects the company from legal exposure; protects the candidate from giving information that should not enter the decision process. (A) gives information that should not enter the decision; (B) lectures the interviewer and creates a hostile dynamic; (D) is an overreaction that loses the loop.
7. **B** — The typical bigtech onsite is 4-6 hours, 4-5 distinct interviewers, 2-3 coding rounds, 1-2 system-design (more at senior+), 1-2 behavioural, plus a lunch, with the debrief in the 24-48 hours after producing the actual decision. (A), (C), (D) are all wrong shapes.
8. **C** — Surface the stuckness clearly, walk through the current approach, name the confusion, invite a hint. The composure-under-stuckness signal is part of the score. (A) wastes time and loses points; (B) collapses the round; (D) is not a real option.
9. **C** — Composed, specific, positive. Names specific interviewers and rounds; gives the recruiter material to carry forward; signals composure. (A), (B), (D) are weaker; (B) in particular tells the recruiter you are anxious and lowers their assessment.
10. **C** — Within 2 hours. The granular detail decays fast; the document is most valuable as input to the next loop's plan, not as a calibration against an outcome that arrives days later.

</details>

If under 7, re-read the lecture you missed. If 9+, you're ready for the homework.
