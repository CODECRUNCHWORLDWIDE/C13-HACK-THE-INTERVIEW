# Week 9 — Resources

Free, public, no signup unless noted. The week leans on three classes of source: (1) Gayle Laakmann McDowell's free conference talks and free podcast appearances, which cover most of the *Cracking the Coding Interview* book material from the interviewer's-side perspective, (2) Hello Interview's free YouTube archive of recorded mock interviews at the live coding level, with running commentary, and (3) Carl Tashian's free essays on the psychological layer of the round — the "interview-coding mindset" framing, the inner-monologue moves, the calm-under-pressure rehearsal.

The week deliberately avoids paid courses, paywalled chapters, and signup-walled content. *Cracking the Coding Interview*, the most-cited single reference on the live coding round, is a paid book and is **not** required for the week. The book's author has hours of free public content; Week 9 cites that content instead. If the free corpus is insufficient for a specific gap, the optional paid follow-ups are listed at the bottom for after the loop.

## Gayle Laakmann McDowell — free conference talks and podcasts

Gayle Laakmann McDowell, author of *Cracking the Coding Interview* and *Cracking the PM Interview*, is the most-cited single voice on the technical-interview corpus. The books are paid. Her free content — Google Tech Talks, Stanford guest lectures, podcast appearances on *Software Engineering Daily*, *CoRecursive*, *Lex Fridman*, *Talk Python*, and her own YouTube channel — overlaps substantially with the book material and adds the interviewer-side commentary that the books under-weight. The free corpus is large enough to ship Week 9 without the book.

- **Gayle's official site (with a free-resources page)**:
  <https://www.crackingthecodinginterview.com/>
- **Gayle on YouTube — main channel** (free, hours of content, includes the canonical "How to prepare for tech interviews" talk):
  <https://www.youtube.com/@gaylelaakmannmcdowell>
- **Gayle's Google Tech Talk — "Cracking the Coding Interview"** (free, on the Talks at Google channel, ~50 minutes). This is the highest-leverage single free resource for the week:
  <https://www.youtube.com/results?search_query=gayle+laakmann+mcdowell+talks+at+google>
- **Gayle on *Software Engineering Daily*** (free podcast, multiple appearances over the years). Search the SE Daily archive for "McDowell" — the older episodes have less of the book-promotion overhead and more of the interviewer-side reflection:
  <https://softwareengineeringdaily.com/>
- **Gayle on *CoRecursive***. The CoRecursive interview is one of the most-frequently-cited single episodes on the technical-interview prep podcast circuit; the episode is free with no paywall:
  <https://corecursive.com/>
- **Gayle's blog and essays** on her site. The essay archive is the closest free analogue to the book's "what the interviewer is looking for" sections:
  <https://www.gayle.com/blog>

Watch the Talks at Google video first. Read her blog essays on "how to think about the algorithm phase" and "the five steps to solve any technical interview question" second. Listen to one full podcast appearance on a long commute or while running. The book itself is excellent but is not required for Week 9; if you already own the book, the free corpus is complementary rather than redundant.

## Hello Interview — free YouTube and free mock-interview archive

Hello Interview is the most-recently-launched free YouTube channel for the live-coding-interview corpus, with the highest-quality recorded mock interviews currently public. The channel publishes full 40-60 minute mocks with one engineer interviewing another, plus running commentary on what the candidate did well and where the round started to slip. The pacing is realistic; the candidate quality is high; the post-mortem commentary is the explicit-method overlay that most YouTube mocks omit.

- **Hello Interview — main YouTube channel** (free, full mocks plus commentary):
  <https://www.youtube.com/@hello_interview>
- **Hello Interview website** (free articles plus some paid courses; the article archive is free):
  <https://www.hellointerview.com/>
- **Hello Interview — recommended starter mocks**: search the channel for "L4 mock interview," "L5 mock interview," and "Google live coding mock." The first 30 minutes of each is the candidate solving the problem; the last 10-15 minutes is the post-mortem. Watch the candidate phase first, write down your own commentary, then watch the post-mortem and compare.

Watch three full mocks across the week. The progression matters: watch the first mock cold (no pause, just observe). Watch the second mock with the predict-and-pause loop (pause at every phase boundary, predict what the candidate will do, resume, compare). Watch the third mock with the rubric from Lecture 1 of this week in hand, scoring the candidate phase-by-phase as you watch. The third pass is the loop that builds the scoring intuition you need to grade your own mini-project recording.

## Carl Tashian — free essays on the interview-coding mindset

Carl Tashian, an engineer who has interviewed hundreds of candidates at multiple companies, publishes free essays on the psychological layer of the live coding round. The essays are short (5-15 minutes each), free, and on his personal blog. They are the most-cited free reference on the framing-and-mindset side of the round — the layer the LeetCode-grinding corpus uniformly ignores.

- **Carl Tashian's blog — main**:
  <https://tashian.com/>
- **Carl Tashian — "How to crack the coding interview" essay** (free, the canonical entry point):
  <https://tashian.com/articles/>
- **Carl Tashian — "Tactics for the technical interview" essay** (free, the practical-moves complement to the mindset essay):
  <https://tashian.com/articles/>
- **Carl Tashian on the interviewer-as-collaborator framing**. The framing is repeated across several of his essays; the canonical statement is in his "How I interview" piece:
  <https://tashian.com/articles/>

Read all of Carl's essays in one sitting on Monday or Tuesday. The total reading time is under an hour. The essays are not a substitute for the practice — no essay teaches the round — but they are the framing the practice sits inside. The candidate who has internalised the "interviewer as future colleague" framing performs visibly more calmly under pressure than the candidate who has not.

## Free problem sources for the mock-interview drills

Week 9's exercises and homework require live problems to solve. The free sources below are the canonical four; pick one or two and stick with them. Switching constantly between platforms adds friction without adding signal.

- **LeetCode (free tier)** — the largest free problem archive. The free tier covers ~80% of the most-asked problems; the company-tagged questions and recent-interview-tagged questions are behind the paid Premium tier. Stick to the free pool for Week 9:
  <https://leetcode.com/>
- **NeetCode 150 (free)** — Navdeep Singh's curated list of the 150 most-asked problems, all linked to the free LeetCode versions, all with free YouTube walkthroughs on the NeetCode channel:
  <https://neetcode.io/>
- **NeetCode YouTube channel** (free, full video walkthroughs of the NeetCode 150 and more):
  <https://www.youtube.com/@NeetCode>
- **HackerRank (free tier)** — the free tier covers the canonical interview-prep tracks. The platform is occasionally the actual round-day environment for some companies, which is a secondary reason to spend an hour in it:
  <https://www.hackerrank.com/>
- **CodeSignal (free tier)** — similar to HackerRank, used by some companies (notably some FAANG-adjacent and fintech) as the actual round-day environment:
  <https://codesignal.com/>

Pick one as your primary source. NeetCode 150 plus the NeetCode YouTube walkthroughs is the recommended combination for the curated-curated path; LeetCode's free archive plus Hello Interview's mock commentary is the recommended combination for the broader-coverage path. Both are sufficient for Week 9.

## Free mock-interview platforms

Mock interviews with another human are the highest-leverage single Week 9 activity that is not the mini-project's record-yourself drill. The free platforms below pair you with another job seeker for a live mock; the experience is qualitatively different from solving alone with a recording.

- **Pramp (free)** — the canonical free peer-to-peer mock-interview platform. You schedule a slot, the platform pairs you with another candidate, you each interview the other for 30 minutes, you switch roles, total session ~60-70 minutes. The pairing quality varies; the time investment is high but the practice signal is real:
  <https://www.pramp.com/>
- **interviewing.io (free tier)** — the free tier allows anonymous mock interviews with engineers from FAANG-tier companies. The free pool is smaller than the paid pool but is sufficient for one or two sessions in the week. The mocks are recorded with the candidate's consent and the recording is reviewable:
  <https://interviewing.io/>
- **DiscordSWE / Reddit r/cscareerquestions mock-interview matchmaking** — multiple Discord servers and subreddit threads exist for peer mock-interview matchmaking, free, ad-hoc, lower quality than Pramp but with zero scheduling friction:
  <https://www.reddit.com/r/cscareerquestions/>

Run at least one Pramp session before the end of the week. The free interviewing.io session is harder to schedule but worth the trouble if you can get one. Both platforms ask you to take both roles (candidate and interviewer); the interviewer-side practice is itself a Week-9 deliverable, since it is the fastest path to the "interviewer's-eye view" that the failure-mode lecture relies on.

## Free reading on the "interviewer's side" of the round

Most of the technical-interview-prep corpus is written from the candidate's perspective. The under-supplied free corpus is the interviewer's perspective — what the interviewer is actually scoring, what makes the difference between a "weak yes" and a "weak no" in the post-round debrief, what the calibration meeting two weeks later actually looks like. The free resources below cover this gap.

- **Steve Yegge — "Get that job at Google"** (free essay on Steve Yegge's blog, 2008, dated but still cited):
  <https://steve-yegge.blogspot.com/2008/03/get-that-job-at-google.html>
- **Joel Spolsky — "The Guerrilla Guide to Interviewing"** (free essay, multiple versions over the years on Joel on Software). The interview-as-signal-extraction framing is the closest free analogue to the "interviewer's-eye view" that the rest of the corpus omits:
  <https://www.joelonsoftware.com/2006/10/25/the-guerrilla-guide-to-interviewing-version-30/>
- **Patrick McKenzie — "Salary Negotiation"** essay (free, the canonical free reference on the offer-stage conversation; out of scope for Week 9 but linked here for Week 11):
  <https://www.kalzumeus.com/2012/01/23/salary-negotiation/>
- **Yossi Kreinin — "How to write good interview questions" / "What interviewers want"** (free essays on his blog, useful for the "what is the interviewer's optimisation function" question):
  <https://yosefk.com/>
- **John Washam — Coding Interview University** (free, GitHub, the canonical free preparation-path repository, ~250k stars):
  <https://github.com/jwasham/coding-interview-university>

The Joel and Yegge essays are dated (both pre-2010) but the framing has held up. Read them in one sitting. They will not teach you the round; they will calibrate your understanding of what the round is for.

## Free recording and self-grading tools

The mini-project pivots on recording yourself coding and grading the recording. The free tools below are the canonical options.

- **OBS Studio** (free, open source, the canonical screen-recording tool):
  <https://obsproject.com/>
- **Loom (free tier)** — screen recording with built-in webcam overlay. The free tier covers 25 recordings up to 5 minutes each; sufficient for the week's drills if you stitch the recording across files, but OBS is the better single-tool answer:
  <https://www.loom.com/>
- **Zoom — record-to-local** (free with a Zoom account). Start a Zoom meeting with just yourself, share your screen, record locally. The output is an MP4 you can re-watch:
  <https://zoom.us/>
- **macOS QuickTime — Screen Recording** (free, built in to macOS). Cmd-Shift-5; pick "Record Entire Screen" or "Record Selected Portion":
  built-in
- **Windows — Xbox Game Bar Screen Recording** (free, built in to Windows 10+). Win-G to open, click the record button:
  built-in

Any of the above is sufficient. OBS is the most-flexible and the most-portable across operating systems; the built-in tools (QuickTime, Xbox Game Bar) have zero setup friction. Pick one before Tuesday; the rest of the week assumes the recording capability is live.

## Optional paid follow-ups (for after the loop)

These are flagged as paid and are listed only for the post-loop reflection. **None is required for Week 9.**

- **Cracking the Coding Interview** by Gayle Laakmann McDowell — the canonical paid book. The free corpus above covers most of the same ground. Public libraries (Libby, Hoopla, OverDrive) carry it for free borrowing.
- **AlgoExpert** — the paid platform from the YouTube channel. The free YouTube content covers the basics; the paid platform adds the curated set of 160+ problems and the mock-interview tool.
- **interviewing.io paid tier** — unlocks the larger pool of engineer-interviewers from FAANG-tier companies. The free tier is sufficient for one or two sessions; the paid tier is the right post-loop investment if you have a specific company target and want to drill with engineers from that company.
- **Educative.io — Grokking the Coding Interview** — the paid pattern-recognition course. The patterns it teaches (sliding window, two pointers, fast-and-slow, etc.) are covered for free across the NeetCode YouTube playlist; the paid course is the assembled-in-one-place version.

The free corpus is sufficient. The paid resources are listed for completeness and for the post-loop investment decision; they are not in the week's critical path.
