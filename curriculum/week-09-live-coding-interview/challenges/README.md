# Week 9 — Challenges

Two challenges. Each isolates one of the two failure modes that a solo timer cannot drill — the human-delivered hint, and the side-by-side comparison of over-built versus simple code.

1. **[Challenge 1 — The hint-ego drill](challenge-01-the-hint-ego-drill.md)** — one 45-minute pair mock with a partner injecting at least three hints whether or not you need them, drilling the take-the-hint pattern (pause, acknowledge, integrate, pivot) without ego, scored on a six-axis rubric by both sides (~1 hr including the role swap)
2. **[Challenge 2 — The over-engineering trap](challenge-02-the-over-engineering-trap.md)** — solve LeetCode 136 twice, once production-quality and once interview-simple, then compare the two versions side by side to make the cost of over-engineering visible (~1 hr)

Both are optional but high-leverage, and both depend on the exercises and lectures being done first. Challenge 1 attacks failure mode 4 — ignoring the hint — which is the hardest failure mode to rehearse alone because a solo timer cannot manufacture the human-delivered hint or the social pressure of conceding a wrong approach. Challenge 2 attacks failure mode 1 — over-engineering — which the candidate with strong software-engineering instincts struggles with most; the calibration comes from running both versions and seeing the line-count and signal-density gap directly.

Challenge 1 needs a partner — a study buddy, a Pramp pairing, an interviewing.io session, or another C13 candidate in the cohort. It is the most-expensive single drill of the week but the highest-leverage; run it twice per week during an active cycle. Challenge 2 is solo and usually one-and-done — the lesson is calibration, and once calibrated it sticks; keep the 5-line simple-version file on hand to re-read the morning of any onsite.

Run Challenge 1 first if a real live-coding round is imminent (the take-the-hint reflex is the one that fails live); run Challenge 2 first if your default is to write the production-quality version under interview pressure. Both pair off the exercises — Challenge 1 puts the stuck-recovery and clarification drills under a live partner, Challenge 2 sharpens the complexity-defence discussion into a trade-off you can deliver in two sentences.
