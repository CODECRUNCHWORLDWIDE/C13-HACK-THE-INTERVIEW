# Week 6 — Exercises

Three exercises. Each operates on a different beat of the technical phone screen.

1. **[Exercise 1 — Mock screen with a peer](exercise-01-mock-screen-with-peer.md)** — run a full 45-minute pair mock on a real shared editor with a peer playing interviewer, recorded and scored (~90 min including coordination)
2. **[Exercise 2 — Narrate on CoderPad](exercise-02-narrate-coderpad.md)** — solo narration drill: three easy LeetCode problems on CoderPad with recording, scored against the narration rubric (~90 min)
3. **[Exercise 3 — Handle the follow-up question](exercise-03-handle-the-followup-question.md)** — drill the four follow-up patterns interviewers use in the last 5-10 minutes (~60 min)

## How to work them

The order matters. **Do Exercise 2 first** — the solo narration drill is the cheapest place to make your mistakes, and it lets you walk into the pair mock already-fluent on the editor and on the five-phase loop. Exercise 1 is the pair mock; it pressure-tests the narration loop in real conditions against a live partner. Exercise 3 is targeted prep for the last 5-10 minutes of the call, which most candidates underprep.

**Don't claim technical-screen readiness until all three exercises are done.** A great pair mock with no follow-up prep is a strong-start-weak-finish; a great follow-up drill with no live-coding rep is theoretical knowledge that fails in the call.

If you cannot find a peer for Exercise 1, run it in solo mode with the recorded script — you'll lose the live-interruption rep and the surprise-hint dynamic, but the timed delivery and the self-review against the rubric still hold most of the value. Try harder for a peer than you would for an HM-screen mock; the technical screen's pair-mock fidelity is the highest of any call in the pipeline.
