# Challenge 1 — The 30-second test

**Time:** ~60 minutes. **Difficulty:** Easy/Medium. **Materials:** Your v2 resume PDF, a stopwatch, two willing peers.

## Problem

A recruiter spends roughly 30 seconds on the first pass of a resume. In that 30 seconds, they answer four questions:

1. **Who are you?** (Name + most recent title + company.)
2. **How senior are you?** (Total years of experience, leveling signal.)
3. **Does any part of your background match the role I'm filling?** (Keyword hit, recent project relevance.)
4. **Anything weird?** (Gaps, job hops, mismatched title for the level, formatting that makes me skip.)

You're going to find out whether your resume answers those questions in 30 seconds.

## The protocol

### Part A — Self-skim (10 min)

Print your v2 resume PDF (or open it in a fresh window, fullscreen). Don't look at it yet.

Open a stopwatch. Start it. Look at the resume for exactly **30 seconds.** Then close the window.

In a new file `c13-week-02/challenge-01-30-second.md`, write down — *from memory* — what you saw:

- The first thing your eye landed on.
- Your name (did you see it?).
- Your most recent title and company (did you see them clearly?).
- Total years of experience (did you compute it, or did your eye skip the dates?).
- Three specific bullets / skills you remember.
- Anything that made your eye stall or skip.

This is the test. **What you remember in 30 seconds is approximately what a recruiter remembers.** Anything you didn't remember might as well not be on the resume.

### Part B — Peer skim (40 min — including coordination)

Find **two peers** — from your C2 study group, your C13 cohort, or anyone willing to spend 5 minutes. Ideally one who knows your work and one who doesn't.

For each peer:

1. Send them your v2 PDF in a fresh email or chat thread. Don't preface it.
2. Tell them: "Look at this for exactly 30 seconds. Then close it. I'll send a few questions."
3. After 30 seconds, send them this question set:
   - What's the most recent role on this resume? (Title + company.)
   - How many years of experience does this person have?
   - What stood out — name one specific bullet, skill, or section.
   - What confused you or made you stop reading, if anything?
   - On a scale of 1-5, how much do you want to talk to this person about a senior backend role at a fintech? (Or substitute the role you're targeting.)

4. **Wait for their answers before sharing anything else.** Do not coach.

### Part C — Diagnose (10 min)

Compare the three skim results (yours + two peers) in your write-up. For each question:

- Did all three skims surface the *correct* answer?
- Did anyone surface a *wrong* answer? If so, what's the layout / wording defect that caused it?
- Did anyone get *distracted* by something that wasn't the most important thing? If so, what was pulling their eye?

## Acceptance criteria

- [ ] `c13-week-02/challenge-01-30-second.md` contains:
  - Your self-skim results.
  - Two peer-skim transcripts (questions + their answers).
  - A per-question diagnosis comparing the three skims.
  - A "Top 3 layout / content changes to make based on this test" section.
- [ ] You have applied at least **one** of the changes to your v2 resume before submitting the mini-project.

## Common findings

- **Name not seen.** Header treatment is too decorative; the actual name is buried or in the wrong font weight.
- **Most recent title misread.** "Senior Engineer" got confused with the company name because of column layout or font choices.
- **YoE undercounted.** Date format inconsistency caused the peer to skip one job or misread an internship as a full-time role.
- **Wrong thing remembered.** A peer remembered a side project from 2019 because it had a flashy company name, but ignored your most recent role at a less-famous company. Implies the visual hierarchy is upside down.

These are all common. None require throwing away your resume. They're 10-minute fixes once you see them.

## Stretch

- Run the test with **five** peers, not two. Patterns emerge with n=5 that you can't see with n=2.
- Run the test on **three different versions** of your resume from Exercise 3 (the tailored versions). Does the 30-second outcome change per JD-focus? It should.
- Reverse the test: skim a peer's resume for 30 seconds. Tell them what you remember. Notice what their layout does and doesn't surface.

## Why this matters

You will revise your resume across the cycle. Every revision risks breaking the 30-second story. After this challenge, **before any major resume change, run the 30-second test again.** Five minutes of peer skim saves you from publishing a resume that reads great line-by-line but loses the recruiter in the first half-page.
