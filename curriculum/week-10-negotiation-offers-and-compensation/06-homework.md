# Week 10 Homework — Six Items, ~6 Hours, With Rubric

The homework integrates the week's reading and drills into work products you can carry forward. One of the six items is "write your own salary letter" — the artefact you would actually send if an offer landed tomorrow.

## Item 1 — Read Patrick McKenzie's salary-negotiation essay end to end

**Time:** 90 minutes. **Difficulty:** Easy.

Read https://www.kalzumeus.com/2012/01/23/salary-negotiation/ end to end. Do not skim; read with a pen and a notepad.

Write down:

- Three specific phrases you would use in your own negotiation that you would not have come up with on your own.
- One section of the essay you disagree with or think has aged poorly. The essay is from 2012; the framing has aged well but not all the specifics have.
- One claim in the essay that surprised you.

**Deliverable:** A half-page of notes with the three phrases, the one disagreement, and the one surprise. Attach to your capstone narrative.

**Rubric:**

- 4/4: Specific quotes from the essay; thoughtful annotation on disagreement and surprise.
- 3/4: Specific quotes; surface-level annotation.
- 2/4: General notes without specific quotes.
- 1/4: Did not finish the read.

## Item 2 — Run the levels.fyi calibrated read for your target triple

**Time:** 60 minutes. **Difficulty:** Easy.

Pick your target company, level, and location. Filter levels.fyi by the last 24 months. Identify the 25th, 50th, 75th, and 90th percentile of total comp. Decompose by base, sign-on, stock, and bonus at the median.

Cross-check by pulling the BLS Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics for "Software developers, applications" (SOC 15-1252) in the same metro. Compute the BLS-to-levels.fyi cash ratio.

**Deliverable:** A one-page document with the calibrated read in the format from Lecture 2 step 8. Use the template:

```text
Company / Level / Location:    [your target]
Filter:                        Last 24 months, n=[X] submissions ([Y] outliers excluded; effective n=[Z])
25th percentile total comp:    $[X]k/year
Median total comp:             $[X]k/year
75th percentile total comp:    $[X]k/year
90th percentile total comp:    $[X]k/year
Component decomposition (median):
  Base:                        $[X]k
  Sign-on (amortised over 1 year): $[X]k
  Stock (annual):              $[X]k
  Bonus (target):              $[X]k
BLS metro median cash:         $[X]k
Tech-company-to-BLS cash ratio: [X.XX]
Walk-away (your number):       $[X]k total / $[X]k base
Target (your number):          $[X]k total / $[X]k base
```

**Rubric:**

- 4/4: All numbers filled in; outliers identified and excluded; ratios computed correctly.
- 3/4: Numbers filled in but outliers not flagged.
- 2/4: Partial data; no BLS cross-check.
- 1/4: Did not complete.

## Item 3 — Write your own salary letter (the capstone homework item)

**Time:** 75-90 minutes. **Difficulty:** Hard.

You have just received a verbal offer from your top-choice company. The recruiter has said:

- Base: $155k
- Sign-on: $25k
- Stock: $200k over 4 years (25/25/25/25, 1-year cliff)
- Bonus: 12% of base
- Year-1 total: ~$233.6k
- Deadline: 10 business days from offer letter date

Your calibrated read of the triple says the median total comp is $260k year-1 and the 75th percentile is $295k. You have no competing offer in hand, but you have one process at a similar-tier company that is 2 weeks from a likely offer.

**Your task:** Write the email you would send to the recruiter after the verbal offer call, requesting the written offer letter and signaling your intent to negotiate. Then write the email you would send 5 business days later as your counter-offer.

The first email should:

- Thank the recruiter genuinely.
- Request the written offer letter.
- Defer the substantive comp response.
- Not contain any specific counter numbers.

The second email should:

- Reference the written offer letter.
- Reference the pre-signing questions (which you would ideally have already asked by phone, but for the exercise, raise them in writing).
- Name the specific counter numbers, by line item.
- Reference the calibrated data without quoting levels.fyi by name (recruiters know the source).
- Reference the in-process competing offer at the appropriate level of disclosure.
- Be 250-400 words.

**Deliverable:** Both emails, drafted in full. Submit as a single document with a heading separating the two.

**Rubric:**

- 4/4: Both emails are specific, calibrated, and would actually be sent. Counter numbers are anchored on the calibrated data. Pre-signing questions are included. The in-process competing offer is referenced honestly.
- 3/4: Both emails are drafted; one is weaker (too vague, too aggressive, or missing a key move).
- 2/4: Only one email is drafted, or both are sketches rather than send-ready.
- 1/4: Did not complete.

This homework item is the "write your own salary letter" deliverable referenced in the brief. It is the artefact you would carry into the next real offer.

## Item 4 — Cost-of-living comparison across two real cities

**Time:** 60 minutes. **Difficulty:** Medium.

Pick two real cities you would consider for your next role. Use NerdWallet's cost-of-living calculator AND numbeo's cost-of-living index to convert a $250k year-1 total comp in City A to its City B equivalent.

Then apply the state-income-tax adjustment by hand using the state tax tables in resources.md.

Then add a one-paragraph note on three qualitative factors that the spreadsheet does not capture and that would affect your decision.

**Deliverable:** A one-page comparison document showing:

- The two cities.
- The NerdWallet conversion (City A $250k → City B equivalent).
- The numbeo conversion (same comparison).
- The state-income-tax adjustment on the post-tax purchasing power.
- The federal-tax adjustment at the marginal bracket.
- The qualitative paragraph with three named factors.

**Rubric:**

- 4/4: All four numerical comparisons present; qualitative factors are specific and personal.
- 3/4: Numerical comparisons present but one is computed incorrectly; qualitative factors are surface.
- 2/4: Only NerdWallet or only numbeo; no tax adjustment.
- 1/4: Did not complete.

## Item 5 — Draft your decline-gracefully email

**Time:** 30 minutes. **Difficulty:** Medium.

Pick a hypothetical scenario: you accepted Company A; you are declining Company B. Write the decline email, 120-200 words, following the template from Challenge 2.

The email must:

- Reference the recruiter by name.
- Name something specific they did during the process.
- Name the company you accepted.
- Name the specific reason for the decision.
- Leave the door open.
- Close warmly.

**Deliverable:** One email, full draft, send-ready.

**Rubric:**

- 4/4: Email is specific, warm, professional, send-ready. Would not embarrass on re-read in five years.
- 3/4: Email is competent but slightly generic in one or two sections.
- 2/4: Email is short and dismissive; misses warmth or specificity.
- 1/4: Did not complete.

## Item 6 — The pre-signing question list, customised

**Time:** 30 minutes. **Difficulty:** Easy.

Take the seven pre-signing questions from Lecture 3 and customise them for the actual company and role you are most likely to interview at next. For each question, write down:

- The standard form of the question.
- Why the answer matters specifically to you.
- The form in which you want the answer (email, phone, in offer letter).

Then add two more questions that are specific to your situation but not in the standard list. Possibilities:

- "What is the path from L3 to L4 at this company, and what is the typical timeline?"
- "Is there a specific team I would be joining, or will I be assigned after starting?"
- "What is the company's policy on conference attendance and travel for engineers?"
- "Is there a sabbatical program at this company, and what are the eligibility requirements?"

**Deliverable:** A one-page customised pre-signing question list with nine total questions, the rationale for each, and the preferred form of the answer.

**Rubric:**

- 4/4: Nine questions; rationales are specific and personal; preferred form is named for each.
- 3/4: Nine questions; rationales are present but somewhat generic.
- 2/4: Fewer than seven questions; or generic rationales.
- 1/4: Did not complete.

## How the homework feeds the capstone

The capstone mini-project (in `mini-project/`) is the integration of all six homework items into a single offer-evaluation, negotiation, and decision narrative. The artefacts produced in the homework are the inputs to the capstone:

- Item 1's notes inform the negotiation moves you commit to in the capstone narrative.
- Item 2's calibrated read is the data anchor for the capstone's negotiation.
- Item 3's salary-letter drafts are referenced in the capstone narrative as the exact emails you would send.
- Item 4's cost-of-living comparison is the secondary decision factor in the capstone.
- Item 5's decline-gracefully email is the closing communication in the capstone narrative.
- Item 6's pre-signing question list is the diligence layer in the capstone narrative.

Do not do the capstone without the homework. The capstone is the integration; without the inputs, it is fiction.
