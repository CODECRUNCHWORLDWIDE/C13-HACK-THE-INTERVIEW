# Exercise 3 — The Multi-Offer Frame Drill

**Time budget:** 30 minutes total. Two sub-scenarios at 10-12 minutes each, plus a 5-minute reflection.

**Format:** Solo with a written response, or partnered.

## Why this drill exists

Competing offers are the single highest-leverage move in the negotiation conversation. The candidate with a real competing offer at $230k can move the original offer from $200k to $225k+ with a 30-minute phone call; the same candidate without a competing offer moves the same original offer from $200k to $208k with the same 30 minutes. The leverage is real and is the largest single lever in the negotiation.

But the move is also the most over-used and the most easily mishandled. The candidate who bluffs ("I have a competing offer at $250k" with no actual competing offer) destroys credibility and sometimes the entire negotiation. The candidate who has a real competing offer but discloses the specific dollar number too early caps their own upside. The candidate who has a competing offer at a lower number wastes the move by trying to leverage what is actually weaker leverage.

This drill rehearses the calibrated competing-offer move — when to use it, how to frame it, what to disclose, and what to do when the recruiter asks for verification.

## Sub-scenario A — The honest strong competing offer

You have an offer from Company A at $220k total comp, year-1. You have a written competing offer from Company B at $260k total comp, year-1, with a 7-day deadline. You prefer Company A's role; you would take Company A at total comp parity (i.e. at $260k). At the current $220k offer, you would take Company B.

The Company A recruiter calls. The conversation reaches the comp moment.

> **Recruiter (Company A):** "I know you have been looking at a few opportunities. Are there other offers in the picture, and is there anything we should know about as we think through this together?"

**Your task:**

1. **Decide what to disclose.** The strongest play here is to disclose that you have a competing offer at a higher number, but to be specific about the dollar value only if (a) you are confident the company can match or close most of the gap, or (b) the recruiter pushes specifically for the number.
2. **Write the response.** Three to five sentences. The response should reference the competing offer, frame Company A as your preference, and ask whether there is room to close the gap.
3. **Anticipate the follow-up.** The recruiter will likely ask "could you share the offer letter so we know what we are comparing against?" Decide your response in advance. The honest move: "I can describe the package — base, sign-on, stock — but I would prefer to keep the specific company anonymous unless we get to the close." Most recruiters accept this.

The strong response in this scenario will:

- **Name the competing offer's existence.** "Yes, I do have a competing offer in hand."
- **Frame Company A as the preference.** "Company A is my preferred opportunity for [specific reason — the team, the technology, the role scope]."
- **Disclose the comp gap directionally, not precisely.** "The competing offer is meaningfully above where Company A is right now — by roughly $30-40k in year 1 — and I want to see if we can close the gap."
- **Invite the next move from the recruiter.** "What is the headroom on Company A's side?"

### Why disclose the gap range but not the exact number

The range ("$30-40k") gives the recruiter enough to bracket the close ("we will need to find $30k to win"). The exact number ($40k) would tell the recruiter the minimum they need to bid ($241k = $1k above the competing). The range preserves the candidate's upside if the recruiter is willing to come in above the minimum to lock the close.

This is a refinement on the simpler "do not disclose at all" advice. At the offer-call stage, do not disclose. At the counter-call stage, when the negotiation is substantive and the recruiter is asking what it would take, the directional range disclosure is appropriate.

## Sub-scenario B — The verbal-only or in-process competing offer

You have an offer from Company A at $220k total comp. You are in the final round at Company B; the recruiter at Company B has indicated verbally that an offer is "very likely" but no written offer has landed. The Company B process will conclude in 5-7 days.

The Company A recruiter asks the same question.

> **Recruiter (Company A):** "Are there other offers in the picture?"

**Your task:**

1. **Decide whether to mention Company B.** The "in-process not yet at offer" is the weakest of the competing-offer framings. Mentioning it gives weak leverage but is honest; not mentioning it preserves credibility but forfeits the (limited) leverage.
2. **Write the response.** The strong response acknowledges the situation honestly without over-stating it.
3. **Plan for the timeline pressure.** Company A wants you to sign within 7-10 days; Company B's offer is 5-7 days away. The negotiation needs to either (a) delay Company A's deadline until Company B's offer lands, or (b) decide on Company A without Company B's number on the table.

The strong response will:

- **Acknowledge the in-process situation honestly.** "I am in the final stages with another company. They have indicated verbally that an offer is likely in the next week, but it is not in writing yet."
- **Frame Company A as the preference if true.** "I am leaning toward Company A regardless of the other process."
- **Ask for the timeline alignment.** "Would it be possible to extend the decision deadline by 5-7 days so I can have both offers in hand and make a fully informed decision?"
- **Not over-state the competing offer.** Specifically, do not name a dollar number for the in-process offer; you do not have one. Naming a guessed number is bluffing.

### When to walk away from the in-process leverage

If Company A's recruiter says "we cannot extend the deadline; we need a decision by Friday," the candidate has to decide:

- **Accept Company A's offer as-is.** Forfeit the option value of Company B.
- **Decline Company A.** Bet that Company B's offer will land and will be acceptable.
- **Push the deadline anyway.** "I understand the constraint. Could you check with the team — even a 3-day extension would help me make the right call." Sometimes works.

The decision depends on your read of Company B's likelihood (verbal indication is sometimes 90% and sometimes 30%; you know the recruiter better than this drill does) and on your preference between the two if both offered. There is no universally right answer; the drill is in producing a calibrated response either way.

## Sub-scenario C (optional, advanced) — No competing offer

You have an offer from Company A at $220k. You have no other live processes. The Company A recruiter asks the same question.

> **Recruiter (Company A):** "Are there other offers in the picture?"

**Your task:**

Resist the urge to bluff. Write down the honest response.

The strong response will:

- **Answer honestly.** "I do not have another written offer in hand right now."
- **Reframe to the substantive case.** "I have done careful research on the comp distribution for this level and location, and I would like to walk through the package on the substantive side."
- **Pivot the negotiation off the competing-offer axis and onto the data axis.**

The negotiation without a competing offer is harder but not lost. The candidate who pivots cleanly to the data ("the levels.fyi median for this triple is $X; the offer is at $Y; can we close the gap?") still produces movement — typically $5-15k of sign-on or a $10-30k stock bump. The candidate who bluffs a competing offer and is exposed can lose the entire offer.

## Reflection (5 minutes)

Write down:

1. **In which sub-scenario did you most want to over-state your leverage?** The temptation is real and almost universal. Note it.
2. **What is your default frame for the "are there other offers" question?** Write down the exact sentence you would use. Memorise it.
3. **If you got the in-process offer (sub-scenario B) and the verbal Company B offer fell through, what would you do?** This is the post-mortem scenario. Plan for it before you are in it.

## What "done" looks like

By the end of the 30 minutes, you have:

- Three written responses (one per sub-scenario, including the optional no-competing-offer scenario if attempted).
- A noted default for the "are there other offers" question that you would use under pressure.
- A clearer sense of which competing-offer framing matches your actual situation in the next live negotiation.

The drill is about calibration. Strong leverage is leverage; weak leverage is liability; no leverage is no leverage. The strong negotiator knows which they have and frames the conversation accordingly.
