# Solutions — Exercises 1, 2, 3 (Strong-Response Scripts)

These are not the only acceptable responses. They are calibrated responses that demonstrate the moves the lectures cover. Use them as comparison, not as a script to memorise verbatim — the candidate who reads a memorised script on a phone call sounds like they are reading a memorised script, and the negotiation suffers.

For each exercise, the strong response is shown along with:

- **The inner-monologue narration** — what the strong candidate is thinking while saying the words.
- **The recruiter's likely counter-move** — what they will say next.
- **The response to the counter-move** — what to do after the recruiter pushes back.

## Exercise 1 — Strong responses

### Scenario 1 — The recruiter screen, opening minute

**Recruiter's question:** "What kind of compensation are you looking for in your next role?"

**Strong response:**

> "Thanks for asking — I want to think about that the right way. At this stage I am more focused on whether the role and the team are the right fit; I think the comp conversation gets more productive once we both know we are excited about each other on the substance. What I can say is that I am looking for a competitive package that reflects the level, the location, and what I bring to the role. Could you share what the comp band looks like for this level at the company? That would help me calibrate against what is realistic for this opportunity."

**Inner-monologue narration:**

> "She is asking me to name a number. The number is the anchor. If I name the bottom of the band, the offer comes back at the bottom of the band. If I name the top, she may flag me as out of range and end the conversation early. The right move is to not name the number — defer to a later stage, pivot to the substantive conversation, and ask her for the band instead. The phrase 'competitive package' is intentional; it is vague enough to commit to nothing and warm enough to not seem evasive."

**Recruiter's likely counter-move:**

> "I understand. We do not typically share the band at this stage — it varies based on the level and the candidate. But to make sure we are not wasting each other's time, could you give me a ballpark? I do not need a specific number, just a range."

**Response to the counter-move:**

> "Sure. Based on what I have seen for software engineers at this level in this market, I would expect total comp in the range of $200-240k. I want to be clear that I am open to learning more about the package and the role before refining that — but that is the rough range I am calibrating against."

**Why this works:**

- The range ($200-240k) is wide enough to not anchor the recruiter on a specific number.
- The range is anchored at the calibrated 50th-75th percentile of the levels.fyi distribution, not at the 25th-50th. The recruiter cannot use the range to under-cut.
- The phrase "open to learning more about the package and the role before refining" leaves the candidate room to negotiate above the upper end of the stated range if a competing offer arrives later.

### Scenario 2 — The recruiter pushes back (after the deflection)

**Recruiter's escalation:** "I appreciate that, but I really do need a number to know if we are in the same ballpark."

**Strong response (the range version):**

> "Understood. Based on what I have seen for this level and metro, I would expect total compensation in the range of $200-240k per year. I want to flag that I am still in the early stages of evaluating opportunities, so my read is calibrated on the market rather than on a specific competing offer."

**Strong response (the deeper-dodge version):**

> "I hear you. The reason I am hesitant to give a number right now is that I think we both benefit from anchoring on the level and the band before we anchor on a specific candidate dollar number — otherwise the conversation gets stuck on either of us second-guessing the number rather than evaluating the fit. Could you share the level you are interviewing me for? That would help me give you a more useful answer."

**Inner-monologue narration:**

> "She pushed. I have two options: give a range that does not undercut me, or push back one more time with a different deflection. The range is the safer move; the deeper dodge is the higher-leverage move but is more confrontational. I will pick based on her tone — if she sounded reasonable, I push back once more; if she sounded frustrated, I give the range. Either way, no specific number; the band is the anchor I want, not my number."

**Recruiter's likely counter to the range:**

> "Got it, $200-240k is in our range for this level. Let me move forward with scheduling the next round."

**Recruiter's likely counter to the deeper dodge:**

> "Fair enough. The role is at the [level X] band. That band is roughly $185-230k total comp at this location. Does that work for you as a starting frame?"

**Why both work:**

The range moves the recruiter forward to the next round without anchoring the candidate below the band. The deeper dodge gets the recruiter to name the band — the strongest possible information transfer for the candidate.

### Scenario 3 — With competing offer at $215k

**Strong response (no-disclosure version):**

> "Thanks. I do have a competing process in motion, and I am evaluating opportunities based on the role and the comp together. I am not going to share the specific number from the other offer at this stage; what I can say is that I am looking for a competitive package and I am excited about this opportunity. Could we keep the comp conversation for later in the process and focus the rest of this call on the role?"

**Inner-monologue narration:**

> "I have leverage. The temptation is to name the $215k number and use it to anchor high. The problem is that $215k becomes the floor of the negotiation; the recruiter will come back at $216-218k and consider it a close. If I hold the number until the counter call, I can disclose directionally ('meaningfully higher than the offer') and let the recruiter bracket the gap upward. Hold the number."

**Recruiter's likely counter:**

> "Of course. I appreciate you being upfront about the competing process. We will not focus on comp today; let's move forward with the role conversation."

**Why this works:**

The candidate has signaled they have a competing offer (which is true), refused to disclose the specific number (which preserves leverage), and pivoted back to the role conversation. The recruiter knows there is competition; the dollar number stays in the candidate's pocket until the counter call.

## Exercise 2 — Strong response (the counter call)

### Preparation — the candidate's pre-call notes

```text
Calibrated read:
  Triple: L4 SWE at major tech, Seattle, last 24 months, n=87
  25th: $245k, Median: $275k, 75th: $310k, 90th: $350k
Offer: $264.75k year-1 (between 25th and median)
Walk-away: $260k year-1 (slightly below median, given preference for role)
Target: $290k year-1 (close to 75th percentile)
Opening counter:
  Base: $175k (from $165k; +$10k)
  Sign-on: $40k (from $25k; +$15k)
  Stock: $280k over 4 years (from $240k; +$40k, or +$10k/year)
  Total year-1 at counter: $175 + $40 + $70 + $26.25 = $311.25k
Rationale: "The levels.fyi distribution for this triple has a median of $275k and a 75th percentile of $310k. The current offer is at $265k, which is below the median. Based on my evaluation of the role and the market, I am asking for a package that aligns with the upper half of the distribution for this level."
Competing offer frame: "I have a competing offer from another major company at $278k year-1 with a 6-day deadline. I prefer this role; I am asking for the gap to be closed and ideally exceeded so I am not deciding on cost alone."
```

### The counter call — strong-response script

> **You:** "Thank you for the offer and for everything during the loop. I have spent the last week reviewing it carefully and I am very interested in the role and the team. I have a few questions about the role, and then I want to walk through the comp side. Would it work to start with the questions?"
>
> **Recruiter:** "Of course."
>
> [You ask the pre-signing questions from Lecture 3 — on-call, performance review cadence, equity refresh, PTO, remote-work policy. Recruiter answers; you take notes.]
>
> **You:** "Thank you, that is really helpful. Now on the comp side, I want to share where I am coming from. I have done careful research on the comp distribution for L4 software engineers at major tech companies in Seattle over the last 24 months, and the median total comp is around $275k year-1, with the 75th percentile at $310k. The current offer is at $265k, which is below the median. I am also evaluating a competing offer that is at $278k year-1 with a 6-day deadline. I want to be clear that this role is my preference — I am very excited about the team and the project — but the comp is the line I would like to discuss."
>
> **Recruiter:** "I appreciate you walking through that. What are you thinking specifically?"
>
> **You:** "Specifically, I would like to see the base move to $175k, the sign-on move to $40k, and the stock grant move to $280k over four years. That would put the year-1 total at about $311k, which is in line with the 75th percentile for this level and metro and meaningfully closes the gap to the competing offer. I am open to mixing the components differently if there is more headroom on one line than another."
>
> **Recruiter:** "Okay. I want to be honest with you — the base move is going to be hard. The L4 base band tops out around $170k at this location. I can take the rest of the package back to the team and see what is possible. Could you give me 2-3 days?"
>
> **You:** "Of course. Thank you for the candor on the base. Even if base is firm at the band ceiling, I am hopeful there is room on the sign-on and the stock. I look forward to hearing back."

**Why this works:**

- **The candidate asks the pre-signing questions first.** This signals holistic evaluation, not just money-chasing. It softens the comp conversation.
- **The candidate names specific numbers by line item.** Not "I want more" — "$175k base, $40k sign-on, $280k stock." The recruiter can take this back to the team without re-asking.
- **The candidate references the data.** "levels.fyi median is $275k; 75th is $310k" — specific, verifiable, calibrated.
- **The candidate references the competing offer directionally.** $278k year-1 with a 6-day deadline. Specific enough to be useful; not so specific that it caps the upside.
- **The candidate is open to substitution.** "I am open to mixing the components differently if there is more headroom on one line than another" — gives the recruiter flexibility to find the close.
- **The candidate accepts the base-band response with grace.** "Thank you for the candor on the base" — does not push past the band ceiling; pivots to the lines that are not yet locked.
- **The candidate closes the call with a clear next step.** "I look forward to hearing back."

### The likely recruiter follow-up (2-3 days later)

> **Recruiter:** "I went back to the team. The base is at the band ceiling at $170k — we are able to bring it up by $5k. On sign-on, we can move to $35k. On stock, we can move to $260k over four years. The revised year-1 total is approximately $291k. I want to be clear that this is the strongest package the team can put together at this level."

**Strong-response candidate's response:**

> "Thank you for going back to the team. I really appreciate the movement. Let me sit with this overnight and confirm tomorrow. Can you send the revised offer letter so I can review the specifics in writing?"

**Inner-monologue narration:**

> "She moved from $265k to $291k — a $26k bump. That is real movement. The new offer is at the 65th-70th percentile of the distribution, which is solid. The competing offer is at $278k, so this beats it by $13k. The base did not move as much as I asked but the package as a whole did. The right move is to take 24 hours, request the written revised offer, and almost certainly accept tomorrow. Pushing past 'this is the strongest package the team can put together at this level' has a small upside ($2-5k) and a non-trivial downside (the recruiter feels jerked around, or the company withdraws). Take the win, close the deal."

### The close

> **You (next day, by email):** "Thank you again for working through this. I have reviewed the revised offer and I am happy to accept. Please send the updated paperwork for signature, and I will countersign promptly. Looking forward to joining the team."

**Why this works:**

- The 24-hour pause communicated seriousness.
- The acceptance is firm and warm.
- The "looking forward to joining" closes the relationship on a positive note.
- The decline-gracefully email to the competing company goes out within 24 hours of signing.

## Exercise 3 — Strong responses

### Sub-scenario A (honest strong competing offer)

**Strong response:**

> "Thank you for asking. Yes, I do have a competing offer in hand. I want to be straight with you: Company A is my preferred opportunity. The team and the project are a closer match to what I am trying to build over the next few years. The competing offer is at a meaningfully higher total comp — by roughly $30-40k in year 1 — and I want to see if there is room to close the gap on Company A's side. What is the headroom on your side?"

**Recruiter's likely follow-up:**

> "Could you share the offer letter or the specific number so we know what we are comparing against?"

**Strong-response candidate's response:**

> "I am comfortable sharing the structure of the package — the base, sign-on, and stock components — but I would prefer to keep the specific company anonymous unless we get to the close. The breakdown is approximately [base / sign-on / stock from the competing offer]. The year-1 total is in the upper $250s. Does that give you enough to work with on your side?"

**Why this works:**

- The candidate is honest about the gap range without committing to a specific number that caps the upside.
- The decision to keep the company anonymous is reasonable and most recruiters accept it.
- The structural breakdown is enough information for the recruiter to take to the team without giving away the exact closing number.

### Sub-scenario B (verbal-only competing offer)

**Strong response:**

> "I am in the final stages with another company. They have indicated verbally that an offer is likely in the next 5-7 days, but it is not in writing yet. I am leaning toward this role regardless of how that process concludes, but I would like to have both offers in hand before making the final decision. Would it be possible to extend the decision deadline by 5-7 days so I can decide on full information?"

**Recruiter's likely follow-up:**

> "We can probably do a 3-5 day extension. Anything longer might push past our hiring window for this quarter."

**Strong-response candidate's response:**

> "5 days would work. Thank you. If the other process concludes earlier, I will come back to you sooner."

**Why this works:**

- The candidate is honest about the situation without over-stating.
- No specific dollar number is named for the in-process offer.
- The deadline extension request is specific and reasonable.
- The candidate frames their preference, which softens the request.

### Sub-scenario C (no competing offer)

**Strong response:**

> "To be straightforward with you, I do not have another written offer in hand right now. I want to make the comp case on the substantive side — based on the comp data I have looked at for this level and location, the offer is below the market median, and I would like to walk through where there might be room to move."

**Why this works:**

- The candidate does not bluff.
- The pivot to the substantive case is clean.
- The conversation can proceed without leverage, but with credibility intact.

**Recruiter's likely response:**

> "I appreciate the honesty. Walk me through what you have looked at."

[The conversation continues on the data-anchored substantive case from Exercise 2.]

## General notes on the strong responses

A few patterns recur across all the strong responses:

1. **The candidate always thanks the recruiter.** Even when pushing back, even when refusing to disclose, the conversation stays warm. The recruiter is the human messenger; the warmth is both the right thing and tactically useful.
2. **The candidate is specific about what they are asking for.** Not "more"; specific dollar numbers, by line item, with a written rationale.
3. **The candidate is calibrated, not maximal.** The opening counter is above the target but not absurd. The walk-away is honest. The negotiation has shape and limits.
4. **The candidate holds silence when needed.** When the recruiter asks for a number too early, the response is a polite deflection, not a panicked answer. When the recruiter says "best and final," the response is a 24-hour pause, not an immediate yes or push.
5. **The candidate closes cleanly.** Every call ends with a clear next step — when to expect a response, when to follow up, what is the action on each side. No loose ends.

The candidate who internalises these patterns produces strong negotiations even on unfamiliar scenarios. The candidate who memorises the scripts and recites them produces stiff, transactional conversations that recruiters can read in five seconds. Use the scripts to calibrate; do not use them to recite.
