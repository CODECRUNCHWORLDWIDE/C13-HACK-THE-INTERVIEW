# Week 10 — Exercises

Three exercises. Each drills one beat of the offer-and-negotiation conversation, from the first comp question through the substantive counter to the multi-offer frame, with a written response scored against a worked answer key.

1. **[Exercise 1 — The first-move drill](exercise-01-the-first-move-drill.md)** — three role-play scenarios on keeping your number out of your mouth and getting it into the recruiter's instead, written word-for-word and read out loud (~30 min)
2. **[Exercise 2 — The counter-offer drill](exercise-02-the-counter-offer-drill.md)** — one full offer-and-counter scenario: build the calibrated counter by line item with a data-anchored rationale, run the counter call out loud, then self-grade (~45 min)
3. **[Exercise 3 — The multi-offer frame drill](exercise-03-the-multi-offer-frame.md)** — two-to-three sub-scenarios on framing competing offers — strong written offer, in-process verbal offer, and no offer at all — calibrating what to disclose and when (~30 min)

[`SOLUTIONS.md`](SOLUTIONS.md) holds the worked strong-response scripts and answer keys for the drill scenarios. Use it only after you have written and read your own response out loud — the drill is the rehearsal, not the reading.

## How to work them

The order follows the negotiation itself: keep your number off the table (Exercise 1) → make the substantive counter (Exercise 2) → frame the competing-offer leverage (Exercise 3). The two drills together cover the offer-call-and-counter-call arc; the third drill calibrates the single highest-leverage move in the whole conversation, the competing offer.

Every scenario in these exercises has the same shape: read the setup, write your response word for word, read it out loud at least twice, then compare against SOLUTIONS.md. The reading-out-loud is not optional — the skill is verbal delivery under pressure, and a response that reads well on paper often collapses the first time it leaves your mouth. Run each scenario in both a confident frame and a five-seconds-into-a-panic frame; the panic version is where the bad habits live, and the rehearsed-for-panic version is what shows up in the real call.

These are repeat drills, not one-and-done. Run Exercise 1 until your default dodge phrase comes out automatically; run Exercise 2 at least twice before any real counter call so you can run it in 30 minutes without freezing; run Exercise 3 once to calibrate which competing-offer frame matches your actual situation, then again the week a real negotiation is live. The exercises also set up the week's challenges, which drill the two pressure scenarios — the exploding offer and the graceful decline — that need a composure rep and a relational rep rather than a comp-calibration rep.
