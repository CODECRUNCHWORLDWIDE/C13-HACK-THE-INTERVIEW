# Mini-Project — The C13 Capstone

**The full offer-evaluation, negotiation, and decision pipeline. The artefact you carry out of C13.**

This is the capstone for the entire ten-week C13 track. Week 10's lectures, exercises, and homework all feed into this single piece of work. The capstone is the integration of the week and the integration of the track; it is the artefact that, when complete, signals "I am ready for the negotiation conversation when it lands."

## What you are producing

A single deliverable document, 8-15 pages, that walks through a complete offer-evaluation-and-negotiation pipeline from verbal offer to signed acceptance (or declined-with-grace), end to end. The capstone has five required sections:

1. **The scenario setup.** The offers in hand. The candidate's situation. The constraints.
2. **The calibrated read.** levels.fyi distribution, BLS cross-check, cost-of-living adjustment for the relevant metros, walk-away number, target number.
3. **The negotiation narrative.** The full back-and-forth from verbal offer to signed acceptance, written as a script with annotated decision points.
4. **The decision rationale.** Why this offer over the other; what the deciding factors were; what was traded off.
5. **The closing communications.** The acceptance email, the decline-gracefully emails for the not-taken offers, the thank-you to the people who helped you in the loop.

## Scenario options

You may use one of two framings. The track's recommendation is the first if you have a real situation in motion, the second otherwise.

### Option A — Your real situation

If you have a live offer in hand (or one expected in the next 30 days), use it. The capstone becomes the planning document for the actual negotiation. The work product is real; the calibrated read is your real read; the negotiation narrative is the actual conversation you intend to run. The lectures and exercises have prepared you for this; the capstone is the rehearsal.

If you choose Option A, anonymise the document if you intend to share it for peer review — change company names to "Company A," "Company B," "Company C" and adjust dollar amounts by a uniform percentage if you do not want to disclose the specific numbers. The structural work is what matters; the specifics are private.

### Option B — A constructed scenario

If you are not currently in a live offer process, construct a realistic scenario. Use the following template:

**You are an L4 software engineering candidate in [your target metro]. You have three live processes:**

- **Company A** (a major public tech company): Onsite complete; offer expected this week. Recruiter has indicated a "competitive package."
- **Company B** (a mid-size public tech company): Onsite complete; offer in hand at $245k year-1 total comp ($150k base, $25k sign-on, $200k stock over 4 years, 10% target bonus). Decision deadline: 10 business days.
- **Company C** (a Series-C startup): In final-round interviews; offer expected in 10-14 days; recruiter has indicated $260k year-1 total comp plus significant pre-IPO equity upside.

**Your preferences (write your own):** Pick one of the three as your top choice. State why. State which factors (compensation, team, technology, learning, equity upside, location, work-life balance) weigh on the decision, and approximately how much.

**Your calibrated read (use the homework Item 2 output):** Use the calibrated read from your homework. If you have not done the homework, do it before starting the capstone.

## Deliverable structure

The capstone document follows this structure. Section lengths are guidance, not hard limits.

### Section 1 — Scenario setup (1-2 pages)

- The three (or two, or one) offers in play, with full details for each: company, level, location, comp breakdown, decision deadline, vesting schedule, equity type (RSU or option).
- Your context: walk-away number, target number, the qualitative factors you weight heavily, the constraints (geographic, family, visa, financial runway).
- The state of the loops as of the capstone date: which companies have made offers, which are pending, what the timeline looks like.

### Section 2 — The calibrated read (1-2 pages)

- The levels.fyi distribution for each company-level-location triple in play. 25th, 50th, 75th, 90th percentile total comp.
- The BLS metro median cross-check for each metro.
- The cost-of-living adjustment between the two metros (if relevant).
- The per-offer percentile placement: where each offer sits in the distribution for its triple.
- The walk-away and target numbers, derived from the distribution and your preferences.
- The comp-calc spreadsheet (see COMP-SPREADSHEET-TEMPLATE.md) filled in with the three offers side by side, with the four-year-average and year-1-total computations.

You may produce the comp-calc spreadsheet as a separate file (CSV, .xlsx, Google Sheets link) or as a Markdown table in this section. If you use the comp-calculator.py helper, paste the script output here.

### Section 3 — The negotiation narrative (3-5 pages)

This is the heart of the capstone. Write the full negotiation script for the offer you intend to accept (or, if Option B, for Offer B and the parallel decline-Offer-C). The script should cover:

- **The offer call.** What the recruiter says; what you say in response; the deferral move.
- **The deliberation window.** What you do during the 5-7 days between the offer call and the counter call. Include the parallel acceleration of the other processes if relevant.
- **The counter call.** The opening; the pre-signing questions; the substantive counter; the rationale; the competing-offer frame; the recruiter's likely response.
- **The negotiation loop.** 1-3 iterations of back-and-forth. The candidate's response to each escalation phrase. The point at which the candidate accepts.
- **The close.** The acceptance message; the request for the revised written offer; the countersign.

Format each interaction as either dialogue (recommended) or first-person narrative. Include an annotation line every 1-3 exchanges noting the move being made and the underlying rationale. The annotation lines are the layer that distinguishes the capstone from a transcript; they are where you show that you understand what is happening, not just what is being said.

Example format:

> **You (offer call, 2 minutes in):** "Thank you so much. I am really excited about this offer. Could you send the full offer letter to my email? I want to take a few days to review the details carefully before I respond on the substance."
>
> *[Annotation: the deferral move from Lecture 3. The recruiter is expecting a quick yes; the strong move is to defer to the written offer and to the deliberation window. The phrase "respond on the substance" signals that I am taking this seriously, not stalling.]*
>
> **Recruiter:** "Of course. The offer letter will be in your inbox today. When would be a good time for a follow-up call?"
>
> **You:** "Let's plan for Tuesday or Wednesday next week. I will email you to confirm a time once I have read through everything."

### Section 4 — The decision rationale (1-2 pages)

A written argument for the offer you accepted. The structure:

- The three (or two, or one) offers and their pros and cons in the format you found useful (a table is fine).
- The deciding factors, named explicitly. Not "I went with Company A because it was the right fit"; specifically "I went with Company A because the team's focus on [X] is closer to where I want to be in three years, the compensation gap to Company B was closed in the negotiation to $4k year-1, and the cost-of-living advantage in Austin compared to San Francisco offset the remaining gap by an additional $15-20k in purchasing-power terms."
- The trade-offs acknowledged. What you gave up. What you might regret in 12 months. What you might celebrate in 12 months.
- The risk-adjustment for any startup offer: if you took a startup offer over a public-company offer, the explicit probability-weighting of the equity upside.

The decision rationale is the section that future-you (re-reading this in 18 months when you are evaluating the role's first-year experience) will reference most. Write it for future-you.

### Section 5 — Closing communications (1-2 pages)

The full set of emails and messages produced by the negotiation:

- The acceptance email to the chosen company.
- The decline-gracefully email to each not-taken company (one per declined offer).
- The thank-you note to the hiring manager (separate from the recruiter email if you had a strong rapport with the hiring manager).
- The thank-you note to any specific interviewer who made a memorable difference in your loop.
- The thank-you note to any informal advisor — a mentor, a senior colleague, a friend who ran a mock with you — who contributed to the loop's success.

Format each as a complete email (subject line, opening, body, closing, signature). The emails should be send-ready; the capstone is the document you would actually transcribe from if the negotiation landed tomorrow.

## How to start

The recommended sequence:

1. **Hour 1:** Section 1 — write the scenario setup. If using Option A, pull the offer letter and the calibrated read from your homework. If using Option B, construct the scenario.
2. **Hour 2:** Section 2 — fill in the comp-calc spreadsheet. Run comp-calculator.py for each offer. Document the calibrated read.
3. **Hours 3-5:** Section 3 — write the negotiation narrative. This is the longest section. Plan for 2 hours minimum.
4. **Hour 6:** Section 4 — write the decision rationale. Be honest; do not write to impress; write to clarify.
5. **Hour 7:** Section 5 — write the closing communications. The decline emails are the longest sub-section here.
6. **Hour 8 (optional):** Re-read the full capstone end-to-end. Edit for clarity. Mark sections you would change after another draft.

The capstone is approximately 5-8 hours of work, depending on how much of the homework is already in the bank. If the homework is complete, the capstone is 5 hours; if not, it is 8.

## Grading

The capstone has a separate rubric in RUBRIC.md. The rubric is for self-grading; there is no instructor in this asynchronous track. The rubric is calibrated against the strongest possible new-grad-to-L4 capstone, and a 70-80% score is typical for a first-pass capstone done well. The rubric is not a pass-fail; it is a calibration of which sections landed and which need a second pass.

## After the capstone

The capstone is the last work product of C13. After completing it, take 30 minutes for the C13 track retrospective described in the Week 10 README. Re-read the "what done looks like" section of each week and write one sentence on what landed and one sentence on what did not.

The capstone is also the artefact to revisit when a real offer lands. The negotiation narrative you wrote here is the rehearsal; the live conversation is the performance. The two will not match exactly — live conversations diverge from scripts — but the structural muscle is what carries through.

C13 is complete when the capstone is complete. The next track (C7 Crunch Wire, C23 CRUNCH-AGENTS, C1 Code Crunch Convos, or whatever the C13 retrospective points to) is the next move. The interview loop, end to end, is now a learned skill.
