# Exercise 2 — The Counter-Offer Drill

**Time budget:** 45 minutes total. One full scenario (30 minutes of preparation + 10-15 minute role-play), plus a 5-minute self-grade.

**Format:** Solo with a written script, or partnered with a roommate / study partner playing the recruiter.

## Why this drill exists

The first-move drill (Exercise 1) keeps the candidate's number out of the recruiter's notes. The counter-offer drill builds the substantive ask that follows — the specific dollar number, by line item, with the calibrated rationale. The two drills together cover the offer-call-and-counter-call arc.

The counter-offer is where most candidates over- or under-shoot. The over-shoot ("I want $50k more base") destroys credibility; the under-shoot ("I was hoping for maybe $3-5k more if possible") signals the candidate is not serious. The calibrated counter — anchored on data, specific by line item, with a rationale that the recruiter can take back to the team — is the move this drill rehearses.

## The scenario

You are an L4 software engineering candidate. You have an offer from a major public tech company. The offer letter you received reads:

```text
Position:         Software Engineer III (L4 / E4 equivalent)
Location:         Seattle, WA
Start date:       4 weeks from signing (flexible by ±2 weeks)
Compensation:
  Base salary:    $165,000 / year
  Sign-on bonus:  $25,000 (one-time, 12-month full claw-back)
  Stock grant:    $240,000 over 4 years (25/25/25/25, 1-year cliff, monthly post-cliff)
  Target bonus:   15% of base ($24,750)
Year-1 total comp (estimated): $264,750
4-year total comp (estimated): $929,000 (= 4*(165+60+24.75) + 25 sign-on)
4-year average annual: $232,250
Decision deadline: 10 business days from offer letter date
```

Your calibrated read of the levels.fyi distribution for this triple (L4 software engineer at major public tech company, Seattle, last 24 months, n=87 entries after outliers) is:

- 25th percentile total comp: $245k/year
- Median total comp: $275k/year
- 75th percentile total comp: $310k/year
- 90th percentile total comp: $350k/year

The offer's year-1 total comp ($264.75k) is between the 25th and 50th percentile of the distribution. The four-year average ($232k) is at the 25th percentile. The offer is real but not strong; there is headroom.

You have one competing offer in hand:

- Competing offer (Company B): Year-1 total comp $278k; four-year average $260k. Decision deadline: 6 business days.

## Your task

### Step 1 — Preparation (15 minutes)

Write down, on paper or in a doc:

1. **Your walk-away number.** The minimum total comp at which you would say yes to this offer over Company B's. Anchor it on the calibrated 25th percentile and on your own preference between the two roles.
2. **Your target number.** The total comp at which you would say yes without hesitation. Anchor it on the calibrated 50th-75th percentile.
3. **Your opening counter.** The specific dollar numbers, by line item, that you will name in the counter call. Aim slightly above the target — the recruiter will negotiate down, so the opening counter should leave room.
4. **Your rationale.** The two-to-three-sentence reason you will give the recruiter for the counter. The rationale should reference the calibrated data, not just your preference.
5. **Your competing-offer frame.** Whether to mention Company B by name; whether to disclose the specific dollar number; whether to characterise the competing offer as "meaningfully higher" or "in a similar range."

### Step 2 — The counter call (10-15 minutes)

Run the counter call out loud. If you have a partner, brief them on the scenario and have them play the recruiter; the recruiter's role is to push back on each line, ask for evidence, and run one of the four escalation phrases at some point in the call. If you are solo, write the recruiter's lines yourself and run both sides.

The call should cover:

1. **Opening pleasantries (30-60 seconds).** Thank the recruiter; signal you have reviewed the offer carefully.
2. **The substantive ask (60-90 seconds).** Name the counter numbers, by line item. State the rationale.
3. **The competing-offer reference (30-60 seconds).** If using, frame the competing offer at the calibrated level of disclosure.
4. **The recruiter's pushback (varies).** Listen. Take notes. Do not respond immediately.
5. **Your response to the pushback (60-120 seconds).** Pivot to the line items not yet locked. Re-state the rationale if needed. Hold the position if the recruiter pushes harder.
6. **The close of the counter call (30 seconds).** "Thank you for considering this. When can I expect a response?" Schedule the next touchpoint.

### Step 3 — Self-grade (5 minutes)

Open SOLUTIONS.md and compare your counter to the strong-response script. Grade your run on:

- **Specificity.** Did you name specific dollar numbers by line item, or did you ask for "more"?
- **Rationale.** Was the rationale anchored on data (levels.fyi distribution, BLS median, competing offer) or on preference ("I think I am worth more")?
- **Competing-offer handling.** Did you disclose the specific dollar number too early? Did you fail to use the leverage you have?
- **Composure under pushback.** When the recruiter pushed back, did you hold? Did you cave on the first push? Did you escalate confrontationally?
- **Close.** Did you schedule the next touchpoint, or did you leave the call open-ended?

## Variations

After the base run, run one or both variations:

### Variation A — No competing offer

Same scenario as above, but you do not have Company B's offer. Run the counter call without competing-offer leverage. The substantive case (the levels.fyi distribution, the data-anchored rationale) is the entire negotiating position. Observe how much harder the counter call is without the competing offer.

### Variation B — The recruiter says "this is our best and final"

Run the base scenario. After your first counter, the recruiter comes back with a small bump ($5k on sign-on, no other movement) and says "this is our best and final offer." Decide whether to accept or push one more time. Run the response either way. Note: the right answer is sometimes accept and sometimes push; the drill is in producing a calibrated response either way.

## What "done" looks like

By the end of the 45 minutes, you have:

- A written counter-offer with specific dollar numbers, by line item, with a written rationale.
- One full run of the counter call out loud, ideally recorded.
- A self-grade against the SOLUTIONS.md rubric.
- A noted improvement: the one thing you would do differently on the next run.

The drill builds the muscle of asking a specific number out loud with a specific rationale. The candidate who has run this drill twice is the candidate who can run the real counter call in 30 minutes without freezing.
