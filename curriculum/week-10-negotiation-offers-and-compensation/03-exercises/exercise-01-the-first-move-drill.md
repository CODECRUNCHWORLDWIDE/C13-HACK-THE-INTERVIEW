# Exercise 1 — The First-Move Drill

**Time budget:** 30 minutes total. Three role-play scenarios at 8-10 minutes each, plus a 5-minute reflection.

**Format:** Solo (with optional partner). Read each scenario; rehearse the response out loud; write down your response; compare against SOLUTIONS.md.

## Why this drill exists

The single most consequential moment in a negotiation is the moment one party names a number first. The McKenzie principle is that you, the candidate, should not be that party. This drill rehearses the move that keeps the number out of your mouth and gets the number into the recruiter's mouth instead.

Almost every candidate, asked to do this drill the first time, names a number within the first sixty seconds of conversation. The number comes out as a defensive move ("I do not want to seem evasive"), as a research-aware move ("I have looked into this so I will demonstrate that"), or as a flat answer to a flat question ("how much do you want? I want $X"). The strong candidate has a different default. The drill builds the different default.

## Scenario 1 — The recruiter screen, opening minute

You are 50 minutes into the recruiter screen. The conversation has gone well; the recruiter is enthusiastic about your background; they have just transitioned to "let me ask a few logistical questions before we wrap up." The first question lands.

> **Recruiter:** "What kind of compensation are you looking for in your next role?"

The recruiter is asking you to name a number. This is the moment to deflect, dodge, or redirect.

**Your task:** Write down what you would say, word for word, in the next thirty seconds of conversation. Then read it out loud. Then write a second version. Then a third. Pick the version that feels most natural and that does not contain a specific dollar number.

The strong candidate's response will:
- Acknowledge the question without dismissing it.
- Defer the substantive answer to a later stage of the process.
- Pivot the recruiter back to talking about the role.
- Not contain a specific dollar number.

Bonus difficulty: do this exercise once where you are confident, and once where you imagine yourself five seconds into a panic. The panic version is where the bad habits live; the rehearsed-for-panic version is what shows up under real pressure.

## Scenario 2 — The recruiter pushes back

You ran your Scenario 1 response. The recruiter does not let it go.

> **Recruiter:** "I appreciate that, but I really do need a number to know if we are in the same ballpark. We are seeing a lot of strong candidates and I want to make sure we are aligned on comp before we invest more interview time. Can you give me a range?"

The recruiter is escalating. They are using the "candidate funnel scarcity" framing to extract a number. The candidate's job is to either provide a range that does not undercut their position, or to find a deeper dodge that does not provoke the recruiter into withdrawing.

**Your task:** Write down two responses. The first names a range; the second does not. Decide which you would actually use given the recruiter's escalation.

The strong "name a range" response will:
- Name a range, not a single number.
- Anchor the range at or above the calibrated 50th-percentile total comp for the company-level-location triple.
- Frame the range as broad, not narrow ("$200-240k total" not "$215-220k total").
- Pivot back to the substantive ask after stating the range.

The strong "deeper dodge" response will:
- Acknowledge the recruiter's constraint genuinely.
- Reference the lack of complete information at this stage of the process.
- Pivot to the level or band conversation ("could you share the comp band for this level so we are aligning on the same data?").
- Hold without naming a number.

The choice between the two is calibration. If the recruiter feels reasonable and the level is clear, the range is fine. If the recruiter feels aggressive and the level is unclear, the deeper dodge is fine. Either way, the candidate has stayed off the bottom of the band.

## Scenario 3 — The candidate has competing-offer leverage

You are in the recruiter screen for Company A. You have a written offer from Company B at $215k total comp in year 1, expiring in 8 days. The recruiter at Company A asks the standard opening question.

> **Recruiter:** "Before we wrap up, what kind of compensation are you targeting for your next role?"

You now have a strategic choice. You can:

- **Disclose the competing offer's specific number** as a hard anchor. "I have a competing offer at $215k total in year 1 with an 8-day deadline. I am looking for a package that improves on that."
- **Reference the competing offer without disclosing the specific number.** "I have a competing offer in hand at a competitive package. I am evaluating Company A on whether the role and the comp can compete."
- **Continue the dodge from Scenario 1.** "I am looking for a competitive package; I would rather align on the role and the band first and have the substantive comp conversation later."

**Your task:** Decide which of the three you would use. Then write down the response for that choice. Then write down what you would do if the recruiter immediately asked you to share the offer letter to verify.

The strong response on the second choice will:
- Acknowledge the competing offer without disclosing the number.
- Use language like "competitive" or "meaningfully above the market median" rather than a specific dollar number.
- Pivot to the conversation about the role, the team, and the band.
- Hold the leverage as a private fact, not a public anchor.

The most common candidate error here is to disclose the specific number too early. The competing offer's dollar value, disclosed in the recruiter screen, becomes the anchor; the recruiter comes back with $1k more and considers the close done. The same competing offer, withheld until the counter call, produces a larger bump because the recruiter has to bracket the unknown.

## Reflection (5 minutes after the three scenarios)

Write down the answer to:

1. **In which of the three scenarios did you most want to name a specific number?** The scenario where the urge was strongest is the scenario where the bad habit is strongest. Note it.
2. **What is your default dodge phrase?** Write down the exact words you would use under pressure. Memorise them. They should come out automatically the next time a recruiter asks the question for real.
3. **If you ran this drill three times today, what would you do differently on the third run?** The repetition is the point. The first run is exploration; the third run is muscle memory.

## What "done" looks like

By the end of the 30 minutes, you have:

- Three written responses (one per scenario) that you have read out loud at least twice.
- A default dodge phrase memorised — three to five sentences, deliverable in 20 seconds, that you would actually say.
- A noted gap: the scenario where you struggled most. This is the scenario to drill with a partner before the real recruiter call.

The drill is small. The skill it builds is the entire first ninety seconds of the negotiation conversation — which is the ninety seconds that, more than any other ninety seconds in the loop, determines the final number.
