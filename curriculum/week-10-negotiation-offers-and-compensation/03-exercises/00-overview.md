# Week 10 — Exercises

Three negotiation drills. Each rehearses one move in the offer call so that when the real call comes, the move is already in muscle memory instead of being improvised under pressure. Do them out loud — the negotiation is a spoken conversation, and rehearsing silently builds the wrong reflex.

1. **[Exercise 1 — The First-Move Drill](./exercise-01-the-first-move-drill.md)** — three role-play scenarios on getting the recruiter to name a number first, so yours stays out of their notes (~30 min)
2. **[Exercise 2 — The Counter-Offer Drill](./exercise-02-the-counter-offer-drill.md)** — build the calibrated, by-line-item counter against a specific offer and run the "let me see what I can do" loop (~45 min)
3. **[Exercise 3 — The Multi-Offer Frame Drill](./exercise-03-the-multi-offer-frame.md)** — use a competing offer honestly, including the branch where you do not have a written one (~30 min)

The answer key — **[SOLUTIONS.md](./SOLUTIONS.md)** — gives a strong-response script for each scenario, the inner-monologue narration behind the words, the recruiter's likely counter-move, and the response to each. Read it *after* you have written your own response, not before.

## How to work them

Do them in order. Exercise 1 keeps your number out of your mouth; Exercise 2 builds the substantive ask that follows; Exercise 3 adds the highest-leverage move on top. Each sits on the one before it.

Rehearse every response out loud, ideally with a partner playing the recruiter. The simulated pressure of another voice on the line is qualitatively different from reading a script in your head — and the gap between the two is exactly where most negotiations are lost.

Write your response down before you open SOLUTIONS.md. The point of the drill is to find out what *your* default move is, then upgrade it — and you cannot see your own default if you have already read the model answer.
