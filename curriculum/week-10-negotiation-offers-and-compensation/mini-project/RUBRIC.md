# Capstone Rubric — Self-Grading Calibration

This rubric is for self-grading. There is no external grader on this asynchronous track. A first-pass capstone typically scores 70-80% against this rubric; that is the target, not a 100%. The 100% is the calibration ceiling — what the strongest possible new-grad-to-L4 capstone would look like.

Each section is scored on a 0-4 scale per dimension. The dimensions are listed below; total possible score is 80 points across the five sections.

## Section 1 — Scenario setup (max 12 points)

### S1-A: Completeness of offer details (4 points)

- 4: Every offer in play has full details — company, level, location, base, sign-on, stock grant, vesting schedule, target bonus, decision deadline, contingencies.
- 3: One or two minor details missing across the offers.
- 2: One offer is fully detailed; the others are sketches.
- 1: Sketch-level for all offers; missing key fields.
- 0: Missing or incomplete.

### S1-B: Context and constraints (4 points)

- 4: Walk-away number, target number, qualitative weighting, geographic and financial constraints all named explicitly.
- 3: Most are named; one or two are implicit rather than written.
- 2: General sense of the candidate's context but no specific numbers.
- 1: Vague context; no numbers.
- 0: Missing or incomplete.

### S1-C: Timeline state (4 points)

- 4: Clear timeline showing which offers are written, which are pending, and the parallel acceleration plan for the pending processes.
- 3: Timeline is present but missing one element.
- 2: Timeline is implicit in the text but not laid out.
- 1: Vague references to timing.
- 0: Missing or incomplete.

## Section 2 — The calibrated read (max 16 points)

### S2-A: levels.fyi distribution (4 points)

- 4: 25th, 50th, 75th, 90th percentile for each company-level-location triple in play. Outliers flagged. Sample size noted.
- 3: Distribution present for the primary offer; missing for one or two of the others.
- 2: Median only; missing the spread.
- 1: A single anecdotal number, not a distribution.
- 0: Missing.

### S2-B: BLS cross-check (4 points)

- 4: BLS metro median pulled for the relevant metros; cash-to-cash ratio computed; interpretation noted.
- 3: BLS pulled but interpretation is sparse.
- 2: BLS referenced but not actually computed.
- 1: Mentions BLS but does not use it.
- 0: Missing.

### S2-C: Cost-of-living adjustment (4 points)

- 4: NerdWallet and numbeo conversions; state-tax and federal-tax adjustments; qualitative-factor paragraph.
- 3: One conversion source plus tax adjustment.
- 2: Conversion only, no tax adjustment.
- 1: Mentioned but not computed.
- 0: Missing or N/A (single-metro scenario).

### S2-D: Comp-calc spreadsheet (4 points)

- 4: Spreadsheet (or Markdown table) with all offers side by side, year-1, year-2, year-3, year-4, four-year total, four-year average. Includes cliff and back-loading where applicable.
- 3: Spreadsheet is complete but missing one column or row.
- 2: Spreadsheet covers one offer fully; others are sketches.
- 1: Computations present but not laid out as a comparison.
- 0: Missing.

## Section 3 — The negotiation narrative (max 24 points)

### S3-A: The offer call (4 points)

- 4: Full scripted dialogue including the deferral move; recruiter's response captured; annotation explains the move.
- 3: Dialogue is present but annotation is thin.
- 2: Narrative summary of the offer call rather than dialogue.
- 1: One paragraph rather than a scripted interaction.
- 0: Missing.

### S3-B: The deliberation window (4 points)

- 4: 5-7-day plan documented; parallel acceleration of other processes detailed; calibration work explicitly produced.
- 3: Plan is present; one element is missing.
- 2: General sense of "I would take some time."
- 1: Mentioned but not detailed.
- 0: Missing.

### S3-C: The counter call (4 points)

- 4: Full scripted dialogue; pre-signing questions included; substantive counter with specific dollar numbers; rationale anchored on data; competing-offer frame calibrated.
- 3: Dialogue is present but one element (pre-signing questions, rationale, competing-offer frame) is weak.
- 2: Dialogue covers the counter only, no pre-signing questions.
- 1: Narrative summary rather than dialogue.
- 0: Missing.

### S3-D: The negotiation loop (4 points)

- 4: 1-3 iterations of back-and-forth scripted; each of the four escalation phrases (or appropriate subset) is shown with calibrated response; the moment of acceptance is identified explicitly.
- 3: Loop is present but only one iteration.
- 2: The recruiter's first response is shown, no further iteration.
- 1: Loop is summarised, not scripted.
- 0: Missing.

### S3-E: Annotations (4 points)

- 4: Every 1-3 exchanges has an annotation explaining the move and the underlying rationale. Annotations reference the lecture concepts.
- 3: Annotations are present but sparse (every 4-5 exchanges).
- 2: Annotations are present only in the offer call; missing in the counter call.
- 1: Single annotation block at the end rather than inline.
- 0: Missing.

### S3-F: The close (4 points)

- 4: Acceptance is explicit; revised written offer is requested; countersign is documented; thank-you is sent.
- 3: Close is present but one element is missing.
- 2: Acceptance is documented but the closing-out moves are sketches.
- 1: "I would accept" without the procedural close.
- 0: Missing.

## Section 4 — The decision rationale (max 12 points)

### S4-A: Comparison structure (4 points)

- 4: Table or structured comparison showing pros and cons of each offer; deciding factors named explicitly; trade-offs acknowledged.
- 3: Comparison is present but one element is implicit.
- 2: Pros only or cons only, not both.
- 1: Vague preference statement.
- 0: Missing.

### S4-B: Specificity of deciding factors (4 points)

- 4: Deciding factors are named with concrete reasoning. Not "team fit" but "the team's focus on [X] aligns with my goal of [Y]." Not "compensation" but "the gap closed to $4k after negotiation."
- 3: Factors are named; some have concrete reasoning, others are abstract.
- 2: Factors are vague ("team fit," "compensation," "growth potential") without specifics.
- 1: A single factor or "everything together" framing.
- 0: Missing.

### S4-C: Trade-offs acknowledged (4 points)

- 4: Explicit list of what was given up, what might be regretted in 12 months, what might be celebrated. Equity probability-weighting if a startup offer was in play.
- 3: Trade-offs are acknowledged but not enumerated.
- 2: One trade-off mentioned.
- 1: Decision is presented as obvious; no trade-offs named.
- 0: Missing.

## Section 5 — Closing communications (max 16 points)

### S5-A: Acceptance email (4 points)

- 4: Send-ready email; warm; references the recruiter and the team by name; specifies the start date and the countersign next step.
- 3: Email is competent; one or two elements (warmth, specificity, next step) are weak.
- 2: Email is functional but generic.
- 1: One-line "I accept" message.
- 0: Missing.

### S5-B: Decline emails (4 points)

- 4: One decline email per declined offer; each is 100-250 words; specific to the recruiter; names the accepted company; gives the specific reason; leaves the door open; closes warmly.
- 3: Decline emails are present; one is weaker (too short, too generic, missing the door-open phrasing).
- 2: A single template decline email reused for multiple companies.
- 1: A short dismissive message.
- 0: Missing.

### S5-C: Thank-you notes to interviewers (4 points)

- 4: Hiring manager thank-you and at least one specific-interviewer thank-you are drafted. Each is specific to the person and references a concrete moment from the loop.
- 3: One thank-you is drafted; references are present but somewhat generic.
- 2: A single generic thank-you template.
- 1: Mentions sending thank-yous without drafting them.
- 0: Missing.

### S5-D: Acknowledgment of informal advisors (4 points)

- 4: Thank-you note to at least one mentor, senior colleague, or peer who contributed to the loop. Specific and personal.
- 3: Acknowledgment is present but generic.
- 2: Mentioned but not drafted.
- 1: Cursory line.
- 0: Missing.

## Total scoring

Sum the points across all sections. Maximum: 80 points.

- **72-80 (90-100%):** Capstone-quality work. The artefact is send-ready and represents a calibrated, integrated read of the week. The candidate is ready for the real negotiation.
- **60-71 (75-89%):** Solid first-pass capstone. The structural muscle is built; specific sections (typically S3 or S4) would benefit from a second pass.
- **48-59 (60-74%):** Functional but incomplete. Several sections are sketches rather than artefacts. Re-run the homework for the corresponding sections before re-attempting the capstone.
- **Below 48 (<60%):** The capstone is premature. Re-read Lectures 1, 2, and 3; re-do the weak homework items; re-attempt the capstone with the homework as input.

## How to use this rubric

After completing your first-pass capstone:

1. Print or copy this rubric into a separate document.
2. Score each dimension honestly. The temptation is to score generously; the value is in honest calibration.
3. Note the lowest-scoring sections.
4. Re-run those sections only — not the whole capstone — for the second pass.
5. After the second pass, score again. The delta is the calibration of what improved.

The capstone is not graded by a third party. The honesty of your self-grade is the entire signal. The candidate who self-grades a 72 and is honest is better-calibrated than the candidate who self-grades a 80 and is generous.

## What the strongest capstones share

After observing the highest-quality first-pass capstones from C13 candidates:

1. **The negotiation narrative is the longest section and the most carefully written.** It is where the week's lectures most directly transfer; it is the section where the work shows.
2. **The annotations reference specific lecture concepts.** "This is the deferral move from Lecture 3" rather than "this is a strong move." The specific reference signals integration.
3. **The decision rationale acknowledges what was given up.** The strongest capstones explicitly name what was traded off — not just what was gained. The strong negotiator is the negotiator who can articulate the cost of the choice.
4. **The decline emails are warm.** The candidate who treats the not-taken recruiter with the same warmth as the taken recruiter is the candidate who has internalised the relational layer of the negotiation.
5. **The closing communications acknowledge the informal network.** The capstone that includes a thank-you to a mock-interview partner, a study buddy, or a senior mentor is the capstone written by the candidate who understands that the loop is a team sport even when the conversation is solo.

These are the dimensions on which to push the second pass.
