# Resources — Week 10: Offers, Negotiation, and Compensation Literacy

All resources below are free. Where a paid edition exists (a book, a paid newsletter, a consulting product), the free alternative is noted and is sufficient for the week.

## Primary corpora

### Patrick McKenzie — "Salary Negotiation: Make More Money, Be More Valued" (2012, free)

- **Link:** https://www.kalzumeus.com/2012/01/23/salary-negotiation/
- **Length:** ~14,000 words; read time ~90 minutes
- **What it is:** The canonical free essay on salary negotiation in tech. Written in 2012; the framing has aged better than almost any other career-advice essay of that era. The specific moves — "make them name a number first," "negotiate the equity grant, not just the base," "the salary range you give them defines the salary range you get back" — are the framing the rest of this week sits on top of.
- **How to use it:** Read it end to end before Tuesday. Annotate. Note the three phrases you would not have said on your own. Re-read once more on Saturday before the capstone.

### levels.fyi — the free compensation database

- **Link:** https://www.levels.fyi
- **Data dump (also free):** https://www.levels.fyi/data
- **What it is:** A crowdsourced compensation database covering most of the major tech companies, broken down by level, location, year, and total comp components (base, sign-on, stock, bonus). The site is the de facto reading room for comp in tech.
- **How to use it:** Lecture 2 walks the full read. The short version: filter by company + level + location + last 24 months; look at the distribution, not the top number; cross-check the median against BLS.

### U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics — Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics (OEWS)

- **Link:** https://www.bls.gov/oes/
- **Direct query (Software developers, applications, SOC 15-1252):** https://www.bls.gov/oes/current/oes151252.htm
- **What it is:** Federally collected wage data for software developers across every U.S. metro area. The data is collected from employers (not from employees) and is the labour-market floor for the occupation. Updated annually.
- **How to use it:** Pull the median and 25th/75th percentile wage for your target metro. Compare to the levels.fyi median for the same metro. The ratio is the "tech-company premium over the broader labour market" for your geography.

### NerdWallet — Cost of Living Calculator (free)

- **Link:** https://www.nerdwallet.com/cost-of-living-calculator/
- **What it is:** A free cost-of-living conversion tool that takes a source metro, a destination metro, and a salary, and returns the equivalent salary that preserves purchasing power.
- **How to use it:** Quick sanity check on the cost-of-living delta between two offers in different cities. Cross-check against numbeo.

### Numbeo — Cost of Living Index (free, crowdsourced)

- **Link:** https://www.numbeo.com/cost-of-living/
- **What it is:** A crowdsourced cost-of-living index covering most cities worldwide. The data is noisier than the BLS and NerdWallet sources but covers a wider geographic spread.
- **How to use it:** Pair with NerdWallet for the U.S. metros. Use as primary for international moves.

### FTC Non-Compete Rule (2024, partially enjoined)

- **Link:** https://www.ftc.gov/legal-library/browse/rules/non-compete-rule
- **What it is:** The Federal Trade Commission's 2024 final rule banning most non-compete clauses in U.S. employment contracts. The rule was partially enjoined by a Texas federal court in August 2024 and has been the subject of ongoing appeals as of 2026. The rule remains the directional signal: non-competes are increasingly disfavoured even where still technically enforceable.
- **How to use it:** Read Section II (the scope of the rule) and Section IV (the exceptions). The new-grad in California should know non-competes have been unenforceable there since 1872. The new-grad in Florida or Texas should read the rule with more care.

## Secondary reading

### Haseeb Qureshi — "Ten Rules for Negotiating a Job Offer" (free, 2016)

- **Link:** https://haseebq.com/my-ten-rules-for-negotiating-a-job-offer/
- **Length:** ~10,000 words; read time ~60 minutes
- **What it is:** The second-most-cited free essay on tech negotiation. Written by someone who went from bootcamp to $250k offers in 8 months; the framing complements McKenzie's. Where McKenzie is procedural, Qureshi is tactical.
- **How to use it:** Read between McKenzie and Lecture 3. The two essays disagree on a few specifics — McKenzie is more cautious on competing offers, Qureshi is more aggressive — and the disagreement is the productive tension Week 10 is built on.

### Holloway — "Holloway Guide to Technical Recruiting and Hiring" (free chapters)

- **Link:** https://www.holloway.com/g/technical-recruiting-hiring (some chapters free)
- **What it is:** A free-tier guide on the recruiter's side of the conversation. Useful for the candidate because it surfaces the recruiter's incentives, the recruiter's hiring quota, and the recruiter's manager's hiring quota — none of which the candidate sees by default.
- **How to use it:** Skim the "compensation" and "offer" chapters. The recruiter is not the candidate's adversary; the recruiter is a person with a quota, a manager, and a deadline. Internalising this changes the tone of the conversation.

### Yossi Kreinin — "Don't Ask Forgiveness, Radiate Intent" (free, 2017)

- **Link:** https://yosefk.com/blog/dont-ask-forgiveness-radiate-intent.html
- **What it is:** A short essay on a related communication move — telegraphing what you are about to do, rather than apologising for what you just did. The framing transfers directly to the negotiation conversation: "I am going to push on the base before we discuss equity" is a stronger opening than "sorry to push back, but...".
- **How to use it:** Read once. Note the linguistic move. Use it in Exercise 2.

### Internet Archive — sample offer letters and term-sheet templates (free)

- **Link:** https://archive.org/details/texts (search "tech offer letter sample")
- **What it is:** A scattered archive of public sample offer letters and term-sheet templates from various sources. Quality varies; the value is in seeing the structural shape of an offer letter before your own arrives.
- **How to use it:** Skim two or three. Note the recurring sections: base, sign-on, equity, vesting, at-will, non-solicit, arbitration, deadline. The structural recurrence is the signal; your offer will look broadly similar.

## Free legal resources

### Nolo — Employment law primer (free articles)

- **Link:** https://www.nolo.com/legal-encyclopedia/employment-law
- **What it is:** Free legal-encyclopedia articles on at-will employment, non-compete enforceability by state, non-solicit clauses, assignment of inventions, and the basics of employment contracts.
- **How to use it:** Reference. Read the article that matches the clause you do not understand on your offer.

### LegalZoom — Contract review (free overview; paid lawyer referral)

- **Link:** https://www.legalzoom.com (free overview pages)
- **What it is:** A free overview of contract review. The paid product is a lawyer-of-record service; the free pages cover the basics.
- **How to use it:** Pre-read before deciding whether to hire a contracts lawyer for the offer review. For most new-grad offers at standard tech companies, a lawyer review is not necessary; the clauses are boilerplate. For startup offers with unusual equity structures, a $400 one-hour lawyer review is high ROI.

### State bar association lawyer-referral services (free first consult, varies by state)

- **California:** https://www.calbar.ca.gov/Public/Need-Legal-Help/Lawyer-Referral-Service
- **New York:** https://www.nysba.org/lawyer-referral-service/
- **Texas:** https://www.texasbar.com/find-a-lawyer
- **Florida:** https://www.floridabar.org/public/lrs/
- **What it is:** Most state bar associations run a lawyer-referral service that offers a free or low-cost first consultation. Useful if the offer has unusual clauses and a paid lawyer feels disproportionate.
- **How to use it:** If the offer has a non-compete you cannot interpret, a clawback you do not understand, or an equity structure that does not match the standard schedules above — use the free first consult before signing.

## Calibration: what good looks like in practice

The strong new-grad-to-L4 candidate, after Week 10, can:

1. **Quote McKenzie's three core moves from memory.** ("Make them name a number first." "Negotiate everything, not just base." "The salary you get is the salary you ask for.")
2. **Read a vesting schedule and compute the four-year total in under three minutes.** Including the cliff math, the year-1 sign-on offset, and the back-loaded Amazon schedule if applicable.
3. **Filter levels.fyi to a company-level-location triple and read the distribution.** Knows the median, the spread, and which entries are crowd-flagged.
4. **Hold a 45-minute negotiation phone call.** Can wait through silence; can ask the recruiter to name their first move; can push on the range without quoting a number unsupported by data.
5. **Decline an offer in writing without burning the bridge.** Has at least one decline email in their drafts folder from the homework.

If any of those are missing, the week's work is not yet done.

## What this week deliberately does not cover

- **Equity at private pre-IPO companies in depth.** The week covers the basics — strike price, 409A valuation, exercise vs vest tax treatment. The full depth (early-exercise, ISO vs NSO, the AMT trap, the 83(b) election) is a one-week course of its own and is in the C13 senior-track companion week.
- **Founder-level negotiation.** The week assumes the candidate is the candidate, not the founding engineer being recruited with a 2% equity stake and a board seat. The founder-level conversation has different dynamics.
- **International tax structuring.** The week is U.S.-focused. Candidates negotiating a U.K., E.U., or Canadian offer should run the week's framework and substitute the local tax tables (HMRC for the U.K., the CRA for Canada). The negotiation moves transfer; the tax math does not.
- **The pre-signing background check.** Most tech offers are contingent on a background check. The check is not negotiable; it is a yes/no gate. If you have something on your record that may surface, raise it with the recruiter before signing, not after.
- **Salary negotiation at a company that has explicitly stated "we do not negotiate."** A small number of companies (most famously Triplebyte during its operational years, certain government-adjacent roles, some unionised positions) genuinely do not negotiate. The negotiation moves in this week do not apply there; the question becomes "is the published comp acceptable" rather than "what can I push on." Recognise this branch when it shows up.
