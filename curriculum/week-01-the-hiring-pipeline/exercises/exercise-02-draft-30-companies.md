# Exercise 2 — Draft 30 companies

**Time:** ~90 minutes. **Output:** Your tracking spreadsheet populated with 30 real, researched companies.

## Goal

Move your spreadsheet from 5 example rows to **30 real rows** with the right tier balance:

| Tier | Count | Notes |
|------|------:|-------|
| A — Reach | 10 | Your dream/stretch companies |
| B — Match | 15 | Strong fit, plausible offer |
| C — Safety | 5 | Real options you'd accept |

If you can't find 30 companies you'd actually accept an offer from, **expand your search criteria** (geography, role type, company stage) until you can. 30 is the floor.

## Process

### Step 1 — Brainstorm (20 min)

Write down every company that comes to mind in 20 minutes. Don't filter yet. Aim for 60-80 names. Sources to prime the pump:

- Your current company's competitors and customers
- Companies whose products you actually use daily
- Companies whose engineering blogs you've read
- The "Who's Hiring?" thread from the last month: <https://news.ycombinator.com/submitted?id=whoishiring>
- Wellfound's industry filters
- The latest Y Combinator batch (`https://ycombinator.com/companies?batch=W2026`)
- The Cloud Native Computing Foundation member list
- Crunchbase top funding rounds

### Step 2 — Filter (20 min)

For each name on your list, ask:

1. Would I genuinely take an offer from them at fair comp? If no, drop.
2. Are they hiring my level / role within the next 3-6 months? (Check careers page or LinkedIn.) If no, drop.
3. Does the geography work? (On-site / hybrid / remote requirements.) If no, drop.

You should now have 30-50 names.

### Step 3 — Tier (30 min)

For each survivor, assign A / B / C honestly:

- **A — Reach.** Higher comp band than your "good outcome." Selective. You'd be excited.
- **B — Match.** Where you'd most likely land at fair comp. The bulk of your offers will come from here.
- **C — Safety.** You'd accept; you'd be okay; you might even thrive — but it's not your top choice.

If you have too many A's, you don't have enough Matches. If you have too few A's, you're under-aiming.

If you can't decide, downgrade: when in doubt, B beats A; C beats B.

### Step 4 — Research the role (20 min)

For each entry, fill in:

- A real role at that company (look at their careers page).
- A researched comp band (levels.fyi or BLS).
- Whether you have a 1st-degree contact (check LinkedIn).
- The source (how you'd plan to make first contact: referral / recruiter / cold).

## Acceptance criteria

- [ ] Spreadsheet has exactly 30 rows.
- [ ] Tier distribution is 10/15/5.
- [ ] Every row has Tier, First-degree-contact, Source, Role, Comp-band populated.
- [ ] Every Tier-A company has a defensible reason for being there in the Notes column.
- [ ] At least 8 of the 30 have a 1st-degree LinkedIn contact you identified.

## Common mistakes

- **All Tier-A.** "Everywhere is my dream job." False; do the work to honestly tier.
- **All Tier-C.** "I just want any job." Aim higher. You're undercutting your own outcome.
- **No comp research.** "I'll figure out the comp later." Then you'll get lowballed. Spend the 5 minutes per company on levels.fyi.
- **Geography mismatch.** "I'll figure out moving later." Then you waste cycles on offers you can't take.

## Stretch

- For each Tier-A: write down 2-3 specific things about that company that excite you, beyond comp. (You'll need these for the "why this company" conversation.)
- For each Tier-C: write down what would make you decline. (Useful negotiation context.)
- Identify the recruiter or hiring manager for the role at 5 companies on the list.

## Submission

Commit a `companies-summary.md` to your portfolio repo with:

- The Tier-A / Tier-B / Tier-C company names and roles (no comp data needed publicly).
- One paragraph reflection on what surprised you about your own tiering.
