# Lecture 2 — Asking the Right Questions Back

> **Duration:** ~1.5 hours. **Outcome:** You can ask a hiring manager 5-8 substantive questions that you could not ask the recruiter, that you could not Google, and that reliably surface real signal about the team, the role, and the manager themselves. You also know which manager-side answers should give you pause.

## 1. The closing 10-15 minutes is scored

The hiring manager screen is roughly 30-45 minutes. The first 20-25 minutes is the recent-project anchor plus its drill-downs and the team-fit probes (Lecture 1). The remaining 10-15 minutes is **your turn**.

Most candidates underweight this segment. They prepared the recent-project story, they rehearsed the conflict answer, they thought about the team-fit probes — and then when the HM says "I'll leave the rest of the time for your questions," they ask one safe question and run out of material.

This is a missed opportunity, but it is also a scoring miss. The HM is taking notes in this segment too. The structure of your questions tells them, in roughly equal weight to the project story, who you are. Specifically: how you think about teams, what you value in a workplace, whether you've thought seriously about *this* role beyond "would I take it," and whether you'd be the kind of teammate who pushes the team to be better or who passively rides along.

The candidates who walk out of HM screens with the highest scores almost always **asked the questions that surprised the HM**. Not weird questions. Specific, substantive questions that the HM hadn't been asked by the previous five candidates that week.

## 2. The reverse-interview principle

Reframe what's happening: the HM screen is the first call in the pipeline where *you are also evaluating them*. The recruiter screen wasn't this, because the recruiter isn't the person you'd work with. The HM is. If you accept this offer, you will spend 8-12 hours a week with this person for the next two years (weekly 1:1s, design reviews, every escalation). They are the single highest-leverage factor in whether the next two years of your career are good or bad.

The reverse interview is not adversarial. It is a parallel evaluation running in the same time window as the HM's evaluation of you. Both sides are taking signal. The HM expects this; the strong HMs welcome it.

### What you are evaluating

Five things, mirroring what the HM is evaluating about you:

1. **Technical credibility on the team's work.** Can the HM describe the team's current technical challenges with depth, or do they hand-wave? Hand-waving here is a flag — either the HM doesn't know the work, or they're not technical enough to evaluate yours.
2. **Their management style.** How do they make decisions, give feedback, run 1:1s, handle disagreement? You will live with their answer.
3. **Team health.** Tenure of the team, recent attrition, on-call structure, the manager's read of strengths and weaknesses. An honest manager will name a real weakness when asked.
4. **The role's actual shape.** What the JD says vs. what the role actually is. These diverge more often than candidates expect. The HM is your best source on the gap.
5. **Red flags.** The signals — verbal, behavioural, structural — that this role is not what it seems, that the team is in trouble, or that the manager is misaligned with what you want.

## 3. The question categories — a 12-question bank

The question bank for hiring managers organises around five categories. Build 2-3 questions per category for a working set of 12. Three of those are your reusable defaults — the questions you ask on every HM screen unless they've already been answered. The rest are situational.

### Category 1 — The team and its work

These questions establish what the team actually does, day to day. They reveal the HM's grasp on their own team's work.

- "Can you walk me through what the team has shipped in the last 6 months, and what's on the roadmap for the next 6?"
- "Where does this team sit in the engineering org — what's upstream, what's downstream, and what's the most common cross-team dependency?"
- "What's a recent technical decision the team made that you found particularly interesting?" (Tests whether they're paying attention to technical depth, not just delivery.)
- "How does the team make technical decisions — RFC, async write-up, room discussion, manager call? What's the actual process?" (The phrasing matters; "what's the actual process" cues honesty.)
- "What's the current source of pages or on-call pain on this team?" (Honest answers here are gold; vague answers are a yellow flag.)

### Category 2 — The role itself

These questions probe what the role actually is, versus the JD.

- "If I joined and was outstanding at this role, what would that look like at 6 months? At 12?"
- "What's the gap on the team that this role specifically fills — what hasn't been getting done that this role will?"
- "What does ramp look like for this role — what does a strong first 90 days look like, in your experience hiring into this team?"
- "Is there a specific project or surface area you'd want this role to own within the first 6 months?"
- "What's the failure case — what does someone who *doesn't* work out in this role typically struggle with?"

### Category 3 — The manager themselves

These questions are about the HM's management style. They are the most-revealing category and the one most candidates skip.

- "How do you run 1:1s? Weekly, biweekly? What's a typical agenda?"
- "What's your style on feedback — synchronous, written, frequency?"
- "How do you handle a situation where you and a senior IC disagree about a technical direction?"
- "Can you tell me about a recent decision you made as a manager that you'd make differently in hindsight?" (The HM-equivalent of "what's your biggest weakness." Their willingness to answer it is itself signal.)
- "What's your read on the gap between {this team's stated mission} and what the team is actually doing day-to-day?"

### Category 4 — Team health and growth

These questions probe whether the team is somewhere you want to be for the next 2-3 years.

- "What's the team's tenure look like — how long have most people been on the team?" (Short tenure is sometimes fine, but a question worth asking.)
- "Who was the last person to leave the team, and (if you can share) what was the reason?" (Don't pry; do listen.)
- "What's the career-growth path on this team? What does promotion look like from {level you're interviewing for} to {one level up}?"
- "Where does the team most disagree with the broader engineering org's direction?"
- "What's something you've changed about how the team works in the last year?"

### Category 5 — The closing question

One question, asked last, that gives the HM the cleanest read on you and an opportunity to give you the cleanest read on them.

- "Based on this conversation, what's the strongest case for me in this role? What's the most likely concern someone on the loop will raise about me, and how should I think about preparing for it?"

This is a high-trust question. It works because it asks the HM to commit a read in front of you. Most HMs will answer honestly because the question itself signals seriousness. Their answer tells you what to emphasise in the rest of the loop and what gap (if any) the loop will probe.

A weaker version of this same question, if you don't want to put the HM on the spot: "What would you want me to know going into the next round that we haven't covered today?"

## 4. The three reusable defaults

Across the 12 questions, mark three as your reusable defaults — the questions you ask every HM screen unless the answer was already given earlier in the call.

The strongest reusable trio:

1. **From Category 1:** "How does the team make technical decisions — what's the actual process?" (Reveals decision culture, which is the single highest-leverage team-health variable.)
2. **From Category 2:** "If I joined and was outstanding at this role, what would that look like at 6 and 12 months?" (Reveals whether the HM has thought concretely about what success looks like; surfaces hidden expectations.)
3. **From Category 5:** "What's the strongest case for me, and the most likely concern, going into the loop?" (Reveals the read, gives you intel for the rest of the rounds.)

These three cover three different surfaces — team, role, you — and are hard for an HM to answer with a script. The vagueness of the answer to any of them is itself signal.

## 5. Reading the HM's answers — what each pattern means

You ask the questions. The HM answers. The *quality* of their answers is the data.

### Strong answer patterns

- **Specificity.** They name actual projects, actual decisions, actual people (anonymised), actual numbers. "The team shipped the consent-management rewrite in Q3 — it was a 4-month project across 3 engineers, and the hardest part was the migration of legacy consents."
- **Self-criticism.** They name the team's or their own weaknesses unprompted, with context. "Honestly, our on-call rotation is currently uneven — two engineers carry 70% of pages and we're working on rebalancing in Q1."
- **Forward-looking honesty.** They distinguish what they're proud of from what they're worried about. "The team's biggest risk right now is that we don't have enough senior IC depth, which is partly why this role is open."
- **Curiosity back.** They turn your questions into a conversation — asking what you'd do, what you've seen in similar situations, how your prior team handled a comparable case. This is the strongest signal an HM gives that they want to hire you specifically.

### Yellow flag patterns

- **Generic answers to specific questions.** You asked "what's a recent technical decision the team made?" They said "we're always making decisions." Either they're not engaged or they don't know.
- **Defensive answers.** You asked "who was the last person to leave?" They got visibly stiff and said "people leave for all kinds of reasons." Possible but worth a follow-up later.
- **All-positive answers.** Every question gets a frictionless, positive answer. No team is uniformly healthy; an HM who can't name a real weakness is either not introspecting or not telling you the truth.
- **The "we don't really do that here" pattern.** You asked about 1:1s; they said they don't really do them on a regular cadence. About decision processes; they said it's "more organic." About feedback; "case by case." Could be a flat structure; could be a manager who's hands-off in ways that won't serve you.

### Red flag patterns

- **They don't know who the team is.** "How many engineers on the team?" → vague answer. The HM is either new or running multiple teams thinly.
- **They badmouth a recent departure.** "{Person} left because they couldn't handle the pace." The HM just told you how they'll describe you if you leave.
- **They badmouth other teams at the company.** "Marketing always changes the requirements" / "the platform team is impossible to work with." Tells you the cross-team relationships are bad and the HM is willing to share that with strangers.
- **They speak about the role differently from the JD.** The JD says senior IC ownership; the HM describes the role as "supporting" the staff engineer. Job description vs. real role gap. This is the single most-common red flag in HM screens and worth its own follow-up.
- **They can't answer the question about success at 6 / 12 months.** This is rare but very loud. It means the role has been opened without a clear definition of what success looks like.

None of these are automatic disqualifiers. They are signals to gather more data — at the technical screen, with current team members in the cross-functional round, in the offer-stage conversations. Note them; don't react to them in the moment.

## 6. What *not* to ask the hiring manager

A few patterns to avoid in this beat:

- **Comp questions.** The HM does not handle comp. Asking here puts them in an awkward position and signals you didn't track who handles what. (The one exception: if the HM brings up comp first, engage briefly and redirect to the recruiter.)
- **Questions answerable from the company website.** "What does the company do?" "What's the product?" Reads as no prep.
- **Questions answerable by the recruiter that you should have asked them.** "How many rounds are in the interview process?" "What's the timeline?" You should know this. If you don't, ask the recruiter after the HM screen, not the HM.
- **Hyper-specific technical questions about systems you couldn't be expected to know about.** "What's your CI/CD pipeline configuration like?" Save these for the technical screen with an engineer.
- **Negotiation moves.** Anything that sounds like positioning for the offer ("if I had a competing offer, how would that affect ramp expectations?") is way too early. The HM is not in offer-negotiation mode at this stage.

## 7. The pacing — when to ask each question

The HM will say something like "I'll leave the last 10-15 minutes for your questions" or "any questions for me?" When that happens, you have a working budget:

- **Question 1 — the substantive opener.** Pick a Category 1 or Category 2 question. Listen to the answer; ask one follow-up. 3-4 minutes total.
- **Question 2 — the manager-style probe.** Pick a Category 3 question. The answer here is often the most-revealing of the call. 3-4 minutes.
- **Question 3 — the role-specific probe.** Pick a Category 2 question if you didn't use one for Q1, or a Category 4 question. 2-3 minutes.
- **Question 4 — the closing question.** The Category 5 question, or a softer variant. 2-3 minutes including their answer.

That's roughly 12 minutes for 4 questions. If you have more time, add a Category 4 question (team health) or a follow-up to the Category 3 manager-style answer. If you have less time, cut Question 3 first.

**Don't ask all 12 prepared questions.** Picking the right 4-5 in the moment, based on what's already come up in the call, is the test. Asking all 12 reads as a checklist.

## 8. The follow-up question — the thing that doubles the signal

When the HM answers, do not move directly to your next prepared question. Ask one follow-up first.

The follow-up structure:

- "That's interesting — can you say more about {specific phrase they used}?"
- "What's an example of {generality they mentioned}?"
- "How has that {process / pattern / decision} changed in the last year?"

The follow-up forces specificity. It also signals that you were actually listening, not just waiting for them to finish so you could ask the next prepared question. The HM notices.

A 4-question segment with one good follow-up per question gets more signal than a 6-question segment with no follow-ups.

## 9. The closing — when the HM is ready to wrap

The HM will signal the end of the call: "Well, we're coming up on time, anything else?" or "I think we have a couple of minutes left."

Three things to do in the last 60 seconds:

1. **Save one specific question for the end.** Something short, asked-and-answered in under a minute. A Category 4 question like "what's a recent change to how the team works?" is a good closer.
2. **Confirm next steps.** "What does the next round typically look like for this loop? Should I expect to hear from {recruiter name} or directly from you?"
3. **Brief thanks with one specific.** "Thanks for the time today — the {specific thing they said} was particularly useful for me to hear." Not a generic "thanks for your time"; one specific acknowledgement.

The brief thanks is the on-call analogue of the follow-up email's specific reference. It primes them to remember you.

## 10. A worked closing-segment script

What this looks like in practice. The HM has just finished probing your conflict story.

**HM:** "Great. We have about 12 minutes left — what questions do you have for me?"

**You:** "I want to ask three things. First, you mentioned earlier the team is moving toward {topic from earlier}. How does the team make technical decisions in practice — is there a written RFC process, an async-doc culture, or is it more in-the-room? I'm asking partly because the project I described earlier worked best when we had a written design before implementation, and I'm curious how that maps to how this team operates."

**HM** (4-minute answer, describes the team's actual process, mentions one area where they're trying to formalise decisions but haven't yet).

**You:** "Got it. Can you give me an example of a recent decision where the process worked — and one where it didn't?"

**HM** (2-minute answer with one positive and one negative example).

**You:** "That's useful, thanks. Second question — what does outstanding look like for this role at 6 and 12 months? I want to make sure I have a concrete picture of what you'd want from someone in this seat."

**HM** (3-minute answer with specific examples).

**You:** "Last one, and a more direct one — based on this conversation, what's the strongest case for me in this role, and what's the most likely concern someone on the loop will raise? I'd rather know going in than guess."

**HM** (2-minute answer; this is the read).

**You:** "That's helpful. Thanks for being direct. What does the next round look like — is it the technical screen, or is there another conversation in between?"

**HM:** "Technical screen next, with one of our senior ICs. {Recruiter} will reach out with scheduling."

**You:** "Perfect. Thanks again for the time — the part about {specific phrase from their answer} is something I want to think about more. I'll look out for the scheduling email."

What just happened:

- Four questions across three categories, well-paced.
- One follow-up after the first question, doubling its signal value.
- A Category 5 closing question that surfaced the HM's actual read of you.
- A specific acknowledgement at the end that wasn't generic.

Total time: roughly 12 minutes. The HM walks away with the impression of a candidate who was actively engaged, asked specific questions, and surfaced the loop's likely friction without being defensive. That's a strong write-up.

## 11. Asking questions that you actually have

A common failure mode: candidates ask questions from the bank that they don't actually care about the answer to, in order to look like they're asking questions.

The HM can tell. Specifically, when you ask a question you don't care about, you:

- Don't follow up on the answer.
- Don't take notes when they answer.
- Don't reference the answer later in the call or in the follow-up email.

The fix is simple: from the 12-question bank, ask the questions whose answers you actually want to know. If you only have two questions you genuinely care about, ask two well, with good follow-ups, rather than four poorly.

The corollary: when you're building the question bank in Exercise 2, draft questions that *you* are actually curious about. The exercise is partly about formalising your own curiosity about a workplace.

## 12. The "I have no questions" trap and how to escape it

Sometimes, by the end of the HM screen, the HM has organically answered every question you'd prepared. The temptation is to say "I don't have any questions — you covered everything."

Don't. Even if mostly true. Two moves work:

- **The compliment-and-probe.** "You actually covered most of what I'd prepared, which is helpful — but I want to follow up on one thing you said: {specific phrase from earlier}. Could you say more about that?"
- **The depth-shift.** "Most of my prepared questions were answered. Let me ask a different one — {ask the Category 5 closing question, since it doesn't rely on having prepared a specific topic}."

Both work because they shift the question to one that draws on the conversation itself rather than a prepared list. They also keep you in the active-engagement frame.

## 13. After the call — what to do with the answers

The HM gave you answers. Now you have a corpus. Within an hour of the call (while it's fresh):

1. **Write down the answers** to the 3-4 questions you asked, in 1-2 sentences each. Not verbatim; the gist.
2. **Score each answer** against the patterns from §5. Strong / yellow flag / red flag. Most calls will have a mix.
3. **List the questions that came up that you didn't ask but want answered later.** Save these for the technical screen, the cross-functional round, or the offer-stage conversations.
4. **Decide your one-sentence read on the manager.** "{HM} is {one-line characterisation based on this call}." This is the read you carry into the rest of the loop and back to your decision-making at offer time.

If your read is strongly negative — three or more red flags — note it but don't decide based on a 40-minute call alone. The technical screen and the cross-functional round will give you more data. If your read is strongly positive, also useful: it shapes how much energy to put into the rest of the loop.

## 14. Self-check

- Why is the closing 10-15 minutes of the HM screen scored as heavily as the opening?
- The 12-question bank organises into five categories. Name them.
- Of the three reusable defaults, what does each one probe for that the others don't?
- A hiring manager gives you a generic, positive-only answer to every question. What's the pattern, and what does it mean?
- You ask "how does the team make technical decisions?" The HM says "more organic, case-by-case." Yellow flag or fine? What's the follow-up?
- You've used all your prepared questions and the HM organically answered everything. What do you ask, and why?
- The Category 5 closing question puts the HM on the spot. Why does it work despite that?
