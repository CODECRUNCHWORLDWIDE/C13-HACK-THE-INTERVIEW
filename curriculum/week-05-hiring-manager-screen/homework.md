## Problem 2 — The four-stress-question deep prep (60 min)

If you didn't do Challenge 1, do it now and use the same time. If you did, this is the *deep* version — same four questions, more rigorous structure.

In `c13-week-05/stress-questions-deep.md`, for each of the four classic stress questions (conflict, weakness, why-you-over-others, first-90-days):

- **Draft a primary answer** to the rubric in Lecture 1 §10. Aim for 60-90 seconds spoken (~150-225 words).
- **Draft a backup answer** using a different example. Some HMs ask "and a different example?" after the first answer. You should have a second story ready for each of the four.
- **Note the structural rubric inline** for each (e.g., for conflict: situation / substance / process / lesson).

**Acceptance:**

- All four questions have primary + backup answers drafted.
- Each answer is 150-225 words.
- Each contains at least one specific named example (a person, a project, a JD line, a specific component).
- Zero banned phrases.
- A note on which of the four you find hardest, and why.

## Problem 3 — Manager-style probe research (45 min)

Pick the **one** Tier-A target where you most expect an HM screen in the next 4 weeks. Research the hiring manager.

In `c13-week-05/hm-research-{company}.md`:

**Part A — Identify the HM (10 min).** From the JD, the recruiter's notes, LinkedIn, or company about pages, identify the most-likely hiring manager. Usually it's the person whose team has the open role. Note: name, title, tenure at the company, prior companies.

**Part B — Public output (15 min).** Look for any public output from this person: talks, blog posts, GitHub, Twitter/X, podcast appearances, LinkedIn posts. List anything you find. If you find nothing, note that.

**Part C — Manager-style hypothesis (10 min).** From the public output (or its absence), hypothesise this manager's likely style:

- **Communication preference:** synchronous vs. async; written vs. verbal.
- **Decision style:** consensus-driven, manager-call, RFC-based.
- **Visibility on the team's work:** writes about it publicly, only shares internally, etc.
- **Likely areas of focus:** what does their public output emphasise?

**Part D — Two questions tailored to them (10 min).** From your Exercise 2 question bank, pick two questions and *tailor* them to this specific manager. E.g., if their LinkedIn shows posts about RFC processes, refine your Category 1 decision-process question to reference what they've written.

**Acceptance:**

- HM identified with role, tenure, prior companies.
- Public output catalogue (with honest "none found" if applicable).
- Manager-style hypothesis written.
- Two tailored questions drafted.
- Total write-up: 200-400 words.

## Problem 4 — The conflict-story badmouth audit (30 min)

Your Exercise 1 or Challenge 1 conflict-story draft exists somewhere. Re-read it with the badmouth filter on.

In `c13-week-05/conflict-story-audit.md`:

**Part A — Re-read the story aloud.** For each sentence, check:

- Does this name a specific person (avoid).
- Does this characterise the other party's view fairly, or as a strawman.
- Does this end with vindication ("and I was right") or with a process learning.
- Would your current/past colleague wince if they read it.

**Part B — Rewrite.** Produce a v2 of the conflict story that:

- Names no specific person (uses role: "my tech lead," "a teammate").
- Characterises the other party's concern at least as fairly as your own.
- Centres the *process* of resolution, not the verdict.
- Includes one sentence on what you took from the process (not who was right).

**Part C — Aloud check.** Read both versions out loud. The v2 should sound dignified even if the original disagreement was significant. If it doesn't, rewrite.

**Acceptance:**

- v1 of the story preserved for comparison.
- v2 rewritten per the rules.
- Aloud check noted (one sentence: did v2 read clean?).
- Word count under 200 for v2 (~90 seconds spoken).

## Problem 5 — Read Will Larson's "Manager README" essay + sample 3 real READMEs (60 min)

Read: <https://lethain.com/manager-readme/>.

Then sample three real manager READMEs from public sources (Larson's post links to several; search "manager readme" + a company name for more).

In `c13-week-05/manager-readme-notes.md`:

**Part A — Three concrete things you'd want in your next manager.** From the READMEs you sampled, list three preferences or operating modes that would matter to *you* if you joined a team where the manager wrote one. Examples: "weekly written feedback in 1:1s," "decision RFCs that include alternatives considered," "explicit on-call rotation rules."

**Part B — Two patterns you noticed as red flags.** Reading other people's READMEs sharpens your eye for what's *not* said. Note two patterns or omissions across the three READMEs that would give you pause.

**Part C — One question you'd add to your bank.** From the READMEs, identify one new question for your Exercise 2 question bank that you hadn't included. Add it to that file with a note ("added from Problem 5").

**Acceptance:**

- Three preferences listed with one-line rationale each.
- Two red-flag patterns named with explanation.
- One new question added to Exercise 2's question bank.
- Total write-up: 300-500 words.

## Problem 6 — Week-5 reflection (40 min)

`c13-week-05/reflection.md`, 300-400 words:

1. Before this week, what was your default answer to "tell me about a recent project"? Be honest: were you leading with the problem or the solution? Was the answer 3 minutes, or did it drift to 7?
2. Of the three exercises this week, which one moved the needle most, and why? (Most candidates report Exercise 1 — the drill-down sheet specifically — because the gap between "I can describe my project" and "I can defend my project under follow-up" is larger than they expected.)
3. The pair mock revealed at least one issue you didn't see in the solo Exercise 1 recording. What was it, and what does that tell you about the limits of solo prep?
4. The HM screen is the first call where you are evaluating the manager in roughly equal weight. Did you notice yourself doing that during Exercise 3, or were you focused entirely on being evaluated? What changes for the real call?
5. What one habit are you carrying into Week 6 — concrete, testable, not aspirational? (Example: "Before every HM screen, I will rehearse the drill-down for at least one of my project's decisions out loud the morning of the call.")
