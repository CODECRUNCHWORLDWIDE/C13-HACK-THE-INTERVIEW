# Exercise 2 — Questions to ask

**Time:** ~60 minutes. **Output:** A personalised 12-question bank for hiring managers, organised across the five categories from Lecture 2, with three questions marked as your reusable defaults and notes on what each question is probing for and what a strong-vs-weak answer would look like.

## Goal

Replace the generic "do you have any questions?" scramble with a structured, opinionated 12-question bank that you can draw from in the closing 10-15 minutes of any HM screen. By the end of the exercise, you know which three you'd ask on every call, which seven are situational, and what each question is actually testing — so you can read the HM's answers, not just collect them.

## Setup

You need:

- Your **target list** from Week 1 (informs which kinds of teams you're talking to).
- The **JDs of any HM screens you have currently scheduled** (informs which role-specific questions to draft).
- A **markdown file**: `c13-week-05/exercise-02-questions-to-ask.md`.
- Optional: **Lara Hogan's "Questions for your potential new boss"** open in a browser tab — useful reference but do not copy verbatim; the exercise is about producing *your* bank, in your voice.

## Process

### Step 1 — Sketch the five categories (5 min)

In your draft file, set up the structure:

```markdown
## My 12-question bank — Week 5 v1

### Category 1 — The team and its work
[2-3 questions]

### Category 2 — The role itself
[2-3 questions]

### Category 3 — The manager themselves
[2-3 questions]

### Category 4 — Team health and growth
[2-3 questions]

### Category 5 — The closing question
[1-2 questions]

### My three reusable defaults
[The three questions you'd ask on every HM screen unless answered organically.]
```

Aim for **12 total** across categories. The split is approximate; what matters is that each category has at least one question.

### Step 2 — Draft Category 1 questions (10 min)

These are about the team and its work. Look at Lecture 2 §3 Category 1 for prompts.

Draft **2-3 questions** in your own voice. For each, include:

```markdown
### Category 1 — The team and its work

**Q1.1:** "{Your question, in your own words.}"
- **Probing for:** [What is this question testing?]
- **Strong-answer pattern:** [What does a good answer look like?]
- **Weak / yellow-flag answer pattern:** [What's a thin or red-flag answer?]
- **Follow-up if the answer is generic:** "{Your prepared follow-up.}"

**Q1.2:** "{...}"
- ...
```

Some example wordings (do not copy directly; rephrase to match how you actually speak):

- "Walk me through what the team has shipped in the last 6 months — and what's on the roadmap for the next 6?"
- "How does the team make technical decisions in practice — is there an RFC process, async-doc culture, in-the-room discussions, or a mix?"
- "What's currently the biggest source of on-call pain on this team?"

### Step 3 — Draft Category 2 questions (10 min)

These probe the role itself — what it actually is vs. what the JD says.

Draft **2-3 questions**, same structure as Step 2.

Example wordings:

- "If I joined and was outstanding at this role, what would that look like at 6 months? At 12?"
- "What's the gap on the team that this role specifically fills — what hasn't been getting done that this role will?"
- "What's the failure mode — what does someone who doesn't work out in this role typically struggle with?"

### Step 4 — Draft Category 3 questions (10 min)

These are about the manager themselves — the most-revealing category and the one most candidates skip.

Draft **2-3 questions**. These are the highest-leverage questions in the bank.

Example wordings:

- "How do you run 1:1s? What's a typical agenda?"
- "What's your feedback style — synchronous, written, frequency?"
- "Tell me about a recent decision you made as a manager that you'd make differently in hindsight."
- "How do you handle a situation where you and a senior IC disagree on technical direction?"

Note especially the "decision you'd make differently" question. A manager's willingness to answer it (and the substance of their answer, if they do) is the single most-revealing data point you'll gather in the call.

### Step 5 — Draft Category 4 questions (5 min)

These probe team health and growth.

Draft **2 questions**.

Example wordings:

- "What's the team's tenure look like — how long have most people been on it?"
- "Where does the team most disagree with the broader engineering org?"
- "What does career growth look like on this team — what's the path from {your target level} to {one level up}?"

### Step 6 — Draft Category 5 — the closing question (5 min)

The Category 5 question is your closing move. Draft **1-2** versions of it — one direct, one softer — so you can pick the right one based on the HM's energy in the call.

Direct version: "Based on this conversation, what's the strongest case for me in this role? What's the most likely concern someone on the loop will raise about me, and how should I think about preparing for it?"

Softer version: "What would you want me to know going into the next round that we haven't covered today?"

Note for each which energy reads it works for:

```markdown
### Category 5 — Closing question

**Direct version:** "..."
- **Use when:** HM is engaged, taking notes, asking substantive follow-ups themselves.

**Softer version:** "..."
- **Use when:** HM is more reserved, the conversation has been brief, or you sense friction.
```

### Step 7 — Mark your three reusable defaults (5 min)

Across the 12 questions, pick the **three** you'd ask on every HM screen unless they were answered organically. Mark them in your file.

The strongest reusable trio, from Lecture 2 §4, is one each from Categories 1, 2, and 5 — they probe team, role, and you in turn. If you choose a different trio, document the reasoning:

```markdown
### My three reusable defaults

1. **{Question ID, e.g., Q1.2}:** "{The question.}"
   - **Why this one is a default:** [One sentence.]
2. **{Q2.1}:** "..."
   - ...
3. **{Q5}:** "..."
   - ...
```

### Step 8 — The substitution audit (5 min)

Sit with all 12 questions open. For each, check:

- **Could this be answered by reading the company's careers page or a blog post?** If yes, rewrite. Save Google-able questions for your own pre-call prep, not the HM screen.
- **Is this a question for the recruiter, not the HM?** Comp, basic process timing, work auth — these belong to the recruiter. Audit each and route appropriately.
- **Is this a question only an HM can answer?** The strongest questions can only be answered by someone with management context and team-internal knowledge.

Rewrite or remove any question that fails the audit. You should end with 10-12 questions; quality matters more than count.

### Step 9 — Honesty check on the reusable defaults (5 min)

Read your three reusable defaults aloud. For each, ask:

- **Do I actually care about the answer?** If the answer is "not really," you'll fail to follow up on it on the live call. Replace with a question whose answer you actually want.
- **Is the question phrased the way I'd actually say it?** Read it out loud. If it sounds like a script, rephrase in your own speech patterns.
- **Can I imagine asking a useful follow-up after any plausible answer?** If you can't picture the follow-up, the question is too narrow.

Replace any default that fails these checks.

## Common mistakes

- **Copying Lara Hogan's list verbatim.** Her list is excellent but it's in her voice. Use it as a prompt; phrase each in your speech pattern. An HM can hear when you're reading a list off Wikipedia.
- **All questions in one category.** Three questions all about engineering culture, zero about the manager themselves. The single-axis bank is weaker than a diversified one because you'll run out of questions if the HM answers one category in their opening.
- **Closed-form questions.** "Does the team use RFCs?" gets a one-word answer. "How does the team make technical decisions in practice?" gets a paragraph. Phrase for paragraphs.
- **Questions that signal anxiety.** "How stable is the company?" "Are layoffs likely?" These are legitimate concerns but the HM screen is the wrong forum. Save for the offer-stage conversation. (If you have specific concerns about stability, the right move is research on the company's runway / recent news, not the HM call.)
- **Questions that signal a pre-decided no.** "If I have a strong remote preference, would that be a problem?" If you have a hard line, ask it of the recruiter early, not the HM late. The HM call is not where you renegotiate the role's terms.
- **Too many questions, no follow-ups.** A 6-question segment with no follow-ups extracts less signal than a 3-question segment with one follow-up each. The follow-up is the multiplier.

## Stretch

- **Draft the per-company variants.** For your top 3 Tier-A targets, draft one Category 2 (role-specific) question that is tailored to *that company's* JD or recent work. The question should not work as-is for any other company.
- **The "what would make you not hire me" variant.** Some candidates pair the Category 5 question with a more direct variant: "What would make this not work — is there anything in my background that you think would be a fit problem for this team?" Drafting this answer-forced version sharpens both the asker and the question.
- **The behavioural-question reciprocity drill.** Pick one classic HM behavioural question ("tell me about a time you disagreed with a teammate"). Imagine the HM has asked it. After your answer, you ask them the same question reframed: "Can I ask you the equivalent — when's the last time you disagreed with a senior IC on technical direction, and how did it resolve?" Note in your file: would you actually ask this? It's a strong move but not for every personality fit. Decide.
- **The "questions for the team interviews" extension.** Draft 3-4 questions specifically for the cross-functional / peer interview rounds (when you talk to engineers who will be your peers, not your manager). The categories overlap with the HM bank but the questions differ — peer interviews are about working with the team, not being managed by the HM.

## Why this matters

The closing 10-15 minutes of the HM screen is scored more heavily than candidates realise. The HM is taking notes on the structure of your questions — which categories you covered, whether you followed up, whether the questions revealed substantive thinking about a workplace or surface-level curiosity. Most candidates show up with one safe question and run out of material; the candidates who walk in with a 12-question bank and pick the right 4-5 in the moment immediately read as more serious.

There is also a strict reverse-interview value: the questions you ask give you the data to decide whether you want this role and this manager, not just whether they want you. Two HM screens with the same overall "fit" read often differ wildly on the answers to "what's a recent decision you'd make differently as a manager" — and that difference predicts the next two years of your career more than most other variables in the loop.

The 60 minutes here pays back across every HM screen for the rest of the cycle. The question bank evolves — by Week 9 it will be different — but the structure of categorising, drafting, and marking defaults is the artifact you'll keep.
