# Week 5 — Quiz

Ten questions. Lectures closed.

---

**Q1.** The hiring manager screen and the recruiter screen differ on five axes. Which of the following is **not** a typical difference?

- A) Who's on the call (recruiter vs. the future direct manager).
- B) The anchor question ("tell me about yourself" vs. "tell me about a recent project").
- C) Whether comp is discussed (always at recruiter; almost never at HM).
- D) Whether the candidate is allowed to take notes during the call.

---

**Q2.** The recent-project answer is the anchor of the HM screen. The target spoken length is:

- A) 60-90 seconds.
- B) 2-3 minutes.
- C) 4-6 minutes.
- D) 10-12 minutes.

---

**Q3.** A candidate describes a project entirely in "we" pronouns for 5 minutes. The hiring manager's most likely read is:

- A) Strong team-player signal; the candidate clearly works well with others.
- B) Individual contribution is hidden; the HM cannot tell what the candidate specifically did.
- C) Neutral — pronoun choice is irrelevant.
- D) The candidate must be in management already.

---

**Q4.** The drill-down structure typically goes three layers deep. Layer 2 ("what would have changed your mind?") tests:

- A) The candidate's vocabulary.
- B) Whether the candidate actually owned the decision, versus inheriting it from someone else.
- C) The candidate's emotional resilience.
- D) Whether the candidate has read the company's engineering blog.

---

**Q5.** Which of the following is the **strongest** answer to "what's your biggest weakness?"

- A) "I work too hard and sometimes burn out."
- B) "I'm bad at meeting deadlines."
- C) "I'm slow at writing design docs — about 2-3x my peers' first-draft time, which delays my projects. For the last 6 months I've been forcing a 30-min timeboxed first draft and reviewing with a teammate. My last three design docs hit a circulated draft within the first day, vs. 3-5 days previously."
- D) "I don't really have any weaknesses I can think of."

---

**Q6.** A hiring manager describes the team's decision process as "more organic, case by case." This is most likely:

- A) A green flag — the team is flexible and non-bureaucratic.
- B) A yellow flag — worth following up on, as it can indicate either healthy flat-structure or undefined process.
- C) A red flag — decisions are clearly made arbitrarily.
- D) Irrelevant — decision processes don't matter at engineering teams.

---

**Q7.** During the closing 10-15 minutes of an HM screen, the candidate asks one safe question and runs out of material. On the HM's rubric, this is most likely scored as:

- A) Positive — efficient candidate, respects time.
- B) Neutral — questions don't really count.
- C) Negative — the closing questions are scored more heavily than candidates realise, and one safe question signals low engagement and underprep.
- D) Bonus points — keeps the call brief.

---

**Q8.** The Category 5 closing question ("what's the strongest case for me / what's the most likely concern?") works because:

- A) It puts the HM on the spot in an awkward way.
- B) It asks the HM to commit a read in front of you, which a serious HM will do honestly — surfacing the loop's likely friction and giving you intel for the rest of the rounds.
- C) It allows you to skip preparation for the rest of the loop.
- D) It is the only legally required question in an HM screen.

---

**Q9.** The conflict question has two classic failure modes. They are:

- A) Refusing to answer, and answering too fast.
- B) Saying "I don't really have conflicts," and badmouthing the specific person you disagreed with.
- C) Making up a story, and giving a real story.
- D) Speaking too quietly, and speaking too loudly.

---

**Q10.** A candidate's "first 90 days" answer is "I'd meet with everyone on the team and understand the codebase." The most likely problem is:

- A) The answer is too specific.
- B) The answer is too generic — it's the floor of the rubric, not the ceiling. A stronger version names specific intake activities (which 1:1s, which docs, which on-call), then names a small-contribution phase, then names a first-ownership phase, ending with a question to the HM about ramp expectations.
- C) The answer is the wrong length.
- D) The answer should have included a comp number.

---

## Answer key

<details>
<summary>Reveal after attempting</summary>

1. **D** — Note-taking is appropriate on both calls. The five real differences are who, what they evaluate, the anchor question, the failure mode, and how comp is (or isn't) handled.
2. **C** — 4-6 minutes is the target. Shorter reads as thin; longer reads as not respecting the time budget.
3. **B** — All-we language hides individual contribution. The fix is to audit pronouns; if a segment has zero "I"s, replace the modest "we"s with "I"s where the work was genuinely yours.
4. **B** — Layer 2 tests ownership. Anyone can repeat a decision; only the actual decision-maker can name the inflection point that would have flipped it.
5. **C** — Specific bounded weakness, concrete cost, active remediation, recent evidence. A is humblebrag; B is disqualifying; D reads as no self-awareness.
6. **B** — Yellow flag. Could be a healthy flat structure, could be undefined process. Follow up: "can you give me an example of a recent decision and how it actually got made?"
7. **C** — The closing questions are scored heavily. One safe question signals low engagement; 3-4 substantive questions across categories signals seriousness.
8. **B** — The question works as a forcing function for the HM's read. Most HMs answer honestly because the question signals you can handle the truth. The answer tells you what to emphasise in the next rounds.
9. **B** — "No conflicts" reads as avoidant or unaware; badmouthing tells the HM how you'll describe future colleagues. The right structure is situation / substance / process / lesson, with no personal attack.
10. **B** — The generic answer is the floor. The three-phase structure (listen / small contribution / first ownership) with a closing question to the HM about expectations is the version that lands.

</details>

If under 7, re-read the lecture you missed. If 9+, you're ready for the homework.
