# Exercise 3 — Pair mock

**Time:** ~90 minutes including coordination. **Output:** A recorded 30-45 minute pair mock of a hiring manager screen, scored by the peer who played manager, scored by you against the same rubric, and a documented top-3 list of things to fix before the real call.

## Goal

Pressure-test Exercises 1 and 2 under live conditions. The recent-project story has to survive interruption and drill-down from a real human; the question bank has to be drawn from in real time based on the flow of the call. The recording captures both halves for review.

## Setup

You need:

- The output of **Exercise 1** (your bulleted recent-project script and drill-down sheet).
- The output of **Exercise 2** (your 12-question bank with three defaults).
- A **peer** willing to spend ~75 minutes on a video call (30-45 min mock + 15 min feedback + 10 min coordination overhead).
- A **video-call tool with recording**: Zoom, Google Meet, Loom, or any tool that produces a reviewable file.
- A **markdown file**: `c13-week-05/exercise-03-pair-mock.md`.
- The **peer's script** (Part B below). Send this to the peer 24-48 hours in advance.

If you cannot find a peer, switch to **solo mode** (Part E below). You lose the live-interruption rep but retain the timed delivery and the self-review value.

## Process

### Part A — Find the peer (10 min coordination)

Ideal peer (ordered by preference):

1. **A current or former engineering manager.** They will probe the way an HM actually probes.
2. **A senior IC who has been on the hiring side recently.** They've seen the HM rubric from the other side.
3. **A C13 cohort member or a C2 peer.** Trade — you play manager for them next.
4. **Any technical friend who can read a script and ask follow-ups.** The script in Part B compensates for a peer without HM experience.

**Not appropriate:** a close friend who knows your work intimately. They will give you the benefit of the doubt on every drill-down and you'll learn nothing. The fresh-eyes constraint is load-bearing.

The one-line request to send:

> "Hey — I'm doing the Week-5 pair mock for the hiring-manager screen. Can you spend 45 minutes on Zoom / Meet with me? You'll play the HM; I'll send a 1-page script you can read in 10 minutes. I'll record it for self-review and you can give feedback against a short rubric afterward. Trade welcome if you want to do the same."

### Part B — The hiring-manager script (send to the peer in advance)

This is what your peer reads. Send 24-48 hours before the mock.

> ## You are playing a hiring manager at a tech company
>
> Your role: an engineering manager at a hypothetical mid-stage tech company (~150-300 engineers, Series C-D or recently public, B2B SaaS or fintech — pick one). You are running a 30-45 minute second-round screen for a Senior Software Engineer position on your team. The recruiter has already screened the candidate. Your job in this call:
>
> - Verify the candidate's technical depth on their own past work.
> - Probe their team-fit and collaboration patterns.
> - Decide whether to move them to the technical screen.
>
> **Your job:** read the candidate's resume in advance (they'll send it). Ask the questions below in roughly this order, with follow-ups. Take light notes. Time-box the call to 35-40 minutes; warn the candidate at the 5-minutes-left mark.
>
> **The structure (use as a backbone; deviate naturally for follow-ups):**
>
> **Beat 1 — Intro + brief self-intro (3-5 min).** "Hi {candidate}, thanks for taking the time. I'm {peer name}, I manage the {made-up team} team at {made-up company}. Before we dive in, can you give me the 60-90 second version of who you are and what you've been working on?" Let them give a compressed intro; this is shorter than the recruiter version.
>
> **Beat 2 — Tell me about a recent project (8-12 min).** "Great. I'd love to hear about a recent project you've worked on. Pick something from the last 12-18 months you owned a substantial piece of, and walk me through it." Let them speak for 4-6 minutes. **Then drill down on two of the decisions they mentioned**, using these probe patterns:
>
> - **Layer 1:** "You mentioned you {chose A over B}. Why?"
> - **Layer 2:** "What would have changed your mind?"
> - **Layer 3 (one of them, as a stress test):** "Did you consider {a third option you invent on the spot}?"
>
> Spend ~2-3 minutes per decision being probed. Watch how they handle:
> - Specificity vs. hand-waving
> - Ownership language ("I" vs. "we")
> - Survival of two layers of "why?"
>
> **Beat 3 — Team-fit probe (5-8 min).** Pick **one** of these and follow up:
>
> - "Tell me about a time you disagreed with a teammate or your manager. How did it resolve?"
> - "What kind of team / manager do you work best with?"
> - "What feedback have you gotten that surprised you, and what did you do with it?"
>
> Probe with one follow-up. Listen for: badmouthing, defensiveness, generic answers.
>
> **Beat 4 — Why this team / role (3-5 min).** "Why are you interested in this team specifically, and this role?" Note: you're a generic mid-stage Series-C / D tech company. They should have prepared something to substitute in — if not, push: "what about *this* team versus a similar one elsewhere?"
>
> **Beat 5 — One stress question (2-3 min).** Pick **one**:
>
> - "What would you do in the first 90 days in this role?"
> - "What's your biggest weakness?"
> - "Why should we hire you over the other senior engineers we're talking to?"
>
> Listen for structure and self-awareness, not for the "right" answer.
>
> **Beat 6 — Their questions for you (10-15 min, the key beat).** "I'll leave the rest of the time for your questions — what would you like to know about the team, the role, or how I work?"
>
> Let them ask. **Answer substantively** — invent details about the made-up team / company as needed. Aim to give them 3-4 question turns. Notice:
>
> - How many questions they ask
> - Whether they follow up on your answers
> - Whether the questions are about the team / role / you (Categories 1-5 from their bank)
> - Whether they ask a Category-5-style closing question
>
> **Beat 7 — Close (1-2 min).** "Thanks — I think we're at time. Anything else before we wrap?" If they ask about next steps, say "{recruiter name} will be in touch within {N days}." End the call.
>
> ## After the call — feedback structure (10-15 min)
>
> Once the call ends, fill in the feedback form below and send to the candidate within 24 hours.
>
> 1. **Self-intro (1-5):** Was the 60-90 second intro tight or rambling?
> 2. **Recent-project structure (1-5):** Did they lead with the problem? Was there a clear decisions-and-trade-offs middle? Did they land on a number and a "what I'd change"?
> 3. **Drill-down survivability (1-5):** When you probed with "why?" did they answer fluently for two layers, or did they hand-wave?
> 4. **Ownership language (1-5):** "I" vs. "we" balance — did individual contribution come through, or was it all team?
> 5. **Team-fit answer (1-5):** Specific story, no badmouthing, structured response?
> 6. **Why this team / role (1-5):** Specific to the role and team or generic?
> 7. **Stress question (1-5):** Structured, self-aware, recent evidence?
> 8. **Their questions to me (1-5):** Did they ask 3-4 substantive questions across multiple categories, with follow-ups?
> 9. **Overall — would you move this candidate to the technical screen?** Yes / No / Maybe. One-sentence reason.
> 10. **One specific thing to fix before their real HM screen.** The single highest-leverage change.

### Part C — Run the mock (30-45 min)

On the call:

1. **Record.** Both video and audio. Confirm with the peer at the start; tell them once, then ignore the camera.
2. **Pretend it's real.** Wear what you'd wear to a real HM call, sit where you'd sit, have your cheat sheet open as a reference (not a script).
3. **Don't pause to coach yourself.** If you fumble a beat, keep going. The recording is for review.
4. **Take notes on the peer's side.** As if they were a real HM — invented team size, stated priorities, follow-up answers. The note-taking is part of the rep.
5. **Use your 12-question bank in Beat 6.** Pick 3-4 questions from the bank in real time based on what's already come up in the call. **Do not ask all 12.**

### Part D — Self-review and peer-feedback integration (15 min)

Within 24 hours of the mock:

1. **Watch the recording yourself first.** Note 3 things that surprised you about how you came across (don't pause to fix in your head; just record).
2. **Read the peer's feedback form.** Note the score per beat and the "one specific thing to fix."
3. **Compare your self-review with the peer's.** Where you agree, that's a confirmed issue. Where you disagree, that's the most-interesting feedback.
4. **List the top 3 issues to fix.** For each, write one sentence on the fix.

In your draft file:

```markdown
## Top 3 issues from the pair mock

1. **{Issue 1}**
   - **Where it showed up:** [Timestamp + beat]
   - **The fix:** [One sentence; what you'll change before the next call.]

2. **{Issue 2}**
   - ...

3. **{Issue 3}**
   - ...

## Peer's "one specific thing to fix"

> [Quote it verbatim.]

**My response:** [Accept / accept with modification / disagree, with reasoning.]
```

### Part E — Solo mode (alternative)

If you couldn't find a peer:

1. **Record yourself.** Open Loom / QuickTime / OBS. Camera on.
2. **Use the script from Part B as a self-prompt.** Read each HM line aloud, pause, then answer it as the candidate. The recording captures only your candidate-side audio/video.
3. **Beat 6 (their questions) — record yourself asking the questions to a virtual HM.** Pause for ~30 seconds where the HM would answer; in the review, imagine what each plausible answer would be.
4. **Time-box at 30 minutes total.** The solo version compresses; that's fine.
5. **Self-review against the same rubric.** Score yourself in each beat 1-5; identify the top 3 issues to fix.

Solo mode loses the live-interruption rep — the part where the HM cuts in mid-sentence with "wait, why did you choose X?" That rep is the single most-valuable part of Part D. If at all possible, do the peer mock; solo is the fallback.

## Common findings

- **Recent-project answer ran 7+ minutes at first attempt.** Even after Exercise 1's recording, the live dynamic stretches the answer because you elaborate under audience attention. Fix: trim Segment 4 by 30-50 words; the implementation specifics are usually the bloat zone.
- **Drill-down cracked at Layer 2.** First "why?" got a fluent answer; second "why?" got a hand-wave. Fix: re-do the drill-down sheet for the decisions that cracked, and rehearse the Layer-2 and Layer-3 answers aloud.
- **All-we language.** Watching the recording, every project description was in "we." Fix: re-write at least one segment of the script to lead with "I" markers and rehearse.
- **No questions back, or all from one category.** You asked one Category 1 question and ran out. Fix: in the cheat sheet, write your three reusable defaults at the top of the questions section so you can't miss them at the close.
- **The conflict story leaked frustration.** The phrasing felt mild on the page; on the recording, the tone read as residually annoyed. Fix: rewrite the story to centre on the *process* not the person, and re-record.
- **The Category 5 closing question — you didn't ask it.** You ran out of time, or you forgot, or it felt too direct in the moment. Fix: write the question on the cheat sheet visibly enough that you can't close the call without it.

## Stretch

- **Run a second mock with a different peer.** Different reactions surface different issues. A technical peer probes design choices more deeply; a non-technical peer catches jargon density and context assumptions.
- **Trade roles.** Play hiring manager for the peer using the same script. Two passes over the structure (yours + theirs) catch issues each individually misses. The 90 minutes turns into 3 hours; you both come out sharper.
- **The behavioural-deep variant.** After Beat 7, ask the peer to spend an extra 5 minutes on a single behavioural question of their choice ("tell me about a time you handled an unexpected production issue"), with two follow-ups. This is the Week 7 preview; banking the rep now is value-positive.
- **Yoodli or equivalent.** Run the recording through Yoodli's free tier. The blunt metrics on filler density, pace, and word choice are useful complement to the peer's qualitative feedback.

## Why this matters

The pair mock is the highest-fidelity simulation you can run for the HM screen short of the real call. It catches the issues that live-recording yourself in a quiet room cannot — interruption handling, the actual flow of a real conversation, the moment when the HM probes a decision you didn't expect them to probe, the energy of someone you've just met asking the same questions that 50 other candidates have answered this week.

Specifically: the drill-down is impossible to rehearse alone. You can practice answering "why X over Y?" in front of a mirror; you cannot practice the follow-up question that an actual person asks in response to your specific answer. The pair mock is where that rep happens. Candidates who walk into an HM screen having done one pair mock outperform candidates who didn't, on the same content, by a margin that HMs notice in the debrief.

The mini-project ([README](../mini-project/README.md)) is the solo-recording version of this exercise, which deepens the self-review against a more-detailed rubric. The pair mock is the harder, more useful version. Do both if you can.
