# Mini-Project — Recorded Mock Hiring-Manager Screen with Self-Feedback

> By the end of this week, the artifacts you'll lean on in every real HM screen — the 4-6 minute recent-project story, the drill-down sheet, the question bank, the conflict story — exist on paper. The mini-project is the operational test: do they hold up when you deliver them under live conditions, on the clock, to a camera?

**Estimated time:** 5-7 hours, spread Friday-Saturday. The recording is non-negotiable; the self-review against the rubric is where the value lives.

## What you ship

A **recorded mock hiring-manager screen with self-feedback**, delivered as five artifacts:

1. **A recorded mock screen** — 30-45 minutes of you delivering all seven beats of an HM call, against a script you read or a peer plays. Video preferred; audio acceptable.
2. **A self-review document** — you re-watch the recording and score yourself against the rubric in §3 of this file. Specific timestamps for each issue.
3. **A revised screen-call cheat sheet (v3)** — the version after self-review, with the top 3 issues addressed.
4. **A second short recording (5-10 min)** — the highest-leverage beat (typically the recent-project answer with drill-down, or the closing questions segment) redone after revising the cheat sheet, demonstrating the fix.
5. **A peer-feedback record (if Exercise 3 done with a peer)** — peer's scored feedback from the live pair mock, plus the changes you applied.

All artifacts committed to `c13-week-05/mini-project/`. The recordings can be stored outside the repo and linked if they're large; the markdown files and the cheat-sheet text live in the repo.

## How to run the recording

Two valid recording modes. Choose one (or do both, if you can).

### Mode A — Solo recording, self-scripted

You play both sides. Time-box at 35 minutes. Use the peer script from Exercise 3 (`exercise-03-pair-mock.md`, Part B) as your prompt list — read each beat's HM line aloud, pause, then answer it as the candidate. The recording captures only your candidate-side audio (or video); the HM-side is implied.

Easier to schedule. The cost: you don't get the live-interruption rep or the surprise drill-down. The benefit: you control the pacing and can deliberately push yourself on weaker beats.

### Mode B — Peer-played recording (the Exercise-3 mock)

If you did Exercise 3 with a peer, the recording from that session **is** the mini-project's recording artifact. Use it directly. The peer-feedback form goes into the artifact pack.

Mode B is strictly higher-fidelity. Use it if at all possible; fall back to Mode A if not.

### Mode A+B — Both

The strongest version of the mini-project: do Mode B on Friday (the pair mock from Exercise 3), then a focused Mode A run on Saturday (30 minutes solo, recording only the segments where the pair mock revealed issues, redone). The Saturday solo run typically scores 4-7 points higher because the Friday review surfaced the specific fixes.

## The self-review rubric

After the recording (within 24 hours, while it's fresh), re-watch with this rubric. Score each beat 1-5; for each score under 4, note a timestamp.

### Beat 1 — Brief self-intro (60-90 sec)

| Score | What it looks like |
|------:|---------------------|
| 5 | Under 90 sec; compressed from the recruiter version (the HM read the notes); ended with a forward-looking handoff to the project question. |
| 4 | Slightly long (100-110 sec) or one minor flat patch. |
| 3 | Re-told the full 3-minute recruiter version; HM had to interrupt. |
| 2 | Meandering; lost the listener's attention. |
| 1 | Failed to land — HM is still unclear who you are. |

**My score:** ____ **Timestamp(s) of issues:** ____

### Beat 2 — Recent-project answer (4-6 min)

| Score | What it looks like |
|------:|---------------------|
| 5 | 4-6 min; led with the problem; clear decisions-and-trade-offs middle; named at least one explicit trade-off; landed on a number; closed with "what I'd change"; mixed "I"/"we" with "I" leading on decisions. |
| 4 | All of the above with 1 minor miss (length slightly off, one all-we segment). |
| 3 | Two or three misses. Length 3 or 7 min. Trade-off implied not stated. |
| 2 | Substantive structural problem (led with solution, no specific number, all-we throughout). |
| 1 | Failed structurally — HM cannot pitch the project in their own debrief. |

**My score:** ____ **Timestamp(s) of issues:** ____

### Beat 2b — Drill-down survivability (the layer test)

Score separately. This is the load-bearing beat for senior+ HM screens.

| Score | What it looks like |
|------:|---------------------|
| 5 | Two layers of follow-up answered fluently on at least 2 of the 3 probed decisions. Named alternatives and inflection points specifically. |
| 4 | Two layers on 1 decision; one layer on the others. No hand-waves. |
| 3 | One layer of survivability across the board. Hand-waved on the second layer of at least one decision. |
| 2 | Hand-waved at Layer 1 ("we just thought it was right"). |
| 1 | HM walks away believing you didn't actually own the decisions. |

**My score:** ____ **Timestamp(s) of issues:** ____

| Score | What it looks like |
|------:|---------------------|
| 5 | Specific story; structured (situation / substance / process / lesson); no badmouthing; under 90 sec for the conflict version. |
| 4 | Slightly long or one mildly defensive phrase. |
| 3 | Meandering or a near-badmouth that you caught mid-sentence. |
| 2 | Rambling; current-employer complaint of any size. |
| 1 | Outright badmouthing or a confused answer. |

**My score:** ____ **Timestamp(s) of issues:** ____

| Score | What it looks like |
|------:|---------------------|
| 5 | Three threads: team-specific, role-specific, manager-specific (if available). Different from the recruiter "why us" answer. Drew on something the HM said earlier. |
| 4 | Two of three threads; minor overlap with recruiter answer. |
| 3 | One specific thread; rest generic. |
| 2 | Repeated the recruiter answer wholesale. |
| 1 | Generic across all dimensions. |

**My score:** ____ **Timestamp(s) of issues:** ____

| Score | What it looks like |
|------:|---------------------|
| 5 | Structured (per Lecture 1 §10); specific named example; recent evidence; under 90 sec; no humblebrag, no generic. |
| 4 | One minor miss (slightly long, slightly generic close). |
| 3 | Two or three misses. Length over 100 sec. |
| 2 | Substantive structural problem (humblebrag, no example, no remediation). |

### Beat 6 — Your questions to the HM (10-15 min)

This is where strong candidates separate themselves. Score carefully.

| Score | What it looks like |
|------:|---------------------|
| 5 | Asked 3-4 substantive questions across at least 3 of the 5 categories; included a Category 3 (manager-style) probe; asked at least one follow-up that doubled the signal; closed with a Category 5 question. |
| 4 | 3-4 questions but only 2 categories; one good follow-up; closing question present but softer than ideal. |
| 3 | 2 questions, mostly one category; no follow-ups. |
| 2 | One question or all generic; no Category 3 or 5. |
| 1 | "Nope, you covered everything." |

**My score:** ____ **Timestamp(s) of issues:** ____

### Beat 7 — Close (1-2 min)

| Score | What it looks like |
|------:|---------------------|
| 5 | Confirmed next steps; one-specific thanks ("the part about X was useful"); professional close. |
| 4 | Confirmed next steps but generic thanks. |
| 3 | Vague close; no specific reference back. |
| 2 | Awkward or rushed. |
| 1 | Awkward / dismissive / forgot to ask about next steps. |

**My score:** ____ **Timestamp(s) of issues:** ____

### Overall

**Total possible:** 40 (Beat 1 + Beat 2 + Beat 2b + Beat 3 + Beat 4 + Beat 5 + Beat 6 + Beat 7, each /5). **Your total:** ___ / 40.

**Interpretation:**

- **32-40:** Strong. You're ready for a real HM screen. Watch once more, fix the timestamped issues, book the call.
- **24-31:** Mid. The structure is there, but specific beats need work. Identify the lowest-scored beat (often Beat 2b or Beat 6) and run a focused drill (one beat, 15 min, two takes) before the real call.
- **16-23:** Needs work. The mock revealed multiple structural issues. Schedule a second full mock — ideally with a peer (Exercise 3) — before booking real HM screens.
- **Below 16:** Stop. The recording is telling you something. Re-read Lectures 1 and 2, redo Exercise 1's recording from scratch, run a second pair mock the next day.

## How to actually re-watch yourself

This is harder for the HM-screen recording than for the recruiter-screen recording, because the HM call is longer and contains more high-stakes moments. A few practical notes:

- **Watch alone first.** Don't show anyone else. The first watch is for diagnosis, not performance.
- **Note before you react.** Pen in hand. When something makes you wince, write down the timestamp and the specific thing. Don't fix in your head; just record.
- **Watch the drill-down segment at 1x first, then 1.5x for a second pass.** The drill-down is where most candidates discover hand-waves they didn't notice in real time.
- **Separate "appearance" from "content."** The rubric scores content. Set appearance discomfort aside.
- **Watch Beat 6 (your questions) twice.** This is the segment most candidates underrate in self-review. Listen for: did you actually follow up on the HM's answers? Did you ask the Category 3 and 5 questions? How did your tone compare to Beats 1-5?

## How the cheat sheet evolves

After every real HM screen, spend 10-15 minutes updating:

1. What drill-down question did the HM ask that you weren't ready for? Add a row to the drill-down sheet.
2. What part of your recent-project answer required clarification? Trim or rewrite.
3. What question did you ask the HM that got a useful answer? Promote it to a reusable default. What got a generic answer? Demote or rewrite.
4. What red flag (if any) did you observe? Add to the cheat sheet's HM red-flag list.
5. What was the HM's tone in the closing minutes? If it was warm, what specifically did you do that earned it? If it was cool, what would you change next time?

By Week 8, your cheat sheet will be the most-revised document in your portfolio. That's the intent. v3 from this week is one stop on the path; v6 by Week 12 is what passes a Staff-level HM screen.

## Stretch

- **Run the mini-project twice this week.** Friday Mode A, Saturday Mode B with a peer. The Saturday version usually scores 4-7 points higher because the Friday review surfaced the specific fixes.
- **Pair-review with a cohort peer.** Trade recordings: you self-review yours, then watch theirs and give scored feedback against the same rubric. Two passes over the rubric catches issues each individually misses.
- **Add a second project drill.** Some HMs steer off your first project and ask for a different one. After the mini-project recording, run a 10-minute focused drill on a second project (using the same Exercise 1 structure). The pair mock's stretch goal pairs naturally.
- **Build the "next-screen prep checklist."** A 10-minute pre-call routine specific to HM screens: re-read the JD, re-read the drill-down sheet, look up the HM's recent public output, queue your three reusable questions. Commit the checklist next to the cheat sheet.

## Why this matters

The HM screen is the first call in the pipeline where you are evaluated as a *future colleague*, not as a candidate. The recruiter was running a filter; the HM is running an audition for a two-year working relationship. The stakes are correspondingly higher and the rubric is correspondingly more qualitative — the HM is writing a paragraph in the debrief, not a checklist.

The mini-project's recording is the diagnostic that surfaces the gap between "I have prepared answers" and "my prepared answers survive a live conversation with someone evaluating me as a colleague." Most candidates discover, the first time they watch the recording, that the drill-down survivability is the loose hinge: the prepared script lands fine, but the second "why?" exposes the parts that weren't actually rehearsed.

This week's artifacts also carry forward. The recent-project recording is the asset you'll re-record after every real HM screen; the question bank evolves; the cheat sheet revises. Week 5 produced the live-delivery toolkit for the *second* stage of the pipeline. Make it count.
