# Week 5 — The Hiring Manager Screen

> *The recruiter screen decides if you're a credible candidate. The hiring manager screen decides if you're someone they want on their team. Two different calls, two different rubrics, two different humans on the other side of the camera.*

Welcome to Week 5 of **C13 · Hack the Interview**. Week 4 produced the live-delivery toolkit for the recruiter screen — a 2-3 minute self-intro, five "why this company" answers, a defensible comp band, a cheat sheet you can keep open during real calls. Inbound that turned into screens last week is now turning into second-stage conversations: the hiring manager wants 30-45 minutes with you.

The hiring manager screen is the first conversation in the pipeline with the person who will, if you're hired, be your direct manager. That changes everything about the call. The recruiter was filtering for *no obvious dealbreakers*. The hiring manager is filtering for *would I want this person reporting to me on Monday morning*. The rubric is different, the questions are different, the failure modes are different, and the prep is different.

The audience this week is the hiring manager. The hiring manager is an engineer or engineering leader (manager, senior manager, director) who currently runs the team that has the open req. They have read your resume and the recruiter's notes. They are not running a structured behavioural pass — they are testing two things across 30-45 minutes: can you describe technical work with depth and ownership, and do you ask the kinds of questions a future team member would ask. The anchor question of the call is some variant of "tell me about a recent project," and the rest of the call orbits around your answer.

The work in Week 5 is not deep system design — that's Week 8. It is also not a coding-on-a-call rep — that's Week 6. The work is two things: rehearsing a single 5-minute "recent project" story until it is sharp, ownership-forward, and survives drill-down follow-ups; and building a curated list of questions you ask the hiring manager back, because the hiring manager screen is the first beat in the pipeline where *asking the right questions* moves you forward or sideways.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this week, you will be able to:

- **Describe** the differences between a recruiter screen and a hiring manager screen on five axes: who's on the call, what they're evaluating, what the anchor question is, what failure looks like, and how comp is (or isn't) discussed.
- **Deliver** a 4-6 minute "tell me about a recent project" answer that leads with the problem, names your specific role and decisions, includes one technical trade-off you owned, and survives at least two layers of follow-up questions without hand-waving.
- **Ask** the hiring manager 5-8 substantive questions about the team, the role, the manager's own management style, and the work itself — questions that you could not ask the recruiter and could not Google.
- **Recognise** the four most common trick or stress questions hiring managers use ("tell me about a conflict," "what's your biggest weakness," "why should I hire you over the other candidates," "what would you do in the first 90 days?") and answer each without flinching.
- **Diagnose** the five most common ways candidates fail hiring manager screens: vague project stories, team-first language that hides individual ownership, no questions back, badmouthing a current manager when asked about conflict, and visible disinterest in the role beyond comp.
- **Run** a recorded pair mock of a hiring manager screen with a peer playing manager, score it against a rubric, and identify the three biggest issues to fix before the real call.

## Prerequisites

- You completed **Week 4**. You have a screen-call cheat sheet, a tight self-intro, and you have either taken at least one real recruiter screen or are scheduled to.
- You have **at least one hiring manager screen scheduled, or you expect one in the next 2-3 weeks**. (If not, work this week as a pre-load; rerun the pair mock the week before your first HM screen.)
- You have **a recent project to talk about** — a piece of work from the last 12-18 months that you owned (or co-owned) a substantive piece of, where you can name decisions you specifically made. If the strongest candidate project is from 3+ years ago, that's a flag: this week is also a forcing function for finding a more-recent story.
- You have a **peer who will play hiring manager for 30 minutes** for Exercise 3 and the mini-project. C13 cohort member, C2 peer, friend on an engineering team, or a former manager you're on good terms with. Solo mode is available as a fallback.

### Bootstrap path

If you have no HM screen scheduled yet, fine — Week 5 still lands. Treat the pair mock as a dress rehearsal. The first real hiring manager call after Week 5 is the live run; the prep either holds or it doesn't, and you'll know in the first 10 minutes. If you have an HM screen scheduled this week, prioritise Exercises 1 and 3 (the recent-project story and the pair mock) and treat Exercise 2 as a one-pass draft to fill in the day before the call.

## Topics covered

- The hiring manager screen vs. the recruiter screen: who, what, why, and how the rubrics differ
- "Tell me about a recent project" — the 5-minute anchor story, structured around problem, your role, decisions, trade-offs, outcome, and what you'd change
- The drill-down follow-ups — what an experienced hiring manager probes for, and how to keep an answer technically credible across two or three layers of "and why did you do it that way?"
- Ownership language — the difference between "we built" and "I built," and when each is the right choice
- The team-fit questions — "tell me about a conflict," "what kind of manager do you work best with," "what does your ideal team look like"
- The trick / stress questions — "what's your biggest weakness," "why should we hire you over the other candidates," "what would you do in the first 90 days"
- Asking the right questions back — what to ask the hiring manager that you could not ask the recruiter, and why the closing 10 minutes of the call is scored as heavily as the opening 10
- The "red flags from the manager" pass — what *you* are screening for in the hiring manager, because this person will be your boss
- Note-taking during the HM screen: what to write down about the team, the work, and the manager themselves
- The post-call follow-up email — different from the recruiter follow-up, sent to the manager (often via the recruiter)
- Pair-mock practice with a peer playing manager, recorded and scored against a rubric

## Stretch goals

- Read **Will Larson's "Manager READMEs"** essay and skim two real manager READMEs: <https://lethain.com/manager-readme/>. They are the closest public artifact to "how the hiring manager actually thinks." Pattern-match what *your* hiring managers say in their screens against what manager READMEs say in writing.
- Read **Camille Fournier's "The Manager's Path,"** chapters 4 and 5 (engineering manager and tech lead). The chapters describe what a manager is hiring for, from the manager's side. ~90 minutes, deeply worth it; library access is fine.
- Listen to **one Pragmatic Engineer episode where a hiring manager discusses their screening process** — Gergely Orosz has multiple. Notice the rubric phrases that recur.
- If you have a friend who is currently an engineering manager, **buy them coffee** and ask: "What's the difference between the candidates whose HM screen makes you want to fight for them, and the ones you politely pass on?" Their answer compresses half of this week's lectures into 20 minutes.

## What "done" looks like by Sunday night

By the end of the week, the following should be true. If any of these aren't, stay with this week's work before moving on.

- You can deliver a 4-6 minute "tell me about a recent project" story from memory, leading with the problem, naming your specific role and decisions, including one technical trade-off, landing on a concrete outcome with a number, and closing on what you'd change.
- The recent-project story survives **two layers of drill-down follow-ups** without hand-waving — you can answer "why did you choose X over Y?" and then "what would have changed your mind?" with specific reasoning, not buzzwords.
- You have written, in your own words, a **12-question bank for hiring managers**, organised across team / role / manager-style / work-itself / red-flag-screening categories. Three are marked as your reusable defaults.
- You have rehearsed answers to the **four classic stress questions**: conflict, weakness, why-you-over-others, first-90-days. Each answer is under 90 seconds and contains a specific example.
- You have **run a recorded pair mock** with a peer playing hiring manager (30-45 minutes), scored it against the rubric, and identified the three biggest issues to fix.
- You have updated your **screen-call cheat sheet** with an HM-screen section that fits on the same single screen — the recent-project bullets, the three reusable questions, the conflict-story compressed to a one-line cue.

This is the hiring-manager-screen baseline. You will revise the recent-project story and the question bank after every real HM screen for the rest of the cycle; this week's version is v1.
