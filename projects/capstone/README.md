# Capstone — Run a Real Loop and Sign a Real Offer

> *Every other course in Code Crunch Worldwide ends with a thing you built. C13 ends with a thing you did. The capstone of an interview-prep track is not a repo you can polish in a quiet room — it is a job search you actually run: a public professional surface that survives the recruiter skim, a loop walked from screen to onsite, and an offer read, negotiated, and signed or declined on the record. The portfolio is the artifact. Landing and negotiating the offer is the capstone.*

This document is the **single source of truth** for the C13 capstone — the deliverable, the milestones, the rubric, and how to submit. It pulls the threads of all ten weeks into one run: the public surface from Week 3, the loop from Weeks 4 through 9, and the offer-and-negotiation close from Week 10. Where the weekly mini-projects each drill one round in isolation, the capstone is the whole loop end to end, scored as one body of work.

Read it once before you start, then keep it open as your checklist while the loop is live.

---

## What you're delivering

A **public interview-readiness package** — one Git repository plus the live links it points to — that proves you ran a real job search and can negotiate the offer it produced. Concretely, the package is two halves that depend on each other:

1. **The portfolio (the artifact).** Your public professional surface from Week 3 — LinkedIn, GitHub profile, three pinned repos, and an optional portfolio site — brought to the standard a recruiter actually clicks through. This is the thing a stranger sees first; it has to hold up on its own.
2. **The loop (the capstone).** A documented run of an actual job search built on top of that surface: a target list and tracker, the screens and mocks from Weeks 4 through 9, at least one real or rigorously simulated offer, and the Week 10 offer-evaluation-and-negotiation close. This is the thing that turns a portfolio into an outcome.

The deliverable is not "a portfolio that *could* get a job." It is "the documented run of a search, anchored to a portfolio, that ends in a signed-or-declined decision you can defend out loud." Learn in the open: commit the tracker, the mock self-grades, and the decision narrative as you go, so the repo reads like a journal of a real loop and not a snapshot staged at the end.

> **A note on the offer.** The strongest capstone is built around a *real* offer you negotiated. If the calendar doesn't cooperate — offers land on their own schedule, not the course's — run the capstone against a **rigorously simulated** offer instead: a real company, a real level, levels.fyi-calibrated numbers, and a mock negotiation run live with another human against the Week 10 recruiter script. A simulated loop, fully documented, scores full marks. A real loop, undocumented, does not.

## Why this is the capstone

Three reasons, and they map onto the three things C13 claims to teach.

1. **A portfolio is a claim; a closed loop is evidence.** Anyone can stage a clean GitHub profile in a weekend. Far fewer can show the tracker, the mock self-grades, the screen-by-screen notes, and the negotiation transcript that prove the surface actually carried a candidate through a loop. The capstone makes you produce the evidence, not just the claim.
2. **You leave with the muscle, not just the artifact.** The thing that compounds over the next thirty years is not the repo — it's the rehearsed negotiation call, the calibrated levels.fyi read, the recovery move when a mock goes sideways. The capstone forces you to run those for real, on a clock, where freezing has a cost.
3. **The offer is where the leverage lives.** Week 10's whole argument is that the negotiation is the only round where the candidate holds more leverage than the company — and that most candidates discover this only after the leverage is gone. The capstone is built so you spend that leverage *while you still have it*, on the record, against a real or simulated offer.

## The package — what "done" looks like

Your capstone repo MUST contain or link to all of the following. Treat each line as a checkbox.

### The portfolio half (from Week 3)

- **A LinkedIn profile** with a specific, role-targeted headline (not the LinkedIn default), an About section of 180–280 words with at least one quantified outcome, and an Experience block that agrees line-by-line with your resume. Link it from the repo.
- **A GitHub profile README** at `github.com/{username}/{username}` that orients a 30-second visitor.
- **Three pinned repositories**, chosen with intent, each demonstrating a distinct skill. The strongest of the three has a README a hiring manager can read in under 90 seconds and a quick-start that works on a fresh clone.
- **A "Google your name" audit** — a short note in the repo confirming the first results for your full name are current and on-message, with anything off-message rewritten or removed.
- **One peer recruiter-skim report** — a write-up of what at least one peer saw walking your name → LinkedIn → GitHub, plus the two pieces of feedback you applied.

### The loop half (from Weeks 4–10)

- **A target list and application tracker** — committed to the repo as a CSV or Markdown table — showing the companies, the stage each reached (applied → screened → onsite → offer), and the offer-rate-per-applied number from Week 1.
- **At least three recorded-and-self-graded mock rounds** drawn from across the loop: a recruiter screen (Week 4), a technical/live-coding round (Weeks 6/9), and either a system-design round (Week 8) or a behavioural round (Week 5/10). Each has the recording link (unlisted is fine) and a one-page self-grade against that week's rubric.
- **One offer — real or rigorously simulated — read line by line**, with a total-comp computation across the four-year vesting window and a levels.fyi-calibrated percentile read for the company-level-location triple.
- **A negotiation transcript or rehearsal write-up** — the call run against the Week 10 recruiter script, showing the first-move dodge, the range push, and at least one escalation-phrase exchange.
- **A final decision narrative** — sign or decline — that names the walk-away number, the target number, and the reasoning, including a cost-of-living adjustment if more than one location was in play. If you declined, include the graceful decline email.

### Required for the whole package

- **A great hub README** for the repo: what the package is, links to every surface and artifact above, and a link to your walkthrough video at the top.
- **Clean Git history.** Multiple meaningful commits made *as the loop happened* — the tracker growing, the mock grades landing, the decision narrative arriving last. No single "added everything" commit. The history is part of the evidence.
- **Free / open-source tools only.** No paid tier is required anywhere. Recording: [OBS](https://obsproject.com/) or [Kap (macOS)](https://getkap.co/). Calibration: [levels.fyi](https://www.levels.fyi/) and the [BLS OES data](https://www.bls.gov/oes/). Negotiation reference: [Patrick McKenzie's salary essay](https://www.kalzumeus.com/2012/01/23/salary-negotiation-make-more-money-be-more-valued/). Mock partners: [Pramp / interviewing.io free tier](https://www.pramp.com/).
- **A walkthrough video.** 3–5 minutes. Walk the portfolio surface, then the loop journal, then talk through the offer decision — *why* you signed or declined, not just *what* the numbers were.
- **A `LICENSE`** (MIT, Apache-2.0, or CC-BY-4.0 for the writing) and a **`.gitignore`** that keeps any private offer details (real names, real dollar figures you don't want public) out of the public repo — redact to ranges where needed.

## Milestones

The capstone runs across the back half of the track and closes in the final week. The loop is not a one-week sprint — offers and onsites land on their own clock — so these milestones are checkpoints, not a rigid day count. Push commits as each lands.

| # | Milestone | Anchored in | Output committed to the repo |
| - | --------- | ----------- | ---------------------------- |
| 1 | **Portfolio live** | [Week 3 — LinkedIn, GitHub, Portfolio](../../curriculum/week-03-linkedin-github-portfolio/00-overview.md) | LinkedIn + GitHub profile + 3 pinned repos links, the name-audit note, and the peer skim report |
| 2 | **Loop opened** | [Week 1 — The Hiring Pipeline](../../curriculum/week-01-the-hiring-pipeline/00-overview.md) | Target list + application tracker, with stages and the offer-rate-per-applied number |
| 3 | **Screens & mocks logged** | [Week 4 — Recruiter Screen](../../curriculum/week-04-recruiter-screen-survival/00-overview.md), [Week 6 — Technical Phone Screen](../../curriculum/week-06-technical-phone-screen/00-overview.md), [Week 9 — Live Coding](../../curriculum/week-09-live-coding-interview/00-overview.md) | At least three recorded mock rounds, each with a one-page self-grade |
| 4 | **Offer read & calibrated** | [Week 10 — Offers, Negotiation, Compensation](../../curriculum/week-10-negotiation-offers-and-compensation/00-overview.md) | Line-by-line offer breakdown + four-year total-comp math + levels.fyi percentile read |
| 5 | **Negotiated & decided** | [Week 10 — Offers, Negotiation, Compensation](../../curriculum/week-10-negotiation-offers-and-compensation/00-overview.md) | Negotiation transcript/rehearsal + final sign-or-decline decision narrative |
| 6 | **Packaged & presented** | the whole track | Hub README finished, video recorded and linked, Discussion post opened |

Visible progress beats invisible perfection. A tracker that grew over four weeks of commits reads as a real loop; a tracker dropped in fully formed on the last day does not.

## Submission

When the loop has closed and the package is ready:

1. **Make the repo public** on GitHub, with private offer details redacted to ranges.
2. **Pin it** to your GitHub profile (Profile → Customize your pins) — the package is itself a portfolio piece.
3. **Upload the walkthrough video** somewhere streamable (unlisted YouTube, Vimeo, or a self-hosted link) and link it at the top of the hub README.
4. **Open a Discussion** on this course's repo with: the package repo URL, the video URL, a two-sentence pitch, your final offer decision (sign or decline, numbers redacted to ranges if you prefer), and the one thing you'd do differently on the next loop.

The cohort celebrates capstones in the community showcase. Sharing the loop — including the parts that didn't go cleanly — is the most useful thing you can give the next person who runs C13.

## After the capstone

You've run the loop once. The muscle is built; now it has to be kept warm.

- **Re-run Week 10 the day your next real offer lands.** It's the only week of C13 whose value is just-in-time. The first offer is where the muscle is built; the tenth is where it pays.
- **Keep the tracker alive.** The offer-rate-per-applied number from Week 1 is the single metric that tells you whether the top of your funnel or the close needs work next.
- **Mentor someone earlier in the loop.** Running a peer recruiter-skim or a mock negotiation for someone else is the strongest test of whether C13 actually landed.
- **Go deeper where the retrospective pointed.** [C7 · Wire Crunch](../../../C7-WIRE-CRUNCH-EMBEDDED-SYSTEMS/) for systems depth, [C23 · CRUNCH-AGENTS](../../../C23-CRUNCH-AGENTS/) for the agent-tooling premium, or [C1 · Code Crunch Convos](../../../C1-Code-Crunch-Convos/) for the Python foundations if that was the gap.

## Questions?

- **"I don't have a real offer yet."** → Run the simulated loop. A real company, a real level, levels.fyi numbers, and a live mock negotiation against another human score full marks. Re-run the negotiation against the real offer when it lands.
- **"My portfolio is thin."** → That's the Week 3 bootstrap path: get *one* genuine repo to portfolio standard, then build the three pins around it. A small, real, well-documented project beats three half-finished ones.
- **"How public does the offer math have to be?"** → As public as you're comfortable with. Redact real dollar figures to ranges and real company names to "a mid-size fintech in Austin" if you prefer. The *reasoning* is what's graded, not the raw numbers.
- **"I declined every offer / got none this cycle."** → A documented decline, or a documented loop that didn't produce an offer plus an honest retrospective on where the funnel broke, is a passing capstone. The evidence of a real run is the deliverable — not a particular outcome.

Run the loop in the open. Sign or decline on the record. Build the package you'd be proud to point a recruiter at — and the negotiation story you'd be glad you have.
